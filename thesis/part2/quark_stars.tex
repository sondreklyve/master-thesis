\chapter{Quark Matter and Compact Stars}

The primary goal of this chapter is to construct an equation of state
for cold and dense quark matter and to investigate its implications
for compact stars. In contrast to the hadronic models considered in
Part~I, the matter described here consists entirely
of deconfined quarks. The resulting stellar configurations therefore
represent idealized quark stars. While such objects provide a useful
theoretical framework for isolating the effects of quark degrees of
freedom, they are not expected to give a complete description of
realistic compact stars. A more realistic scenario involves a
transition between hadronic and quark matter, leading to so-called 
hybrid stars. While such configurations are not considered in this
thesis, the equations of state constructed here could
serve as the quark-matter component in such a description.

Within the models considered in this chapter, the thermodynamic properties of the system are
determined from the grand canonical potential evaluated in the
mean-field approximation. In this framework, the scalar meson field
develops a non-vanishing expectation value, which acts as an order
parameter for chiral symmetry breaking. The constituent quark mass is
then generated dynamically through the relation
\begin{equation}
M_q = g \bar{\sigma} ,
\end{equation}
and becomes a function of the thermodynamic state of the system. This
feature distinguishes the model from free fermion descriptions and is
crucial for obtaining a realistic equation of state.

A first objective of this chapter is to evaluate the grand potential
numerically and determine the equilibrium value of the scalar
condensate by minimizing the thermodynamic potential at each value of
the quark chemical potential. This yields a function
$\bar{\sigma}(\mu_q)$, and therefore a density-dependent constituent
quark mass $M_q = g\bar{\sigma}(\mu_q)$.

At low chemical potential, the condensate remains close to its vacuum
value, corresponding to a phase with spontaneously broken chiral
symmetry and large constituent quark masses. As the chemical
potential increases, the minimum of the thermodynamic potential shifts
toward smaller values of the condensate, leading to a reduction of the
quark mass. This behavior reflects the gradual restoration of chiral
symmetry and, depending on the model parameters, may occur through a
proper (first-order) phase transition or a smooth crossover
\cite{Folkestad2018,Buballa2005,Schmitt2010}.

Once the thermodynamic quantities have been determined, we impose the
physical conditions relevant for compact stars, namely charge
neutrality and weak equilibrium. These constraints fix the relative
abundances of the different particle species and ensure that the
resulting equation of state is suitable for astrophysical
applications.

In addition to the basic QM model, we also consider extensions that
include additional interaction channels relevant at high density. In
particular, we investigate the possibility of diquark condensation,
which leads to color-superconducting phases of quark matter. These
phases arise from attractive interactions in the quark--quark channel
and are expected to play an important role in the phase structure of
dense QCD \cite{Alford2008,Schmitt2010}. We restrict the analysis to
the two-flavor superconducting (2SC) phase, described within the
Quark--Meson--Diquark (QMD) model, which represents the
simplest realization of color superconductivity. The inclusion of such
effects modifies the thermodynamic potential and can have a
significant impact on the resulting equation of state.

Finally, the equation of state obtained from the models is used as
input to the TOV equations, which determine the structure of
non-rotating compact stars in general relativity. By solving these
equations, we obtain mass--radius relations for quark stars and
analyze how the microscopic properties of the model influence
macroscopic observables.

\section{A Simple Quark--Meson Equation of State}

\subsection*{Thermodynamic framework}

Having established the two-flavor Quark--Meson model in vacuum, the
next step is to use it as a model for cold and dense matter. The
central quantity in this construction is the grand potential density
$\Omega$, since all thermodynamic observables may be obtained from it.
In the present work we restrict ourselves to zero temperature,
\begin{equation}
T=0,
\end{equation}
which is the regime relevant for compact stars to a very good
approximation.

The thermodynamic treatment is carried out in the mean-field
approximation. In practice, this means that the mesonic fields are
replaced by their expectation values, while the quark fields are kept
as dynamical degrees of freedom and integrated out. This is the same
large-$N_c$ logic used in the derivation of the effective potential in
the previous chapter: quark-loop contributions are retained, whereas
mesonic fluctuations are neglected at this stage. 

With this approximation, the thermodynamics is governed by the grand
potential
\begin{equation}
\Omega = \Omega(\bar{\sigma}; \mu_u,\mu_d,\ldots),
\end{equation}
viewed as a function of the mean scalar field and of the relevant
chemical potentials. In the simplest case considered first below, the
medium is parameterized by a common quark chemical potential. Later,
when the model is adapted to quark-star matter, additional chemical
potentials are introduced in order to impose weak equilibrium and
charge neutrality. The basic thermodynamic structure is, however, the
same in both cases: one first constructs $\Omega$ as a function of the
mean fields and chemical potentials, and then determines the physical
state by minimizing it.

In this simplified setting we consider isospin-symmetric matter with
\(\mu_u = \mu_d\), so the isospin chemical potential vanishes and
pion condensation does not occur. This assumption does not hold in
the stellar calculation, where $\beta$-equilibrium requires
$\mu_u \neq \mu_d$.
The thermodynamic potential
therefore depends only on the scalar condensate, and the
stationarity condition reduces to
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \bar{\sigma}} = 0.
\label{eq:qs_gap_eq}
\end{equation}
This equation is often referred to as the \emph{gap equation}. Its
solution gives the medium-dependent condensate
$\bar{\sigma}(\mu_u,\mu_d,\ldots)$ and thereby the constituent quark
mass,
\begin{equation}
M_q = g\bar{\sigma}.
\label{eq:qs_constituent_mass}
\end{equation}
The importance of this relation is that the quark mass is not fixed,
but generated dynamically through the chiral condensate. The equation
of state is therefore determined self-consistently rather than by
assuming a prescribed fermion mass. This dynamical mass generation is a
central feature of the Quark--Meson model and is precisely what makes
it qualitatively different from models in which quark mass is treated as
a fixed parameter \cite{Buballa2005,Schmitt2010,sletmoen2022}.

The vacuum expectation value \(\sigma_0=f_\pi\) is fixed by the pion
decay constant, whereas the sigma mass is less sharply constrained and
is therefore commonly treated as a model parameter 
\cite{SchaeferWambach2008,SchaeferPawlowskiWambach2007,Buballa2005}.
In the simple case considered here, the model is therefore
evaluated for
\(m_\sigma=500,600,\) and \(700\,\mathrm{MeV}\). This
variation changes the curvature of the effective potential and thereby
the detailed thermodynamic response, while leaving the underlying
two-flavor mean-field setup unchanged \cite{Folkestad2018,Andersen2024}.
These values are chosen as representative illustrations of the parameter
dependence; the question of which values are physically admissible is
deferred to the stellar analysis in Sec.~\ref{subsec:vacuum_stability}
where a vacuum stability condition restricts the allowed range.

Once the stationary point of $\Omega$ has been found, the pressure is
identified as minus the grand potential evaluated at the minimum,
\begin{equation}
P = -\Omega_{\min}.
\label{eq:qs_pressure_def}
\end{equation}
The particle number densities follow from derivatives with respect to
the corresponding chemical potentials,
\begin{equation}
n_f = -\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_f},
\label{eq:qs_density_def}
\end{equation}
and the energy density is obtained from the thermodynamic relation
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon = -P + \sum_f \mu_f n_f.
\label{eq:qs_energy_def}
\end{equation}
In the constrained stellar calculation, the sum is extended to include
the electron contribution as well.

It is useful to keep in mind that this thermodynamic framework serves
two distinct purposes in the chapter. First, it allows us to study the
Quark--Meson model in a simplified setting, where the role of the
chiral condensate and the onset of dense matter can be seen clearly.
Second, the same framework can be extended by imposing the additional
conditions required for stellar matter, namely local charge neutrality
and $\beta$-equilibrium. The unconstrained and constrained equations of
state are therefore not based on different formalisms, but on the same
grand-potential construction with different choices of thermodynamic
constraints.

\subsection*{Medium contribution at zero temperature}

The derivation of the medium contribution from the fermion determinant
at finite chemical potential is given in Sec.~\ref{subsec:fermion_determinant};
here we quote the zero-temperature results used in the equation-of-state
construction.

The medium contribution is nonvanishing only when the chemical potential
exceeds the constituent quark mass, $\mu_f > M_q$, forming a Fermi surface at
momentum $k_{F,f} = \sqrt{\mu_f^2 - M_q^2}$ where $M_q = g\bar{\sigma}$.
For $\mu_f < M_q$, no states are occupied and the medium contribution vanishes,
leaving the system in the vacuum state with zero pressure and vanishing number
density \cite{andresen2023,sletmoen2022}.

The number density for each quark flavor is
\begin{equation}
n_f = \frac{N_c}{3\pi^2} k_{F,f}^3.
\end{equation}
These quantities depend explicitly on
the chemical potentials and on the dynamically generated mass, and
therefore inherit the medium dependence of the chiral condensate. The
equation of state is thus determined self-consistently once the
condensate has been computed from the gap equation. The behavior of
the condensate together with the onset of finite density is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_sigma_density_mu}.

\includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/number_density_vs_mu_multi.pdf}
\caption{Upper panel: chiral condensate as a function of the quark chemical potential. Lower panel: quark number density as a function of the quark chemical potential, for the simple two-flavor Quark--Meson model at \(T=0\) for \(m_\sigma = 500\), \(600\), and \(700\,\mathrm{MeV}\).}
\label{fig:qs_sigma_density_mu}
\end{figure}

As the chemical potential increases, the solution to the gap equation
changes, leading to a reduction of the condensate and the onset of
finite quark density. The character of this change depends on
$m_\sigma$. For $m_\sigma = 500~\mathrm{MeV}$, the transition is
first-order and discontinuous: at $\mu_q \approx 295~\mathrm{MeV}$
the condensate drops abruptly from its vacuum value
$\bar{\sigma} \approx 93~\mathrm{MeV}$ to $\bar{\sigma} \approx
28~\mathrm{MeV}$ in a single step, and the quark number density jumps
simultaneously from zero to a finite value. For $m_\sigma = 600$ and
$700~\mathrm{MeV}$, the onset occurs at $\mu_q \approx 301~\mathrm{MeV}$
and is continuous: the density turns on smoothly and the condensate
falls gradually over a range of tens of MeV rather than jumping
discontinuously. This transition from first-order to crossover
behavior with increasing $m_\sigma$ reflects the changing curvature
of the effective potential \cite{andresen2023,sletmoen2022}.

Changing $m_\sigma$ therefore affects both the location and the
character of the onset. The three density curves nevertheless converge
to a common trajectory at high chemical potential, shown as a dashed
reference line in the lower panel of
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_sigma_density_mu}. This line corresponds to the
analytical result for free massless quarks,
\begin{equation}
n_q = \frac{N_c N_f}{3\pi^2}\mu_q^3,
\end{equation}
and confirms that all three curves approach the ultrarelativistic
limit at large $\mu_q$, as expected when the constituent quark mass
becomes negligible compared with the chemical potential.

Beyond the transition, the Fermi momentum grows with increasing
chemical potential, and the pressure and energy density increase
monotonically. The behavior of these quantities as functions of the
chemical potential forms the basis for the numerical construction of
the equation of state.

\subsection*{Construction of the equation of state}

The full equation of state is obtained by solving the gap equation for
$\bar{\sigma}$ at each value of the chemical potential and evaluating
the thermodynamic quantities at the corresponding minimum of the grand
potential. In practice, this procedure defines a mapping from the
chemical potentials to the thermodynamic observables,
\begin{equation}
\mu_f \;\longrightarrow\; \{\bar{\sigma},\, M_q,\, n_f,\, P,\, \varepsilon\}.
\end{equation}

For each value of the chemical potential, the gap equation
\eqref{eq:qs_gap_eq} is solved to determine the condensate
$\bar{\sigma}$. This fixes the constituent quark mass through
$M_q = g\bar{\sigma}$, which in turn determines the Fermi momenta and
the medium contribution to the thermodynamic potential. The pressure,
number densities, and energy density are then evaluated using
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:qs_pressure_def}--\eqref{eq:qs_energy_def}. Numerically, the global
minimum of the grand potential is selected at each value of the
chemical potential, so that the thermodynamically preferred phase is
obtained.

The renormalized thermodynamic potential is generally nonzero in the
vacuum state, which means the pressure as defined by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qs_pressure_def} does not vanish at $\mu_q = 0$.
Since the physical vacuum should correspond to zero pressure, a
constant shift
\begin{equation}
B_0 = \Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}(\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{vac}})
\end{equation}
is subtracted from $\Omega$, where $\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{vac}}$
is the solution of the gap equation at vanishing chemical potential.
This ensures $P(\mu_q=0) = 0$ and $\varepsilon(\mu_q=0) = 0$ by
construction. The subtraction has no effect on thermodynamic
derivatives such as number densities, and does not alter the physical
content of the results. 

The equation of state is obtained numerically by tabulating $P$ and
$\varepsilon$ as functions of the chemical potential and performing a
parametric representation of the curve. The resulting function
$\varepsilon(P)$ serves as input for the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff
equations governing the structure of compact stars \cite{sletmoen2022}.
In the numerical construction, only the branch with non-negative
pressure is retained in the plotted equation of state.
The corresponding pressure as a function of chemical potential, together
with the resulting equation of state, is displayed in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_pressure_panels}.

The upper panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_pressure_panels} shows the
pressure as a function of $\mu_q$. For all three sigma masses the
pressure is identically zero below the onset, reflecting that no
quark states are occupied in the chirally broken vacuum. At the onset,
the pressure rises sharply for $m_\sigma = 500~\mathrm{MeV}$,
consistent with the first-order transition identified above, while
for $m_\sigma = 600$ and $700~\mathrm{MeV}$ the rise is slightly more gradual,
reflecting the smoother crossover. At fixed $\mu_q$ above the onset,
larger $m_\sigma$ gives lower pressure, because the larger vacuum
subtraction $B_0$ removes more from the kinetic quark contribution.

The lower panel shows the equation of state $\varepsilon(P)$. All
three curves begin at the origin, confirming the vacuum normalization.
The $m_\sigma = 500~\mathrm{MeV}$ curve is the stiffest: it returns
the highest energy density at a given pressure. This ordering reflects
the same $B_0$ dependence seen in the pressure plot. At large density
the curves approach a common linear behavior, consistent with the
conformal limit $\varepsilon = 3P$ expected for massless relativistic
quarks.

\subsection*{Speed of sound}

An additional characterization of the equation of state is provided by
the speed of sound,
\begin{equation}
c_s^2 = \frac{dP}{d\varepsilon},
\end{equation}
where \(P\) is the pressure and \(\varepsilon\) is the energy density.
This quantity measures the response of the pressure to compression and
therefore reflects the stiffness of the equation of state.

In the present calculation, both \(P\) and \(\varepsilon\) are obtained
as functions of the quark chemical potential \(\mu_q\). The speed of
sound can therefore be evaluated parametrically as
\begin{equation}
c_s^2
=
\frac{dP/d\mu_q}{d\varepsilon/d\mu_q}.
\end{equation}
This form is convenient in numerical implementations, since the
equation of state is constructed as a table in \(\mu_q\), from which
derivatives can be computed directly.

The numerical results are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_speed_of_sound_mu}. The speed of sound is
identically zero in the vacuum phase, where the pressure does not
depend on $\mu_q$. At the onset it rises sharply for
$m_\sigma = 500~\mathrm{MeV}$, consistent with the abrupt
first-order jump in the equation of state. 
For $m_\sigma = 600$ and $700~\mathrm{MeV}$ the
behavior is qualitatively different: $c_s^2$ rises slowly from
the onset, then flattens or slightly dips,
before rising steeply toward the conformal limit. This reflects
the soft crossover regime in which the condensate is falling
continuously but the quarks are not yet fully liberated; the
pressure response is briefly suppressed before the kinetic quark
contribution takes over. The dip is more pronounced and extends over
a wider range of $\mu_q$ for $m_\sigma = 700~\mathrm{MeV}$, where
the chiral restoration is slowest.

At larger $\mu_q$, all three curves approach the conformal limit
$c_s^2 \to \frac{1}{3}$ from below. This limit corresponds to a gas of
massless relativistic fermions, in which the pressure scales as
$P \propto \mu_q^4$ and the energy density as $\varepsilon = 3P$.
It is approached from below in the present model because the
constituent quark mass remains finite at any fixed $\mu_q$, and the
approach to the massless limit is only asymptotic. The speed of sound
never exceeds the conformal limit in the simple QM model; this is an expected
consequence due to the absence of repulsive vector interactions or
diquark pairing, both of which can stiffen the equation of state
beyond the conformal bound at intermediate densities
\cite{Andersen2024}.

\section{Quark stars from the Quark--Meson model}

In the previous section, the Quark--Meson model was used to construct
an equation of state for cold quark matter in a simplified setting,
where the system was characterized by a single quark chemical
potential. While this provides a useful baseline for understanding the
thermodynamic structure of the model, it does not yet correspond to
the conditions realized in compact stars.

In the present section, the same framework is extended to describe
electrically neutral matter in weak equilibrium. The resulting
equation of state is then used as input to the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff
equations in order to construct quark-star configurations and study
their mass--radius relations.

The main objective is to investigate how the stellar properties depend
on the microscopic inputs of the model. In particular, we analyze
the sensitivity to the sigma mass \(m_\sigma\), which
controls the shape of the effective potential, and to the bag
constant \(B\), which fixes the vacuum normalization and shifts the
equation of state.

\subsection*{Stellar matter and construction of the equation of state}
\label{subsec:stellar_matter_qm}

We now construct the equation of state for quark matter under the
conditions relevant for compact stars. As discussed in Part~I, the
matter inside a mature neutron star can be treated to a very good
approximation as cold and in weak-interaction equilibrium. In the
present two-flavor framework, the relevant degrees of freedom are up
quarks, down quarks, and electrons, while neutrinos are assumed to
escape from the star and therefore do not contribute to the
thermodynamics.

The composition of the system is determined by the requirement of
$\beta$-equilibrium. Weak processes of the form
\[
d \leftrightarrow u + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e
\]
convert down quarks into up quarks and electrons, and vice versa.
In chemical equilibrium, the sum of chemical potentials must be
conserved in such reactions. This leads to the condition
\begin{equation}
\mu_d = \mu_u + \mu_e + \mu_{\bar{\nu}_e}.
\end{equation}

In the present context, neutrinos and antineutrinos are not trapped
inside the star but escape on timescales much shorter than the
evolution timescale of the system. As a result, they do not contribute
to the thermodynamic equilibrium and their chemical potential can be
set to zero,
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\bar{\nu}_e} = 0.
\end{equation}
The condition for $\beta$-equilibrium therefore reduces to
\begin{equation}
\mu_d = \mu_u + \mu_e.
\label{eq:qs_beta_eq}
\end{equation}

In addition to weak equilibrium, bulk matter must satisfy local
electric charge neutrality. For two-flavor quark matter, this condition
reads
\begin{equation}
\frac{2}{3} n_u - \frac{1}{3} n_d - n_e = 0,
\label{eq:qs_charge_neutrality_sub}
\end{equation}
where \(n_u\), \(n_d\), and \(n_e\) denote the number densities of
up quarks, down quarks, and electrons, respectively. This ensures that
the net electric charge of the system vanishes, which is required to
avoid large Coulomb energies in macroscopic matter.

A further constraint in quark matter is color neutrality. In general,
this requires that the densities associated with the color charges
corresponding to the diagonal generators of SU(3), commonly denoted
by \(\phi_3\) and \(\phi_8\), vanish. In the present two-flavor
Quark--Meson model, however, all color degrees of freedom are treated
symmetrically, and the quark distributions are identical for each
color. As a consequence, the net color charge vanishes automatically,
and no additional chemical potentials are required to enforce color
neutrality.
This situation changes in more general phases of quark matter, such as
color-superconducting phases, where pairing patterns can distinguish
between different colors. In such cases, color neutrality must be
imposed explicitly through additional constraints.

The thermodynamic potential is therefore generalized to include both
the quark and electron contributions. Before imposing the constraints,
it may be written schematically as
\begin{equation}
\Omega = \Omega_q(\bar{\sigma};\mu_u,\mu_d) + \Omega_e(\mu_e),
\end{equation}
where \(\Omega_q\) is the Quark--Meson contribution and \(\Omega_e\)
is the contribution from the electron gas. The mean-field structure of
the quark sector remains unchanged. In particular, the equilibrium
value of the scalar condensate is still obtained from the gap equation
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \bar{\sigma}} = 0,
\label{eq:qs_gap_eq_stellar}
\end{equation}
which determines the medium-dependent condensate \(\bar{\sigma}\) and
thereby the constituent quark mass,
\begin{equation}
M_q = g\bar{\sigma}.
\end{equation}

It is useful to impose \(\beta\)-equilibrium by changing variables from
the individual quark chemical potentials to a quark-number chemical
potential \(\mu_q\) and the electron chemical potential \(\mu_e\). Since
\(\mu_e\) couples to negative electric charge, while the electric
charges of the quarks are \(q_u = 2/3\) and \(q_d = -1/3\), the quark
chemical potentials are written as
\begin{equation}
\mu_u = \mu_q - \frac{2}{3}\mu_e,
\qquad
\mu_d = \mu_q + \frac{1}{3}\mu_e.
\label{eq:qs_mu_u_mu_d}
\end{equation}
This parametrization automatically satisfies the condition for
\(\beta\)-equilibrium, since
\begin{equation}
\mu_d - \mu_u = \mu_e.
\end{equation}
The remaining independent composition variable is therefore
\(\mu_e\), which is fixed by imposing electric charge neutrality.

With this parametrization, the thermodynamic potential becomes
\begin{equation}
\Omega(\bar{\sigma};\mu_q,\mu_e)
=
\Omega_q\!\left(\bar{\sigma};
\mu_q - \frac{2}{3}\mu_e,
\mu_q + \frac{1}{3}\mu_e
\right)
+
\Omega_e(\mu_e).
\end{equation}
The electron term is included because the electrons required by charge
neutrality are part of the thermodynamic system. In the present model
they are not coupled to the chiral condensate and are treated as a free
relativistic Fermi gas. At zero temperature, the grand potential of a
fermion species is obtained by filling all momentum states up to the
Fermi momentum. For electrons, the corresponding contribution is
therefore the standard degenerate Fermi-gas result
\cite{shapiro_teukolsky,glendenning2000,Schmitt2010}.

The numerical calculation uses the standard massive degenerate
Fermi-gas expressions with the physical electron mass
\(m_e = 0.511~\mathrm{MeV}\). At zero temperature, the electron Fermi
momentum is
\begin{equation}
k_{F,e}
=
\sqrt{\mu_e^2-m_e^2},
\end{equation}
for \(\mu_e>m_e\), while the electron density vanishes for
\(\mu_e\leq m_e\). The massive electron grand potential is
\begin{equation}
\Omega_e(\mu_e)
=
-\frac{1}{24\pi^2}
\left[
\mu_e k_{F,e}\left(2\mu_e^2-5m_e^2\right)
+
3m_e^4
\ln\left(
\frac{\mu_e+k_{F,e}}{m_e}
\right)
\right],
\label{eq:qs_electron_omega_massive}
\end{equation}
again for \(\mu_e>m_e\). For \(\mu_e\leq m_e\), one has
\(\Omega_e=0\). The corresponding number density is
\begin{equation}
n_e
=
-\frac{\partial\Omega_e}{\partial\mu_e}
=
\frac{k_{F,e}^3}{3\pi^2}.
\label{eq:qs_electron_density_massive}
\end{equation}

For reference, in the ultrarelativistic limit \(m_e \ll \mu_e\), these
expressions reduce to
\begin{equation}
\Omega_e(\mu_e)
=
-\frac{\mu_e^4}{12\pi^2}.
\label{eq:qs_electron_omega_ultrarel}
\end{equation}
The corresponding limiting expressions for the pressure and number
density are
\begin{equation}
P_e = -\Omega_e = \frac{\mu_e^4}{12\pi^2},
\qquad
n_e = -\frac{\partial \Omega_e}{\partial \mu_e}
= \frac{\mu_e^3}{3\pi^2}.
\end{equation}
The electron energy density is then
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_e
=
-P_e + \mu_e n_e
=
\frac{\mu_e^4}{4\pi^2}.
\end{equation}
Although the electron contribution is usually small compared with the
quark contribution, it must be included in the total pressure and
energy density in order to maintain thermodynamic consistency.

For each value of the control chemical potential \(\mu_q\), the physical
state is obtained by solving the gap equation together with the
neutrality condition. The latter can be written as stationarity with
respect to \(\mu_e\),
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_e} = 0.
\end{equation}
Using Eq.~\eqref{eq:qs_mu_u_mu_d} together with
\(n_i = -\partial\Omega/\partial\mu_i\), this gives
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_e}
=
\frac{2}{3}n_u
-
\frac{1}{3}n_d
-
n_e,
\end{equation}
and therefore reproduces the electric charge neutrality condition
\begin{equation}
\frac{2}{3}n_u
-
\frac{1}{3}n_d
-
n_e
=
0.
\end{equation}
Thus, the coupled problem is reduced to solving for the condensate and
the chemical potentials subject to the gap equation and electric charge
neutrality.

Once the stationary point has been found, the total pressure is given
by
\begin{equation}
P = -\Omega_{\min},
\end{equation}
where both the quark and electron contributions are included. The
particle number densities follow from
\begin{equation}
n_f = -\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_f},
\end{equation}
and the energy density is obtained from
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon
=
-P
+
\mu_u n_u
+
\mu_d n_d
+
\mu_e n_e .
\end{equation}

The construction of the equation of state therefore proceeds
parametrically. For each point in the chemical-potential grid, the gap
equation and the neutrality condition are solved, with
\(\beta\)-equilibrium imposed through
\(\mu_e=\mu_d-\mu_u\). This yields
\begin{equation}
\{\mu_u,\,\mu_d,\,\mu_e\}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\{\bar{\sigma},\, M_q,\,
n_u,\, n_d,\, n_e,\, P,\, \varepsilon\}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the result may be viewed as a parametrization in terms
of the quark-number chemical potential \(\mu_q\). By eliminating the
chemical potentials, one obtains the relation
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon = \varepsilon(P),
\end{equation}
which defines the equation of state required for the stellar structure
calculation.

The resulting composition reflects the requirement of electric charge
neutrality. Since electrons carry negative charge, the system contains
a larger density of down quarks than up quarks, \(n_d > n_u\), while
the electron density remains much smaller than the quark densities.
This behavior is characteristic of cold, two-flavor quark matter in
\(\beta\)-equilibrium and is consistent with previous studies of dense
QCD matter in effective models
\cite{Buballa2005,Schmitt2010,Alford2008}.

\subsection*{Vacuum structure, normalization, and phase construction}
\label{subsec:vacuum_stability}

Before constructing the stellar equation of state, it is necessary to
ensure that the underlying model provides a physically consistent
description of the vacuum. In the Quark--Meson model, the vacuum is
determined by the effective potential \(\Omega(\sigma)\) evaluated at
vanishing temperature and chemical potentials. The physical vacuum
corresponds to the global minimum of this potential and fixes the
vacuum value of the chiral condensate.

As discussed previously, the vacuum expectation value is required to
reproduce the pion decay constant, \(\sigma_0 = f_\pi\). This imposes
constraints on the model parameters, in particular on the sigma mass
\(m_\sigma\), which controls the curvature of the effective potential.
If \(m_\sigma\) is chosen too small, the potential may fail to develop
a stable minimum at \(\sigma \approx f_\pi\), and the model no longer
provides a consistent description of the vacuum.

To determine the range of acceptable parameter values, the vacuum
effective potential \(\Omega(\sigma)\) is evaluated for different
choices of \(m_\sigma\). The results are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_vacuum_scan}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/vacuum_potential_sigma_scan.pdf}
\caption{Vacuum effective potential \(\Omega(\sigma)\), shown in units of
\(f_\pi^4\), as a function of the chiral condensate for several values of
the sigma mass \(m_\sigma = 300, 350, 400, 500, 600, 700~\mathrm{MeV}\).
The vertical dashed line indicates the physical vacuum value
\(\sigma = f_\pi = 93~\mathrm{MeV}\). Curves shown with dotted lines
correspond to parameter choices that do not exhibit a stable minimum at
\(\sigma = f_\pi\) and are therefore excluded.}
\label{fig:qs_vacuum_scan}
\end{figure}

The figure shows that the existence of a physically acceptable vacuum
depends sensitively on the value of \(m_\sigma\). For sufficiently small
values, the point \(\sigma=f_\pi\) is no longer a stable minimum of the
effective potential. Although the stationarity condition may still be
satisfied at this point, the curvature is not positive and the
potential is not bounded from below in the present truncation. The
corresponding parameter choices therefore do not define a stable vacuum
state and are excluded from the stellar calculation. In the present
setup, this occurs for
\(m_\sigma \lesssim 395~\mathrm{MeV}\).

Based on this analysis, we restrict the parameter space to values of
\(m_\sigma\) above this threshold. In the following, we consider the
representative choices
\begin{equation}
m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV},
\end{equation}
which correspond to the lowest values for which a stable vacuum solution
is obtained. These values provide a controlled range for studying the
impact of the effective potential on the thermodynamic and stellar
properties of the model.

Having established the parameter range, we turn to the normalization
of the thermodynamic potential. After renormalization, the vacuum
contribution to \(\Omega\) is generally nonzero, which implies that the
pressure does not vanish in the vacuum state. Since the physical
vacuum should correspond to zero pressure, a constant shift is
introduced,
\begin{equation}
\Omega \rightarrow \Omega + B_0,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
B_0 = \Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}(\sigma_0).
\end{equation}
This ensures that \(P = 0\) in the vacuum configuration.

In addition to this vacuum subtraction, one may introduce an
additional constant shift \(B\), leading to the transformation
\begin{equation}
P \rightarrow P - B, \qquad
\varepsilon \rightarrow \varepsilon + B.
\end{equation}
This parameter plays a role analogous to the bag constant in
phenomenological quark-matter models \cite{Chodos1974,FarhiJaffe1984}.
While \(B_0\) is fixed by the requirement of vacuum normalization, the
additional constant \(B\) can be used to control the onset and
stiffness of the equation of state. In particular, increasing \(B\)
shifts the equation of state toward higher energy densities and
delays the point at which the pressure becomes positive.

A further important aspect is the treatment of the phase transition.
Within the mean-field approximation, the thermodynamic potential may
develop multiple local minima as a function of the chemical
potential. Selecting the global minimum ensures thermodynamic
consistency, but the resulting equation of state may still contain
regions where
\begin{equation}
\frac{dP}{d\mu_q} < 0,
\end{equation}
which corresponds to a mechanically unstable branch.

To construct a physically meaningful equation of state, this unstable
region is removed by performing a Maxwell construction. This replaces
the non-monotonic segment by a constant-pressure interval,
corresponding to a first-order phase transition between two phases at
equal pressure and chemical potential. The resulting equation of state
is thermodynamically stable and can be used as input for the stellar
structure calculation \cite{glendenning2000,Schmitt2010}.

\subsection*{Equation of state and parameter dependence}

With the constraints of $\beta$-equilibrium and charge neutrality
imposed, and the vacuum structure fixed as discussed above, the
equation of state can now be constructed. The procedure follows
directly from the thermodynamic framework: for each point in the
chemical-potential grid, the gap equation and the neutrality condition
are solved, and the pressure and energy density are evaluated at the
minimum of the grand potential.

The resulting equation of state is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_eos_zoom} for 
\(m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV}\).

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/pressure_vs_energy_density.pdf}
\caption{Equation of state for charge-neutral, $\beta$-equilibrated
two-flavor quark matter. The curves are shown for
\(m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV}\). The dotted segments
represent the direct mean-field result, while the solid curves show
the equation of state after applying the Maxwell construction.}
\label{fig:qs_eos_zoom}
\end{figure}

The dotted curves correspond to the direct mean-field solution,
obtained by selecting the global minimum of the thermodynamic
potential at each value of the chemical potential. As discussed in the
previous subsection, this construction may contain regions where the
pressure decreases with increasing chemical potential, indicating a
mechanically unstable branch. In the \(\varepsilon(P)\) representation,
this manifests as a non-monotonic behavior that cannot be used in a
consistent stellar calculation.

The physically relevant equation of state is obtained by performing a
Maxwell construction, shown by the solid curves in the figure. In this
procedure, the unstable region is replaced by a constant-pressure
segment, corresponding to a first-order phase transition between two
phases at equal pressure and chemical potential. This ensures that the
resulting equation of state is thermodynamically stable and
single-valued.

The dependence on the sigma mass is clearly visible. Lower values of
\(m_\sigma\) lead to a stiffer equation of state, with a more rapid
increase of pressure with energy density, while higher values produce
a softer response. This behavior can be traced back to the shape of
the effective potential and its influence on the evolution of the
chiral condensate with increasing density.

At higher pressures, the curves approach a common behavior, reflecting
that the system becomes increasingly dominated by relativistic quark
degrees of freedom. In this regime, the constituent quark masses become
small compared with the chemical potentials, and the equation of state
approaches that of an ultrarelativistic ideal Fermi gas. Correspondingly,
the different curves tend toward the limiting relation
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon = 3P,
\end{equation}
or equivalently \(c_s^2=dP/d\varepsilon = \frac{1}{3}\). This is the standard
high-density behavior of a gas of weakly interacting relativistic
fermions \cite{Buballa2005,Schmitt2010}.

\subsection*{Stellar structure and mass--radius relations}
\label{subsec:qm_stellar_structure}

The macroscopic properties of the quark-star configurations are
obtained by solving the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff (TOV) equations,
as introduced in Part~I. Given an equation of state, the TOV equations
are integrated outward from a chosen central pressure until the
pressure vanishes, which defines the radius of the star. Repeating
this procedure for a range of central pressures yields a sequence of
equilibrium configurations characterized by their mass and radius.

\subsubsection*{Gravitationally-bound sequences}
\label{sec:qm_grav_bound}

Before imposing the absolute-stability constraint, we consider the
simplest quark-star configuration available in this model: the equation
of state with vacuum pressure fixed to zero through the \(B_0\)
subtraction alone, without any additional bag constant. This is the
analogue of the Quark--Meson--Diquark calculation discussed later in
this chapter, where the renormalized vacuum serves as the surface
condition without any externally imposed energy constraint. The
resulting mass--radius sequences for \(m_\sigma = 400\), \(500\), and
\(600~\mathrm{MeV}\) are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:qm_grav_bound_mr}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/qm_grav_bound_mass_radius.pdf}
\caption{Mass--radius relations for gravitationally-bound quark stars
(vacuum subtraction \(B_0\) only, no additional bag constant) for
\(m_\sigma = 400\), \(500\), and \(600~\mathrm{MeV}\) (viridis color
scale, dark to light). Filled circles mark the maximum-mass
configuration on each stable branch. All three sequences use the
simple \(B_0\) normalization: the stellar surface sits at the onset of
the electron-present quark phase, where the pressure is effectively
zero on the stellar scale
(\(P_{\mathrm{onset}}/P_{\max} \sim 10^{-7}\text{--}10^{-8}\)).}
\label{fig:qm_grav_bound_mr}
\end{figure}

The \(m_\sigma = 500\) and \(600~\mathrm{MeV}\) sequences exhibit
the standard gravitationally-bound topology: maximum masses of
\(M_{\max} = 2.15~M_\odot\) at \(R = 12.3~\mathrm{km}\) and
\(M_{\max} = 1.80~M_\odot\) at \(R = 11.8~\mathrm{km}\), respectively,
with radii at canonical mass \(R(1.4\,M_\odot) = 12.9~\mathrm{km}\) and
\(14.1~\mathrm{km}\). As the central pressure decreases below the
maximum-mass value, the stellar radius grows rather than shrinking
toward a compact island, and configurations with
\(M \lesssim 0.5\,M_\odot\) extend beyond \(20~\mathrm{km}\), reflecting
the gravitationally-bound structure in which the quark core is
surrounded by effectively vacuum. The \(m_\sigma = 400~\mathrm{MeV}\)
sequence is structurally more complex: a residual first-order phase
transition at low pressure modifies the mass--radius topology at very
low stellar masses, but at astrophysically relevant masses the sequence
remains gravitationally-bound, supporting \(M_{\max} = 2.45~M_\odot\)
at \(R = 13.7~\mathrm{km}\) with \(R(1.4\,M_\odot) = 13.4~\mathrm{km}\).
Across all three sigma masses, the sequences differ quantitatively
--- higher \(m_\sigma\) gives a softer equation of state and a lower
maximum mass --- but each has its stellar surface at vanishing baryon
density rather than at the finite surface density of a self-bound star.

These sequences should be understood as a theoretical baseline
--- the pure QM prediction without any absolute-stability tuning ---
rather than as models of astrophysically realistic compact stars: the
near-vacuum surface densities and large radii at low mass are not
compatible with observed neutron-star properties.

The remainder of this section examines what happens when an additional
bag constant is imposed to satisfy the Bodmer--Witten absolute-stability
criterion. This constraint shifts the surface to a finite baryon density
and can produce topologically self-bound stellar configurations,
but the effect depends sensitively on \(m_\sigma\).

\subsubsection*{Bodmer--Witten sequences}

In the present calculation, this procedure is applied to the
equations of state constructed for the selected sigma masses
\(m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV}\). For each value of
\(m_\sigma\), a reference bag constant \(B_{\min}\) is first determined
from a stability condition at zero pressure. More precisely, the
shifted equation of state is written as
\begin{equation}
P = P_{B_0} - B,
\qquad
\varepsilon = \varepsilon_{B_0} + B,
\end{equation}
where \(B_0\) denotes the vacuum subtraction discussed above. The value
\(B_{\min}\) is chosen such that the energy per baryon at the
zero-pressure surface satisfies
\begin{equation}
\frac{E}{A} = 931~\mathrm{MeV}.
\end{equation}
This reference value is used as a conventional
stability benchmark, obtained by comparing the energy per baryon of
bulk quark matter at zero pressure with that of ordinary nuclear
matter, represented by iron. This criterion is commonly used in
discussions of absolutely stable quark matter and the Bodmer--Witten
hypothesis \cite{Bodmer1971,Witten1984,FarhiJaffe1984}. In the present
two-flavor calculation, it should be understood as a reference scale
for fixing \(B_{\min}\), rather than as a claim that the model realizes
absolutely stable strange matter.

To test the sensitivity of the stellar solutions to this
phenomenological shift, two additional equations of state are generated
around the reference value. The variation is made in fourth-root units,
\begin{equation}
B^{1/4} = B_{\min}^{1/4} - 10~\mathrm{MeV},
\qquad
B^{1/4} = B_{\min}^{1/4},
\qquad
B^{1/4} = B_{\min}^{1/4} + 10~\mathrm{MeV}.
\end{equation}
The two shifted values are not independent physical constraints, but
are included to show how the mass--radius sequence responds to a
smaller or larger effective bag constant.

For each resulting equation of state, the TOV equations are solved
numerically using the tabulated relation \(\varepsilon(P)\). The
integration produces a family of stellar configurations, which are
represented as mass--radius relations. Along each sequence, the
maximum mass marks the onset of instability, separating stable and
unstable configurations.
The resulting mass--radius relations are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_mass_radius_clean}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering

\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/mass_radius_combined.pdf}

\caption{Mass--radius relations for quark stars obtained from the
Quark--Meson model for \(m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV}\)
(top to bottom).
For each value of \(m_\sigma\), three curves are shown corresponding to
\(B^{1/4}=B_{\min}^{1/4}-10~\mathrm{MeV}\), \(B_{\min}^{1/4}\), and
\(B_{\min}^{1/4}+10~\mathrm{MeV}\). Solid colored lines indicate

branches beyond the maximum mass.}
\label{fig:qs_mass_radius_clean}
\end{figure}

It is important to note that the low-mass part of these sequences
should not be interpreted as a realistic description of ordinary
compact stars. These sequences apply the stability bag constant \(B_{\min}\), which sets
the zero-pressure surface at a finite baryon density. For
\(m_\sigma = 400\) and \(500~\mathrm{MeV}\), the resulting surface density is high
enough to produce compact, self-bound topology at low mass. For
\(m_\sigma = 600~\mathrm{MeV}\), the surface density is lower, and the sequences
retain a gravitationally-bound character despite satisfying the
absolute-stability energy criterion.
In a realistic
hybrid model, one would replace the low-density region with hadronic
matter and a nuclear crust. The low-mass, small-radius segments should therefore be regarded
as a consequence of the self-bound construction rather than as
realistic predictions; the same caveat applies to the gravitationally-bound
sequences in Fig.~\ref{fig:qm_grav_bound_mr}, whose low-mass tails
extend to unphysically large radii.
Neither pure-quark-matter topology can be carried over to realistic
neutron-star modeling without the addition of a nuclear-matter crust.

Comparing Fig.~\ref{fig:qm_grav_bound_mr} with the lower panel of
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_mass_radius_clean} for \(m_\sigma = 600~\mathrm{MeV}\)
makes the topology contrast directly visible: the self-bound curves
collapse toward a compact island at low mass, while the
gravitationally-bound sequence extends to large radii. The maximum mass
is almost identical in both constructions
(\(M_{\max} \approx 1.80~M_\odot\)), but the gravitationally-bound
sequence gives \(R(1.4\,M_\odot) = 14.1~\mathrm{km}\) versus
\(12.6~\mathrm{km}\) for the self-bound case, so the bound-state
construction primarily affects radii and low-mass topology rather than
the maximum mass.

This topology comparison identifies a sharper distinction than is sometimes
drawn in MIT-bag-type discussions: the Bodmer--Witten energy
criterion (\(E/A = 931~\mathrm{MeV}\) at the zero-pressure surface)
is necessary but not sufficient for topologically self-bound stellar
configurations. At \(m_\sigma = 600~\mathrm{MeV}\), satisfying the
energy criterion at \(B = B_{\min}\) produces matter that is
absolutely stable against decay into iron nuclei, but with a surface
density of only about 4 percent of nuclear saturation. Stellar
configurations at this surface density extend to large radii at low
mass, in the same gravitationally-bound topology that the
Quark--Meson--Diquark model produces without any bag constant.
Genuine self-bound topology, with compact configurations at low
mass, requires a bag constant several times larger than the
Bodmer--Witten threshold: \(B^{1/4}\) of order
\(47\text{--}67~\mathrm{MeV}\) at \(m_\sigma = 600~\mathrm{MeV}\).
At lower sigma masses (400 and 500~MeV), satisfying the energy
criterion also produces a surface density sufficient for self-bound
topology, so the two notions effectively align at these parameter
choices.

The results exhibit a clear dependence on the bag constant. Increasing
\(B\) shifts the equation of state toward higher energy densities and
effectively softens it. As a consequence, the corresponding stellar
sequences support lower maximum masses and typically smaller radii.
Conversely, smaller values of \(B\) lead to a stiffer equation of
state and allow for more massive stars. This behavior is consistent
with the interpretation of the bag constant as a parameter controlling
the onset and stiffness of the quark-matter phase
\cite{FarhiJaffe1984,Buballa2005}.

The dependence on the sigma mass reflects the underlying chiral
dynamics of the model. Lower values of \(m_\sigma\) correspond to a
stiffer effective potential and therefore to a stiffer equation of
state, resulting in more massive and more compact stellar
configurations. Higher values of \(m_\sigma\) lead to softer equations
of state and reduced maximum masses.

Quantitatively, for the Bodmer--Witten parameter sequences, the
maximum masses are found to lie in the range
\begin{equation}
M_{\max} \approx 1.79\text{--}2.13~M_\odot,
\end{equation}
depending on the choice of \(m_\sigma\) and \(B\). For
\(m_\sigma = 400~\mathrm{MeV}\), the lowest bag constant considered
yields a maximum mass of approximately \(2.13~M_\odot\), while
increasing \(B\) reduces the maximum mass to below
\(1.9~M_\odot\). A similar trend is observed for
\(m_\sigma = 500~\mathrm{MeV}\), where the maximum mass remains close
to \(2~M_\odot\) for moderate values of the bag constant but decreases
for larger shifts. For \(m_\sigma = 600~\mathrm{MeV}\), all considered
configurations yield maximum masses below \(1.8~M_\odot\), indicating
that the corresponding equations of state are too soft to support the
most massive observed neutron stars.

Overall, the results demonstrate that both the sigma mass and the bag
constant play a central role in determining the stellar properties.
The sigma mass controls the underlying stiffness through the chiral
dynamics of the model, while the bag constant provides an additional
phenomenological shift that can significantly modify the mass scale of
the solutions. Only a subset of the parameter space is capable of
supporting stars with masses of order \(2~M_\odot\), which provides an
important constraint when comparing the model to astrophysical
observations.

\section{Color-superconducting quark matter}

The quark-matter equations of state constructed above were based on the
ordinary two-flavor Quark--Meson model. In that description the only
mean field was the chiral condensate, and the main microscopic effect was
the density dependence of the constituent quark mass. At sufficiently high
density, however, another instability may become relevant. Quarks occupy
states up to a Fermi surface, and an attractive interaction between quarks
near this surface can lead to the formation of Cooper pairs. This is the
same basic mechanism as in BCS superconductivity, but the pairs are now
formed from quarks rather than electrons. Since quarks carry color charge,
the corresponding phase is called a color-superconducting phase
\cite{Shovkovy2005}.

The microscopic origin of the pairing can be illustrated by quark--quark
scattering through one-gluon exchange, shown schematically in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qq_one_gluon_exchange}. In QCD, this interaction is
attractive in the antisymmetric color-antitriplet channel. This makes the
normal quark Fermi surface unstable toward diquark condensation
\cite{Shovkovy2005}. In this sense, the pairing mechanism is more direct
than in ordinary electronic superconductivity: for electrons, single-photon
exchange is repulsive, and the effective attraction responsible for Cooper
pairing arises from the interaction with the medium, usually through
phonon exchange.

      i2 [particle=\(q_1\)]
      -- [anti fermion] b

\label{fig:qq_one_gluon_exchange}
\end{figure}

A diquark condensate corresponds to a nonzero expectation value of a
quark--quark operator. Since such an operator carries color, it is not
gauge invariant in QCD. By Elitzur's theorem, a local gauge symmetry
cannot be spontaneously broken in a gauge-invariant formulation
\cite{Elitzur1975}. The condensate should therefore not be interpreted as
a gauge-invariant order parameter in the same sense as the chiral
condensate. In an effective model, or after fixing a color orientation, the
diquark mean field can instead be used as a convenient representation of
the pairing pattern and of the resulting quasiparticle spectrum.

In the present work we restrict the discussion to two flavors, \(u\) and
\(d\). The relevant color-superconducting phase is then the two-flavor
color-superconducting phase, usually denoted by 2SC. In this phase, quarks
pair in a scalar channel that is antisymmetric in both color and flavor.
With a conventional choice of color orientation, the condensate pairs red
and green quarks, while the blue quarks remain unpaired, as shown
schematically in Fig.~\ref{fig:2sc_pairing_schematic}. This choice fixes
the orientation of the condensate in color space and is made for
mathematical convenience. An equivalent description could be obtained by a
color rotation, so the choice of which color remains unpaired should not
be interpreted as a physical preference for blue quarks. In the fixed color
basis used by the effective model, the corresponding pairing pattern
leaves an \(SU(2)_c\) subgroup of the original color structure unchanged
\cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2024}.

This distinction is important when using the Quark--Meson--Diquark model.
The model contains quarks, mesons, and diquark fields, but no dynamical
gluons. As a result, the color symmetry appearing in the model is treated
as a global symmetry. A nonzero value of \(\Delta_0\) therefore signals
spontaneous breaking of this global symmetry within the effective model.
This should be understood as a model representation of the 2SC pairing
structure, not as a gauge-invariant statement about symmetry breaking in
QCD. With this caveat, the effective model used below follows the formulation of
Andersen and Nødtvedt~\cite{Andersen2024}. The full renormalized one-loop
thermodynamic potential is not rederived in the main text.

\subsection*{Diquark degrees of freedom}

Following Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, the superconducting channel is
represented by a complex diquark field \(\Delta_a\), with color index
\(a=1,2,3\). Its schematic quark--quark structure may be written as
\begin{equation}
\Delta_a
\sim
\epsilon_{abc}\epsilon_{ij}
\bar{\psi}^{C\,i}_b \gamma_5 \psi^{\,j}_c ,
\label{eq:2sc_diquark_operator}
\end{equation}
where \(b,c\) are color indices and \(i,j\) are flavor indices. The field
\(\psi^C=C\bar{\psi}^T\) is the charge-conjugate quark field. The tensor
\(\epsilon_{abc}\) is antisymmetric in color space, while
\(\epsilon_{ij}\) is antisymmetric in the two-flavor space. Equivalently,
the flavor structure may be written using \(\tau_2\), up to a conventional
factor.

The structure of Eq.~\eqref{eq:2sc_diquark_operator} fixes the quantum
numbers of the diquark field. The antisymmetric tensor
\(\epsilon_{abc}\) makes the pair a color antitriplet, while
\(\epsilon_{ij}\) gives the antisymmetric flavor-singlet combination. The
factor \(\gamma_5\) selects the scalar spin-zero channel. This is the
standard diquark degree of freedom used in the two-flavor
Quark--Meson--Diquark model \cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2024}.

With the color orientation fixed as in
Fig.~\ref{fig:2sc_pairing_schematic}, we allow the third component of the
diquark field to develop a nonzero expectation value,
\begin{equation}
\Delta_3 \rightarrow \Delta_0 + \tilde{\Delta}_3 ,
\label{eq:2sc_condensate_choice}
\end{equation}
where \(\Delta_0\) is taken to be real and
\(\langle \tilde{\Delta}_3\rangle=0\). This choice fixes the orientation
of the condensate in color space. The other components of \(\Delta_a\) are
not set to zero as fields; rather, their expectation values vanish in this
chosen color basis.

The quantity \(\Delta_0\) is the mean field that appears in the
thermodynamic potential. The quasiparticle gap is proportional to
\begin{equation}
g_\Delta \Delta_0 ,
\end{equation}
where \(g_\Delta\) is the quark--diquark coupling introduced explicitly in
the Lagrangian below.

\subsection*{The Quark--Meson--Diquark Lagrangian}

The two-flavor Quark--Meson--Diquark model is obtained by adding the
diquark field to the Quark--Meson degrees of freedom. Following
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, we separate the Lagrangian into three parts,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QMD}}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QM}}
+
\mathcal{L}_{\Delta}
+
\mathcal{L}_{q\Delta},
\label{eq:qmd_lagrangian_split}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QM}}\) is the previously introduced
Quark--Meson Lagrangian, \(\mathcal{L}_{\Delta}\) contains the diquark
sector, and \(\mathcal{L}_{q\Delta}\) couples quarks to the diquark field.

The Quark--Meson part is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QM}}
&=
\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\sigma)(\partial^\mu\sigma)
+
\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\pi_0)(\partial^\mu\pi_0)
+
(\partial_\mu+i\mu_I\delta_{\mu0})\pi^+
(\partial^\mu-i\mu_I\delta^{\mu0})\pi^-
\nonumber \\
&\quad
-
\frac{1}{2}m^2(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})
-
\frac{\lambda}{24}(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})^2
+
h\sigma
+
\bar{\psi}(i\slashed{\partial}+\gamma^0\hat{\mu})\psi
-
g\bar{\psi}
\left[
\sigma+i\gamma^5\vec{\pi}\cdot\vec{\tau}
\right]\psi .
\label{eq:qmd_qm_part}
\end{align}
This is the same chiral quark--meson sector used in the previous sections,
now with the chemical-potential matrix \(\hat{\mu}\) allowing different
color and flavor components.

The diquark part is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\Delta}
&=
(\partial_\mu+2i\mu_a\delta_{\mu0})\Delta_a^\dagger
(\partial^\mu-2i\mu_a\delta^{\mu0})\Delta_a
-
m_\Delta^2\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a
\nonumber \\
&\quad
-
\frac{\lambda_3}{12}
(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a
-
\frac{\lambda_\Delta}{6}
(\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a)^2 .
\label{eq:qmd_diquark_part}
\end{align}
Here \(m_\Delta\) is the diquark mass parameter. The quantity \(\mu_a\)
denotes the chemical potential associated with the diquark field
\(\Delta_a\). With the convention used in Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, it
enters the covariant derivative as \(2\mu_a\). For the condensate chosen
in the third color direction, the relevant chemical potential is denoted
by \(\mu_3\). Its relation to the quark chemical potentials is specified
below when the thermodynamic potential is constructed. The coupling
\(\lambda_3\) links the chiral and diquark sectors, and
\(\lambda_\Delta\) is the diquark self-coupling that stabilizes the
potential in the diquark direction.

The quark--diquark interaction is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{q\Delta}
&=
\frac{1}{2}g_\Delta
\bar{\psi}_b^C
\Delta_a\gamma_5\tau_2\epsilon_{abc}\psi_c
+
\frac{1}{2}g_\Delta
\bar{\psi}_b
\Delta_a^\dagger\gamma_5\tau_2\epsilon_{abc}\psi_c^C .
\label{eq:qmd_quark_diquark_part}
\end{align}
The first term couples two quarks to the diquark field, while the second
term is its Hermitian conjugate. The coupling \(g_\Delta\) controls the
pairing strength; when \(\Delta_0\neq0\), the product
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\) is the gap scale in the paired quasiparticle
spectrum. The factors \(\tau_2\) and \(\epsilon_{abc}\) implement the same
antisymmetric flavor and color structure shown explicitly in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:2sc_diquark_operator}. The interaction is therefore in the
color-antitriplet, flavor-singlet scalar channel.

Combining Eqs.~\eqref{eq:qmd_qm_part}--\eqref{eq:qmd_quark_diquark_part},
the full Lagrangian is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}
&=
\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\sigma)(\partial^\mu\sigma)
+
\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\pi_0)(\partial^\mu\pi_0)
+
(\partial_\mu+i\mu_I\delta_{\mu0})\pi^+
(\partial^\mu-i\mu_I\delta^{\mu0})\pi^-
\nonumber \\
&\quad
-
\frac{1}{2}m^2(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})
-
\frac{\lambda}{24}(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})^2
+
h\sigma
\nonumber \\
&\quad
+
(\partial_\mu+2i\mu_a\delta_{\mu0})\Delta_a^\dagger
(\partial^\mu-2i\mu_a\delta^{\mu0})\Delta_a
-
m_\Delta^2\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a
\nonumber \\
&\quad
-
\frac{\lambda_3}{12}
(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a
-
\frac{\lambda_\Delta}{6}
(\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a)^2
\nonumber \\
&\quad
+
\bar{\psi}(i\slashed{\partial}+\gamma^0\hat{\mu})\psi
-
g\bar{\psi}
\left[
\sigma+i\gamma^5\vec{\pi}\cdot\vec{\tau}
\right]\psi
\nonumber \\
&\quad
+
\frac{1}{2}g_\Delta
\bar{\psi}_b^C
\Delta_a\gamma_5\tau_2\epsilon_{abc}\psi_c
+
\frac{1}{2}g_\Delta
\bar{\psi}_b
\Delta_a^\dagger\gamma_5\tau_2\epsilon_{abc}\psi_c^C .
\label{eq:qmd_full_lagrangian}
\end{align}

Compared with the ordinary Quark--Meson model, the superconducting
extension introduces the four new parameters
\begin{equation}
m_\Delta,\qquad
g_\Delta,\qquad
\lambda_3,\qquad
\lambda_\Delta .
\end{equation}
They are not fixed by the vacuum pion, sigma, and constituent-quark input
that determines the mesonic sector. They must therefore be treated as
additional phenomenological parameters of the effective model
\cite{Andersen2024}, and the superconducting equation of state depends on
their chosen values.

\subsection*{Mean-field thermodynamic potential}

Using the mean-field shifts introduced above for \(\sigma\) and
\(\Delta_3\), the fields \(\phi_0\) and \(\Delta_0\) are treated as real
background fields. The chiral mean field determines the constituent quark
mass,
\begin{equation}
M_q = g\phi_0 ,
\end{equation}
while the diquark mean field determines the superconducting gap scale
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\). The mean-field problem is therefore a coupled
minimization in \((\phi_0,\Delta_0)\), as in standard treatments of
color-superconducting quark matter \cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2024}.

The field shifts give the tree-level thermodynamic potential
\begin{align}
\Omega_0
&=
\frac{1}{2}m^2\phi_0^2
+
\left(m_\Delta^2-4\mu_3^2\right)\Delta_0^2
+
\frac{\lambda}{24}\phi_0^4
-
h\phi_0
\nonumber \\
&\quad
+
\frac{\lambda_3}{12}\phi_0^2\Delta_0^2
+
\frac{\lambda_\Delta}{6}\Delta_0^4 ,
\label{eq:qmd_tree_potential}
\end{align}
where
\begin{equation}
\mu_3=\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{ur}+\mu_{dg}\right).
\end{equation}
Here \(\mu_{ur}\) denotes the chemical potential of an up quark with red
color, while \(\mu_{dg}\) denotes the chemical potential of a down quark
with green color. These are two of the quarks that participate in the 2SC
pairing pattern. The diquark field \(\Delta_3\) represents a Cooper pair
made from two quarks, so the chemical potential associated with the pair is
\(\mu_{ur}+\mu_{dg}\). With the convention used in the Lagrangian, this
sum is written as \(2\mu_3\), which gives the term
\(-4\mu_3^2\Delta_0^2\) in Eq.~\eqref{eq:qmd_tree_potential}.

The term proportional to \(\lambda_3\) couples the chiral and diquark
sectors already at tree level, while the term proportional to
\(\lambda_\Delta\) is the diquark self-interaction.

The full thermodynamic potential also contains the quark one-loop
contribution. In the approximation used here, quark fluctuations are
included, while mesonic and diquark fluctuations are neglected. Because the
diquark condensate couples quarks to charge-conjugate quarks, the
fermionic determinant is evaluated in the Nambu--Gorkov form introduced
below.

Following Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, the resulting renormalized
thermodynamic potential is denoted by
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{0+1}
=
\Omega_{0+1}(\phi_0,\Delta_0;\bar{\mu},T).
\end{equation}
The full expression is lengthy and is therefore given in
Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential}. In the main text we only need its
structure: for each value of the chemical potential, the equilibrium state
is found by minimizing \(\Omega_{0+1}\) with respect to both mean fields,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial\Omega_{0+1}}{\partial\phi_0}=0,
\qquad
\frac{\partial\Omega_{0+1}}{\partial\Delta_0}=0 .
\label{eq:qmd_gap_equations}
\end{equation}
The first equation is the chiral gap equation, while the second is the
diquark gap equation. A solution with \(\Delta_0=0\) corresponds to
normal quark matter, whereas a solution with \(\Delta_0\neq0\)
corresponds to a color-superconducting state.

The physical pressure is obtained from the value of the thermodynamic
potential at the global minimum,
\begin{equation}
P=-\Omega_{0+1,\min}.
\end{equation}
Thus the superconducting equation of state is determined by the same
thermodynamic principle as in the ordinary Quark--Meson model, but with an
additional order parameter and a modified quasiparticle spectrum. When the
model is later adapted to compact-star matter, the same potential must also
be supplemented by the neutrality constraints appropriate for electrically
and color-neutral matter \cite{Andersen2025}.

\subsection*{Quasiparticle spectrum}

The main effect of the diquark condensate is to modify the fermionic
spectrum. Once the field \(\Delta_3\) develops the expectation value
\(\Delta_0\), the quark--diquark interaction in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qmd_quark_diquark_part} generates terms that mix quarks with
charge-conjugate quarks. This is written in the Nambu--Gorkov basis,
originally introduced for superconductivity \cite{Gorkov1958,Nambu1960}
and standard in color-superconducting mean-field calculations
\cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2024}.

For the present 2SC condensate, it is convenient to introduce the
Nambu--Gorkov spinor
\begin{equation}
\Psi =
\begin{pmatrix}
\psi \\
\psi^C
\end{pmatrix}.
\label{eq:qmd_nambu_gorkov_spinor}
\end{equation}
In this basis the inverse quark propagator is
\begin{equation}
D^{-1}(p)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\slashed{p}-g\phi_0+\gamma^0\hat{\mu}
&
i g_\Delta \tau_2\lambda_2\gamma^5\Delta_0
\\
i g_\Delta \tau_2\lambda_2\gamma^5\Delta_0
&
\slashed{p}-g\phi_0-\gamma^0\hat{\mu}
\end{pmatrix}.
\label{eq:qmd_inverse_propagator}
\end{equation}
The diagonal entries describe quarks and charge-conjugate quarks with
constituent mass \(g\phi_0\). The off-diagonal entries are proportional to
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\) and encode the pairing induced by the diquark
condensate. The tensor \(\epsilon_{3ab}\) associated with the chosen
condensate direction has been written as the Gell-Mann matrix
\(i\lambda_2\), which acts in the red--green color subspace
\cite{Andersen2024}.

The quasiparticle spectrum is obtained from the zeros of
\begin{equation}
\det D^{-1}(p)=0 .
\end{equation}
Because the condensate pairs only red and green quarks in the chosen color
basis, the blue quarks remain unpaired. Their dispersion relations are
\begin{align}
E_{ub}^{\pm}(p) &= E(p)\pm\mu_{ub}, \\
E_{db}^{\pm}(p) &= E(p)\pm\mu_{db},
\end{align}
where
\begin{equation}
E(p)=\sqrt{p^2+g^2\phi_0^2}.
\end{equation}
These modes have the same form as ordinary massive quark excitations.

The paired red-green quasiparticles have dispersion relations
\begin{equation}
E_{\Delta^\pm}^{\pm}(p)
=
E_\Delta^\pm(p)\pm\delta\mu ,
\label{eq:qmd_paired_spectrum}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{align}
E_\Delta^\pm(p)
&=
\sqrt{
\left(E(p)\pm\bar{\mu}\right)^2
+
g_\Delta^2\Delta_0^2
},
\\
\bar{\mu}
&=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{ur}+\mu_{dg}\right)
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{ug}+\mu_{dr}\right),
\\
\delta\mu
&=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{dg}-\mu_{ur}\right)
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{dr}-\mu_{ug}\right).
\end{align}
The quantity \(\bar{\mu}\) is the average chemical potential of the paired
quarks, while \(\delta\mu\) measures the mismatch between their Fermi
surfaces. When \(\delta\mu=0\), the paired modes have the standard
BCS-like form, with gap \(g_\Delta\Delta_0\).

The fermionic one-loop contribution to the thermodynamic potential is
obtained by summing over these quasiparticle energies. This completes the
theoretical definition of the QMD framework used here. The full
renormalized potential is collected in Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential},
including the finite residual integral \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\), whose
treatment is part of the numerical implementation. The next section uses
this framework to construct the equation of state for the 2SC phase.

\section{Numerical QMD equation of state with 2SC pairing}
\label{sec:qmd_section1}

The Quark--Meson--Diquark model defined above is now used as a
numerical equation of state. The additional mean field
\(\Delta_0\) makes the calculation more involved than the unpaired
Quark--Meson case, because the physical state is obtained by
minimizing the thermodynamic potential in the coupled
\((\phi_0,\Delta_0)\) plane and, for stellar matter, by imposing
electric and color neutrality at the same time. This section therefore
builds the QMD result in stages: first the parameter set and numerical
conventions are fixed, then a common-chemical-potential calculation is
used as a controlled benchmark, and finally the neutral stellar
equation of state is passed to the TOV equations. Unless explicitly
stated otherwise, all results use the full one-loop potential from
Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential}, including the finite residual
integral \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\).

\subsection*{Numerical setup and parameter choices}
\label{subsec:qmd_setup}

The thermodynamic potential depends on the chiral condensate
\(\phi_0\), the diquark condensate \(\Delta_0\), and the relevant
chemical potentials. The model parameters are separated into a vacuum
sector, which is fixed by on-shell matching, and a diquark sector,
which is not constrained by vacuum observables and must therefore be
chosen phenomenologically.

The Quark--Meson part of the Lagrangian contains four parameters:
\(m^2\), \(\lambda\), \(g\), and \(h\). These are fixed by matching to
the physical pion mass \(m_\pi\), the pion decay constant \(f_\pi\),
the constituent quark mass \(M_q\), and the sigma meson mass
\(m_\sigma\). We use the same on-shell scheme adopted in
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, in which the tree-level mass relations are
required to hold order by order in the loop expansion. The vacuum
expectation value of the sigma field is identified with the pion decay
constant, \(\phi_0 = f_\pi\).

The physical inputs are
\begin{align}
m_\pi &= 140~\mathrm{MeV}, &
f_\pi &= 93~\mathrm{MeV}, \\
M_q &= 300~\mathrm{MeV}, &
m_\sigma &= 600~\mathrm{MeV}.
\end{align}
These values are physical observables; they are identical to those used
in Ref.~\cite{Andersen2025}. They differ from the chiral-limit choice
\(m_\pi = 0\), \(m_\sigma = 500~\mathrm{MeV}\) used in the original
QMD paper~\cite{Andersen2024}, where the chiral limit was taken to
simplify the renormalization-group analysis. The analytic structure of
the one-loop potential is unchanged: the loop functions \(F(p^2)\) and
\(G(p^2)\) defined in Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential} simply evaluate
to their respective limits.

The on-shell matching conditions yield
\begin{align}
g &= \frac{M_q}{f_\pi} \approx 3.23, &
\lambda_0 &= \frac{3(m_\sigma^2 - m_\pi^2)}{f_\pi^2} \approx 118.1, \\
h &= f_\pi\, m_\pi^2 \approx 1.82\times 10^6~\mathrm{MeV}^3, &
m^2 &= \frac{1}{2}(3m_\pi^2 - m_\sigma^2) \approx -(388~\mathrm{MeV})^2.
\end{align}
The negative value of \(m^2\) confirms spontaneous chiral symmetry
breaking in the vacuum. The parameter \(h\) encodes the explicit
breaking and obeys the partially conserved axial current relation
\(h = f_\pi m_\pi^2\).

The renormalization scale is fixed at the vacuum value of the
constituent quark mass, \(\Lambda_0 = M_q\). At the vacuum field value
\(\phi_0 = f_\pi\), the arguments of the logarithms in the one-loop
potential reduce to \(\log(M_q^2/g^2\phi_0^2) = 0\), so that the vacuum
state is free of logarithmic corrections by construction. This is the
on-shell condition. With this choice, \(g\) and \(h\) do not run with
the renormalization scale, and the remaining parameters run according
to the renormalization group equations derived in
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024} and reproduced in
Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential}.

The diquark sector contains four parameters that cannot be fixed by
vacuum physics: \(m_\Delta\), \(g_\Delta\), \(\lambda_3\), and
\(\lambda_\Delta\). For the main results in this section we adopt a
single parameter set, hereafter referred to as QMD~SET~A:
\begin{align}
m_\Delta &= 500~\mathrm{MeV}, &
g_\Delta &= 2g, \\
\lambda_3 &= \lambda_0, &
\lambda_\Delta &= \lambda_0/4 .
\end{align}
This is the same diquark sector used in Set~1 of
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2025}, which makes our results directly comparable
with their analysis. The sensitivity of the model to these choices is
studied systematically in Sec.~\ref{sec:qmd_parameter_sensitivity}.

Each of these parameters has a distinct physical role. The bare diquark
mass \(m_\Delta\) determines the chemical potential at which the 2SC
condensate first becomes energetically favorable. At tree level, the
onset occurs at \(\bar{\mu} = m_\Delta/2\), so smaller \(m_\Delta\)
leads to earlier onset. The Yukawa coupling \(g_\Delta\) sets the
magnitude of the superconducting gap \(g_\Delta \Delta_0\) and
controls how strongly diquark pairing back-reacts on the chiral
condensate. The cross-coupling \(\lambda_3\) links the chiral and
diquark sectors at tree level; it shifts the onset and influences how
the chiral condensate responds to the formation of \(\Delta_0\). The
diquark self-coupling \(\lambda_\Delta\) stabilizes the potential
against unbounded growth of \(\Delta_0\) and sets the saturation value
of the gap in the deep 2SC phase.

One convention issue is worth making explicit. The one-loop potential
contains a term proportional to
\(\phi_0^2 \Delta_0^2\) with a logarithmic prefactor, arising from the
mixing of chiral and diquark fluctuations. In our notation this is
\begin{equation}
\Omega \supset
\frac{c\, g_0^2 g_{\Delta,0}^2}{(4\pi)^2}
\left[
\log\!\left(\frac{M_q^2}{g_0^2\phi_0^2 + g_{\Delta,0}^2\Delta_0^2}\right)
- F(m_\pi^2) - m_\pi^2 F'(m_\pi^2)
\right]
\phi_0^2 \Delta_0^2 ,
\label{eq:mixed_log_term}
\end{equation}
where the coefficient \(c\) deserves brief comment. Equation~(36) of
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024} gives \(c = 4\), while Eq.~(29) of
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2025} gives \(c = 8\); the discrepancy arises from
a different convention in the diquark loop counting. We use \(c = 4\),
consistent with the field normalization of Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}
and the explicit derivation in Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential}. The
choice has no practical consequence for any result in this chapter:
the mixed term is proportional to \(\phi_0^2 \Delta_0^2\) and therefore
vanishes as chiral symmetry is restored inside the 2SC phase, where
\(\phi_0 \ll f_\pi\). Numerical comparison confirms that switching
\(c = 4 \to 8\) with \(g_\Delta\) held fixed shifts the gap by less than
\(0.05~\mathrm{MeV}\) at all densities relevant to compact stars.

At fixed chemical potentials, the equilibrium state is obtained by
selecting the global minimum of the thermodynamic potential with
respect to \((\phi_0, \Delta_0)\). The chiral condensate determines the
constituent quark mass \(M_q = g\phi_0\), and the diquark condensate
determines the superconducting gap scale \(g_\Delta \Delta_0\). The
residual integral \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is evaluated numerically
with adaptive quadrature, using a momentum cutoff
\(\Lambda_\mathrm{res} = 3000~\mathrm{MeV}\) that lies well above the
relevant physical scales and is sufficient for convergence of the
integrand.

The thermodynamic potential is minimized using a bounded
quasi-Newton method, with multiple starting points chosen to span the
\((\phi_0, \Delta_0)\) plane in order to avoid local minima. The
solution at one value of \(\mu_q\) is used as the initial guess for the
neighboring grid point, providing a continuation procedure that is
particularly useful near first-order transitions where the global
minimum can move discontinuously.

\subsection*{Common-chemical-potential benchmark}
\label{subsec:qmd_benchmark}

Before imposing the stellar neutrality constraints, we first consider a
simpler benchmark calculation. In this case all quark flavors and colors
are assigned the same quark chemical potential,
\begin{equation}
\mu_{ur}=\mu_{ug}=\mu_{ub}=\mu_{dr}=\mu_{dg}=\mu_{db}=\mu_q.
\end{equation}
The average chemical potential of the paired quarks is then
\begin{equation}
\bar{\mu}=\mu_q,
\end{equation}
and the mismatch between the paired Fermi surfaces vanishes,
\begin{equation}
\delta\mu=0.
\end{equation}
This limit is not the physical compact-star case, since electric and
color neutrality are not imposed and the paired up and down quarks share
identical Fermi momenta. Its purpose is to validate the coupled
\((\phi_0,\Delta_0)\) minimizer in the simplest possible setting and to
establish the qualitative features of the 2SC phase in this model before
neutrality, the Fermi-surface mismatch, and the electron contribution are
added.

At each value of \(\mu_q\), the thermodynamic potential is first
evaluated at its global minimum,
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\min}(\mu_q)
=
\min_{\phi_0,\Delta_0}
\Omega_{0+1}(\phi_0,\Delta_0;\mu_q).
\end{equation}
After this minimization, \(\Omega_{\min}\) is a function only of the
external parameter \(\mu_q\). The pressure is obtained by subtracting the
corresponding vacuum reference,
\begin{equation}
P(\mu_q)
=
-\left[\Omega_{\min}(\mu_q)-\Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}\right],
\qquad
\Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}
=
\Omega_{\min}(\mu_q=0).
\end{equation}
This subtraction fixes \(P(0)=0\) and corresponds to a bag constant
\(B_0 = -\Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}\) with \(B_0^{1/4}\approx 155.3~\mathrm{MeV}\)
for QMD~SET~A. This is a property of the model vacuum, not an
independently tunable phenomenological parameter.

The quark number density follows from
\begin{equation}
n_q=\frac{dP}{d\mu_q},
\end{equation}
and the energy density from the zero-temperature thermodynamic identity
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon = -P + \mu_q\, n_q.
\end{equation}
The speed of sound squared is computed parametrically,
\begin{equation}
c_s^2 = \frac{dP}{d\varepsilon} = \frac{dP/d\mu_q}{d\varepsilon/d\mu_q}.
\end{equation}
In practice the derivatives are evaluated by finite differences on a
uniform \(\mu_q\) grid of 5000 points covering
\(\mu_q \in [0,900]~\mathrm{MeV}\), giving a step size of approximately
\(0.18~\mathrm{MeV}\).

The resulting condensates are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_benchmark_condensates}. At low chemical potential the
global minimum has \(\Delta_0=0\) and the chiral condensate sits at its
vacuum value \(\phi_0=f_\pi\). At \(\mu_q = 266.5~\mathrm{MeV}\) the
global minimum jumps discontinuously to a solution with nonzero
\(\Delta_0\), signaling a first-order transition into the 2SC phase.

The character of this transition deserves comment. At the onset itself
the chiral condensate barely moves: \(\phi_0\) drops only from
\(93.0~\mathrm{MeV}\) to \(92.7~\mathrm{MeV}\) across the transition,
while the gap jumps from zero to \(g_\Delta\Delta_0 \approx
14~\mathrm{MeV}\). The transition is therefore driven by the diquark
sector. Once inside the 2SC phase, the chiral condensate falls
continuously and rapidly: \(\phi_0 \to 5.9~\mathrm{MeV}\) at
\(\mu_q=400~\mathrm{MeV}\) and \(\phi_0 \to 0.7~\mathrm{MeV}\) at
\(\mu_q=900~\mathrm{MeV}\). Chiral restoration thus happens within the
2SC phase, not at the boundary.

\centering

\caption{Chiral condensate \(\phi_0\) (left) and superconducting gap
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\) (right) as functions of the common quark chemical

\(\mu_q=266.5~\mathrm{MeV}\). The horizontal dashed line on the right

Eq.~\eqref{eq:qmd_asymptotic_gap}.}
\label{fig:qmd_benchmark_condensates}
\end{figure}

At large \(\mu_q\) the gap saturates toward a constant value. This
asymptotic behavior was derived in Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024} from the gap
equation in the limit \(\bar{\mu} \gg \Delta_0\), with the result
\begin{equation}
\bar{\Delta}_0^{\,2}
=
\frac{M_q^2}{g_\Delta^2}
\exp\!\left[
\frac{(4\pi)^2}{4 g_\Delta^2}
- 1
- F(m_\pi^2) - m_\pi^2 F'(m_\pi^2)
\right],
\label{eq:qmd_asymptotic_gap}
\end{equation}
which for QMD~SET~A gives \(g_\Delta\bar{\Delta}_0 \approx
281.6~\mathrm{MeV}\) using physical pion masses. The numerical
calculation gives \(g_\Delta\Delta_0 = 252~\mathrm{MeV}\) at the upper
end of our grid, \(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\). The gap has not yet
saturated at this chemical potential; pushing the calculation to
\(\mu_q = 6~\mathrm{GeV}\) on an extended logarithmic grid yields a gap
of \(267~\mathrm{MeV}\), within ten percent of the analytic limit. The
analytic formula thus serves as a useful cross-check on the asymptotic
behavior of the numerical solution.

It is worth comparing the onset to the tree-level estimate. At tree level
the 2SC onset occurs at \(\bar{\mu} = m_\Delta/2 = 250~\mathrm{MeV}\) for
SET~A. The full one-loop result of \(266.5~\mathrm{MeV}\) lies about
\(7\%\) above this, reflecting the additional one-loop contributions to
the diquark sector.

The pressure as a function of \(\mu_q\) is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_benchmark_pressure}. The pressure vanishes at the
onset and rises monotonically into the 2SC phase. The corresponding
equation of state is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_benchmark_eos}: at low
pressure the relation \(\varepsilon(P)\) is steep, reflecting the
relatively soft EoS just above the transition, while at large \(\mu_q\)
the ratio \(\varepsilon/P\) approaches the conformal value \(3\).

approaching \(\frac{1}{3}\) from above as \(\mu_q\) increases. On the extended

The behavior of \(c_s^2\) is the most informative feature of the
benchmark. The peak above the conformal limit is a known qualitative
feature of the QMD model~\cite{Andersen2024,Andersen2025}, where it has
been argued to reflect the stiffening of the equation of state by the
diquark condensate: the pairing energy contributes to the pressure but
not to the leading \(\mu_q^4\) behavior of the energy density. The
approach to the conformal limit from above is consistent with hard
dense-loop resummed perturbation theory~\cite{Andersen2024} and contrasts
with naive perturbative QCD expectations of an approach from below.

Together, these benchmark results show that the numerical implementation
reproduces the qualitative features of the QMD model documented in
Refs.~\cite{Andersen2024,Andersen2025}: a first-order transition into
the 2SC phase, asymptotic gap saturation, and a speed of sound that
peaks above the conformal limit before approaching it from above. The
direct comparison with Andersen~2024 is partial, since that paper
worked in the chiral limit (\(m_\pi=0\), \(m_\sigma=500~\mathrm{MeV}\))
while we use physical meson masses. The qualitative features are
unaffected, but the onset \(\mu_q\) and asymptotic gap differ at the
several-percent level.

The benchmark also makes clear what remains to be done before the
calculation describes stellar matter. Neutrality is not imposed, so
\(\mu_e=\mu_8=0\) throughout. The Fermi surfaces of the paired quarks
are exactly matched, \(\delta\mu=0\), so the model cannot produce a
gapless 2SC phase. The pressure has no electron contribution. None of
these features can be carried over to the compact-star calculation. The
benchmark serves only to validate the QMD machinery; the physically
relevant equation of state requires the additional ingredients
introduced below.

\subsection*{Truncation comparison: effect of $\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}$}
\label{subsec:truncation_comparison}

The common-\(\mu_q\) benchmark established that the QMD model with the
full one-loop potential reproduces the expected qualitative features of
the 2SC phase: a first-order onset, asymptotic gap saturation, and a
speed of sound that peaks above the conformal limit. Before extending
the calculation to neutral stellar matter, we examine the role of the
finite residual integral \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) by comparing
results obtained with and without this term.

The motivation for this comparison is practical. The integral
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is evaluated numerically at every step of
the \((\phi_0,\Delta_0)\) minimization and is the most computationally
expensive part of the calculation. An earlier version of the present
work set \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}} = 0\) under the assumption that this
finite, UV-suppressed contribution would only shift quantitative results
by a few percent. The comparison below shows that this assumption is
incorrect: setting \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}} = 0\) qualitatively changes
the phase structure of the model.

The residual integral arises in the renormalization of the one-loop
quark contribution to the thermodynamic potential in the presence of a
diquark condensate. Following the derivation in
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, the divergent parts of the quark loop are
absorbed into the running couplings using \(\overline{\mathrm{MS}}\)
renormalization. After subtraction, a finite, UV-convergent integral
remains, given in our notation by
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}
=
-\frac{2}{\pi^2}\int_0^{\Lambda_\mathrm{res}}
dp\,p^2
\left[
E^\Delta_+(p)+E^\Delta_-(p)
- 2 E_\Delta(p)
- \frac{\bar{\mu}^2\, g_\Delta^2 \Delta_0^2}{E_\Delta(p)^3}
\right],
\label{eq:omega_1_num}
\end{equation}
where \(E(p)=\sqrt{p^2+g^2\phi_0^2}\),
\(E_\Delta(p)=\sqrt{p^2+g^2\phi_0^2+g_\Delta^2\Delta_0^2}\), and
\(E^\Delta_\pm(p)=\sqrt{(E(p)\pm\bar{\mu})^2 + g_\Delta^2\Delta_0^2}\).
The final subtraction term in the integrand is the counterterm that
removes the ultraviolet divergence; the remainder is finite in three
dimensions.

A reader unfamiliar with the structure of the calculation might
reasonably expect \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) to be a small correction.
Unlike the medium term in the analytic part of the potential, which
scales as \(\bar{\mu}^4\) at large density,
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is UV-finite by construction and contains
no leading \(\bar{\mu}^4\) contribution. Its leading density dependence
enters through the chemical-potential dependence of the paired
quasiparticle energies \(E^\Delta_\pm\), and is logarithmically
suppressed. Truncating \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is therefore
superficially defensible on power-counting grounds.

To isolate its effect, we compare two calculations that differ only in whether
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is included. Both use the same parameter
set (QMD~SET~A) and the same common-\(\mu_q\) calculation as in
Sec.~\ref{subsec:qmd_benchmark}. The full calculation uses
\(\Lambda_\mathrm{res} = 3000~\mathrm{MeV}\) as the upper limit of the
quadrature; the truncated calculation sets the entire integrand to
zero. All other model parameters, the minimizer settings, and the
numerical grid are held fixed. The truncated calculation uses 2000
grid points; the full calculation uses 5000. The truncated window
width has been verified to converge at higher resolution; the values
quoted are stable to better than $0.3\%$ at $12\times$ finer grid
spacing.

The condensate profiles for the two calculations are compared in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_truncation_condensates}. The contrast is immediate.

\caption{Comparison of the chiral condensate \(\phi_0\) (left) and the

\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}=0\) (amber). Both calculations use QMD~SET~A

In the full calculation the 2SC condensate appears at
\(\mu_q = 266.5~\mathrm{MeV}\) and persists across the entire range up
to \(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\), with the gap growing toward its
asymptotic value of approximately \(282~\mathrm{MeV}\). The chiral
condensate is still near its vacuum value \(\phi_0 \approx f_\pi\) at the
2SC onset, and falls continuously to a few MeV as \(\mu_q\) increases
within the 2SC phase. In the truncated calculation, the chiral
condensate begins to fall at \(\mu_q \approx 302~\mathrm{MeV}\) in a
smooth crossover, reaching \(\phi_0 \approx 15~\mathrm{MeV}\) before any
diquark condensate appears. The 2SC condensate then appears at
\(\mu_q = 329.7~\mathrm{MeV}\), reaches a peak gap of approximately
\(183~\mathrm{MeV}\) almost immediately, and collapses back to zero by
\(\mu_q = 334.0~\mathrm{MeV}\). The window in which the 2SC phase
exists is only approximately \(4.4~\mathrm{MeV}\) wide; beyond it, the system reverts
permanently to normal quark matter.

The dip-and-recovery feature visible in the truncated \(\phi_0\) curve
across the 2SC window is real. When \(\Delta_0\) briefly turns on, the
\(\lambda_3 \phi_0^2 \Delta_0^2\) cross-coupling pulls \(\phi_0\) down
further. When \(\Delta_0\) collapses, \(\phi_0\) rebounds. This is a
direct visualization of the \(\sigma\)--\(\Delta\) coupling in the
Lagrangian and is the only signature of diquark physics that survives
in the truncated calculation.

The qualitative difference between the two calculations can be traced
to the structure of the diquark sector of the potential. The
tree-level coefficient of \(\Delta_0^2\) is \((m_\Delta^2 -
4\bar{\mu}^2)\), which for QMD~SET~A turns negative at
\(\bar{\mu} = m_\Delta/2 = 250~\mathrm{MeV}\) and drives the
formation of a non-zero \(\Delta_0\). With
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) included, the loop-level binding term
proportional to \(\bar{\mu}^2 \Delta_0^2\) with a logarithmically
growing prefactor deepens the 2SC minimum as \(\bar{\mu}\) increases,
sustaining the phase indefinitely. Without \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\),
only the analytic one-loop logarithmic terms remain to balance the
tree-level driving. The combination of these terms with the quartic
self-coupling produces a 2SC minimum that exists only in a narrow
window of \(\bar{\mu}\): at lower chemical potential the tree-level
driving is too weak to overcome the cost imposed by the chiral-sector
log terms proportional to
\(\log[m_q^2 / (g^2\phi_0^2 + g_\Delta^2 \Delta_0^2)]\), and at
higher chemical potential the same log terms grow rapidly with
\(\Delta_0\) and eliminate the 2SC minimum entirely. A reduced
two-term model retaining only the tree-level mass term and the quartic
gives a permanent 2SC phase above \(\bar{\mu} = m_\Delta/2\) with no
upper closure, confirming that the closure observed in the truncated
calculation is driven by the analytic one-loop log structure rather
than by the quartic alone.

The role of \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is to provide an additional
attractive contribution that scales as \(\bar{\mu}^2 \Delta_0^2\) with
a logarithmically growing prefactor. Schematically, the deep-2SC
expansion of \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) yields a contribution to the
\(\Delta_0^2\) coefficient of order
\begin{equation}
-\frac{16 g_\Delta^2}{(4\pi)^2}
\left[
\log\!\frac{M_q^2}{g_\Delta^2 \Delta_0^2}
- F(m_\pi^2) - m_\pi^2 F'(m_\pi^2)
\right]
\bar{\mu}^2,
\end{equation}
which deepens the 2SC minimum as \(\bar{\mu}\) increases and prevents
the quartic term from closing it. This term has the same parametric
structure as the diquark-loop binding in the BCS gap equation, and is
responsible for the asymptotic saturation behavior derived in
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024} and discussed in
Sec.~\ref{subsec:qmd_benchmark}. Removing
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) removes the loop-level binding that
underlies the 2SC phase at finite density. The narrow window observed
for QMD~SET~A is itself parameter-sensitive across the diquark sector,
confirming that the truncated 2SC phase is not a stable feature of the
model but a knife-edge configuration whose existence and extent depend
strongly on the chosen parameters.

The parameter sensitivity of the truncated calculation is shown
explicitly in Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_truncation_parameter_variations},
which compares the full and truncated diquark gap for three
representative parameter choices. At \(m_\Delta = 400~\mathrm{MeV}\)
the truncated window widens to approximately \(86~\mathrm{MeV}\); at
\(m_\Delta = 700~\mathrm{MeV}\) the truncated 2SC phase disappears
entirely; at \(\lambda_\Delta = \lambda_0/8\) the window broadens
compared to QMD~SET~A. In all three cases the full calculation
produces a stable, sustained 2SC phase. The chaotic dependence of the
truncated behavior on the diquark parameters confirms that what
stabilizes the 2SC phase in the full calculation is the residual
integral \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\), and that the surviving
configurations in the truncated calculation are accidents of how the
analytic terms balance at particular parameter values.

The qualitative failure of the truncated calculation has direct
quantitative consequences for the equation of state. Near the full
2SC onset at \(\mu_q = 270~\mathrm{MeV}\), the truncated pressure is
essentially zero while the full pressure is already \(\sim 10^6~
\mathrm{MeV}^4\); the relative difference is effectively \(100\%\). At
\(\mu_q = 350~\mathrm{MeV}\), inside the stable normal-quark-matter
phase of the truncated model, the truncated pressure is about
\(28\%\) of the full pressure. The difference closes only slowly, with
the truncated pressure still \(\sim 14\%\) below the full result at
\(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\).

The speed of sound shows a similar story. In the stable normal phase of
the truncated model, \(c_s^2\) reaches the conformal value
\(c_s^2 = \frac{1}{3}\) by \(\mu_q \approx 500~\mathrm{MeV}\) and remains
there. It never exceeds the conformal bound. The peak \(c_s^2 \approx
0.41\) that characterizes the 2SC phase in the full calculation is
entirely absent.

The conclusion is that \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is not a numerical
convenience but a structurally necessary part of the QMD potential at
stellar densities. The truncated calculation does not produce a stable
prediction for the 2SC phase at all: as
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_truncation_parameter_variations} demonstrates, its
behavior depends chaotically on the diquark parameters, with some
choices producing narrow windows of a few MeV, others producing wide
windows of order \(100~\mathrm{MeV}\), and others producing no 2SC
phase whatsoever. None of these truncated configurations corresponds
to a thermodynamically meaningful 2SC phase at densities reachable in
compact stars. This is the opposite of what the full calculation
predicts, and is at odds with the analytic asymptotic gap
formula~\eqref{eq:qmd_asymptotic_gap} derived in
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, which is itself a consequence of including
the diquark-loop contribution.

All stellar calculations in the remainder of this chapter therefore
use the full one-loop potential. The remaining task is to impose
electric and color neutrality on the model and to extract the
equation of state of neutral stellar matter.

\subsection*{Neutral stellar equation of state}
\label{subsec:neutral_stellar_eos}

The common-\(\mu_q\) calculation served to validate the numerical
implementation of the QMD model. To describe matter in a compact star
we now impose the additional conditions appropriate to bulk stellar
matter: electric charge neutrality, color charge neutrality, and weak
(\(\beta\)) equilibrium. Each of these introduces a new chemical
potential or constraint, and the result is a single-parameter equation
of state \(\varepsilon(P)\) suitable for input to the TOV equations.

The physical reasons for imposing the three constraints are well
documented in the color-superconductivity literature. We summarize them
briefly here; the conditions themselves and their consequences are
discussed in detail in Refs.~\cite{Shovkovy2005, Andersen2025}.

As in the two-flavor stellar calculation of Sec.~\ref{subsec:stellar_matter_qm}, we
impose $\beta$-equilibrium, $\mu_d = \mu_u + \mu_e$, and electric charge neutrality,
$\tfrac{2}{3}n_u - \tfrac{1}{3}n_d - n_e = 0$. The 2SC condensate introduces an
additional requirement: it carries a non-zero color charge \(Q_8\), since only the
red and green quarks participate in pairing while the blue quarks remain unpaired.
We therefore introduce the color chemical potential \(\mu_8\) and impose
\begin{equation}
n_8
=
-\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_8}
=
0.
\end{equation}
The third color generator \(T_3\) does not require a separate chemical
potential: the chosen 2SC condensate is symmetric between the red and
green colors, so \(n_3 = 0\) is satisfied automatically.

The individual quark chemical potentials are then
\begin{align}
\mu_{ur} = \mu_{ug} &= \mu_q - \frac{2}{3}\mu_e + \frac{1}{3}\mu_8,
\\
\mu_{dr} = \mu_{dg} &= \mu_q + \frac{1}{3}\mu_e + \frac{1}{3}\mu_8,
\\
\mu_{ub} &= \mu_q - \frac{2}{3}\mu_e - \frac{2}{3}\mu_8,
\\
\mu_{db} &= \mu_q + \frac{1}{3}\mu_e - \frac{2}{3}\mu_8.
\end{align}
The average chemical potential of the paired (red--green) up and down
quarks is
\begin{equation}
\bar{\mu}
=
\frac{1}{2}(\mu_{ur} + \mu_{dg})
=
\mu_q - \frac{1}{6}\mu_e + \frac{1}{3}\mu_8,
\end{equation}
and the mismatch between their Fermi surfaces is
\begin{equation}
\delta\mu
=
\frac{1}{2}(\mu_{dg} - \mu_{ur})
=
\frac{1}{2}\mu_e.
\end{equation}
The mismatch \(\delta\mu\) measures the stress on Cooper pairs. When
\(\delta\mu\) approaches the gap \(g_\Delta \Delta_0\), the paired
quasiparticle modes become gapless, and the system enters the gapless
2SC regime~\cite{Shovkovy2005, Alford2008}. We monitor this condition
explicitly below.

The total thermodynamic potential for neutral stellar matter is
\begin{equation}
\Omega_\mathrm{tot}(\phi_0, \Delta_0; \mu_q, \mu_e, \mu_8)
=
\Omega_{0+1}(\phi_0, \Delta_0; \mu_q, \mu_e, \mu_8)
+
\Omega_e(\mu_e),
\end{equation}
where \(\Omega_{0+1}\) is the full one-loop QMD potential introduced in
Sec.~\ref{subsec:qmd_benchmark}, and \(\Omega_e(\mu_e)\) is the
contribution of a free electron Fermi gas at zero temperature. The
electrons are treated as free since electromagnetic loop corrections
are negligible at the scales of interest, and the electron mass is
small relative to \(\mu_e\) in the 2SC phase.

At each fixed value of the quark chemical potential \(\mu_q\), the
physical state is determined by the four coupled equations
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial \Omega_\mathrm{tot}}{\partial \phi_0} &= 0,
&
\frac{\partial \Omega_\mathrm{tot}}{\partial \Delta_0} &= 0,
\label{eq:qmd_gap_equations_neutral}
\\
\frac{\partial \Omega_\mathrm{tot}}{\partial \mu_e} &= 0,
&
\frac{\partial \Omega_\mathrm{tot}}{\partial \mu_8} &= 0.
\label{eq:qmd_neutrality_conditions}
\end{align}
The first pair are the gap equations for the chiral and diquark
condensates. The second pair are the neutrality conditions. Together
they determine \(\{\phi_0, \Delta_0, \mu_e, \mu_8\}\) as functions of
\(\mu_q\).

Numerically, the system is solved by a nested procedure. For each
trial pair \((\phi_0, \Delta_0)\), the two neutrality conditions are
solved as a nonlinear root-finding problem for \((\mu_e, \mu_8)\). The
resulting neutral thermodynamic potential is then minimized over
\((\phi_0, \Delta_0)\). The solution at one value of \(\mu_q\) is used
as the starting point for the next, which is essential for tracking
the solution across the first-order onset where the global minimum
moves discontinuously. The calculation uses 700 grid points in
\(\mu_q \in [250, 900]~\mathrm{MeV}\), giving a spacing of
approximately \(0.93~\mathrm{MeV}\).

The neutral 2SC phase appears via a first-order transition at
\begin{equation}
\mu_q^{\,\mathrm{2SC}} = 278.8~\mathrm{MeV}.
\end{equation}
This is shifted upward by \(12.3~\mathrm{MeV}\) relative to the
common-\(\mu_q\) onset at \(266.5~\mathrm{MeV}\) found in
Sec.~\ref{subsec:qmd_benchmark}. The shift is a direct consequence of
the Fermi-surface mismatch \(\delta\mu = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu_e\): at the onset of
pairing in neutral matter, \(\mu_e\) jumps from a negligibly small
value to several MeV, and the resulting \(\delta\mu\) raises the
threshold chemical potential for the 2SC condensate.

The condensate profiles are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_stellar_condensates}, overlaid with the
common-\(\mu_q\) results from Sec.~\ref{subsec:qmd_benchmark} for
comparison. The qualitative behavior is the same in both calculations:
\(\phi_0\) jumps slightly at the onset and then falls continuously
through the 2SC phase, while the diquark gap grows from a small value
at the onset toward an asymptotic plateau. In neutral matter,
\(\phi_0 = 92.95~\mathrm{MeV}\) immediately after the transition and
falls to \(\phi_0 \approx 1~\mathrm{MeV}\) by
\(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\). The gap rises from \(g_\Delta \Delta_0
= 5.6~\mathrm{MeV}\) at onset to \(225.6~\mathrm{MeV}\) at the upper
end of the grid. This is slightly below the common-\(\mu_q\) value of
\(252~\mathrm{MeV}\) at the same \(\mu_q\); the mismatch
\(\delta\mu\) reduces the available phase space for pairing and
suppresses the gap.

The electron and color chemical potentials are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_stellar_neutrality}. Their behavior is essentially
that anticipated by the asymptotic analysis of
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2025}: \(\mu_e\) jumps to a finite value at the
2SC onset and rises approximately linearly with \(\mu_q\) thereafter,
while \(\mu_8\) starts at a substantial negative value near the
onset, crosses zero, and turns positive at high density.
Quantitatively, the ratio \(\mu_e/\mu_q\) plateaus between
\(0.60\)--\(0.63\) over \(\mu_q \in [330, 700]~\mathrm{MeV}\) and
relaxes toward \(0.50\) at the upper end of the grid, consistent with
the asymptotic prediction \(\mu_e \to 0.57\,\mu_q\) from
Eq.~(53) of Ref.~\cite{Andersen2025}. The color potential \(\mu_8\)
crosses zero at \(\mu_q \approx 630~\mathrm{MeV}\) and reaches
\(+0.024\,\mu_q\) at \(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\), approaching the
predicted asymptotic value \(\mu_8 \to 0.09\,\mu_q\) from
Eq.~(54) of Ref.~\cite{Andersen2025} but not yet converged at the
upper end of our grid.

\end{figure}

The large negative value of \(\mu_8\) immediately after the onset
reflects the strong color imbalance when the diquark condensate is
small: a substantial compensating color chemical potential is needed
to enforce neutrality between the paired red--green sector and the
unpaired blue sector. As the gap grows, the imbalance is reduced and
the magnitude of \(\mu_8\) decreases.

We monitor the difference \(g_\Delta \Delta_0 - \delta\mu\)
throughout the 2SC phase. A positive value indicates conventional BCS
2SC with fully gapped quasiparticles; a negative value indicates the
gapless 2SC regime. For QMD~SET~A with neutrality imposed, the gap
exceeds the mismatch over essentially the entire density range. The
difference becomes marginally negative only at \(\mu_q
\approx 858~\mathrm{MeV}\), where \(g_\Delta \Delta_0 - \delta\mu
\approx -0.004~\mathrm{MeV}\), and reaches \(-1.4~\mathrm{MeV}\) at
\(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\). The stellar densities reached in the
mass--radius calculation below all lie well within the
conventional, fully gapped 2SC phase. We do not pursue the gapless
regime further here.

The pressure, energy density, and speed of sound are constructed from
the solved \(\Omega_\mathrm{tot}(\mu_q)\) using the same expressions
as in the common-\(\mu_q\) case,
\begin{equation}
P(\mu_q)
=
-[\Omega_\mathrm{tot,min}(\mu_q) - \Omega_\mathrm{vac}],
\qquad
n_q = \frac{dP}{d\mu_q},
\qquad
\varepsilon = -P + \mu_q n_q,
\end{equation}
where \(\Omega_\mathrm{vac}\) is the value of the neutral
\(\Omega_\mathrm{tot}\) at \(\mu_q = 0\). The speed of sound squared
is then \(c_s^2 = (dP/d\mu_q)/(d\varepsilon/d\mu_q)\).

The raw \(P(\mu_q)\) is non-monotonic in a narrow window around the
2SC onset, reflecting the first-order character of the transition.
This is mechanically unstable and cannot be passed directly to the
TOV equations. We apply a Maxwell construction to remove the
unstable segment, replacing it with a constant-pressure tie line
between the two coexisting branches. For QMD~SET~A, the Maxwell
construction removes three points in the onset region.
Points with \(P \leq 0\) or \(\varepsilon \leq 0\) are then
excluded; twenty-two grid points fall in the low-density vacuum
region where the bag-subtracted pressure is negative. Three further
points are removed by a monotonicity filter applied to the stable
branch. After these steps (\(700 - 3 - 22 - 3 = 672\)), the EoS
table consists of \(672\) points satisfying
\(P > 0\), \(\varepsilon > 0\), and monotonic \(\varepsilon(P)\).

The resulting \(P(\varepsilon)\) is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_stellar_eos}. The speed of sound, shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_stellar_cs2}, rises well above the conformal limit
\(c_s^2 = \frac{1}{3}\). The raw finite-difference table contains a narrow
maximum \(c_s^2 \approx 0.45\) at \(\mu_q \approx 478~\mathrm{MeV}\);
the smoothed curve plotted in the figure suppresses this local grid
spike and peaks at about \(c_s^2 \approx 0.39\). At higher \(\mu_q\)
it relaxes toward \(\frac{1}{3}\) from above, reaching \(c_s^2 = 0.34\) at
\(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\). The qualitative behavior of \(c_s^2\)
matches the common-\(\mu_q\) benchmark: the neutral calculation
retains the same stiffening feature, while the composition degrees of
freedom (\(\mu_e\), \(\mu_8\)) absorb part of the response of the
system to changes in density and slightly soften the EoS.

A point that requires explicit comment is the behavior of the EoS at
zero pressure. In the present QMD calculation,
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon(P = 0) \approx 0.
\end{equation}
This is in contrast to MIT-bag-type models, in which an explicit bag
constant \(B\) gives \(\varepsilon(P=0) = 4B\) and the resulting
quark stars are self-bound, terminating at a finite surface energy
density. In the QMD model there is no explicit bag constant: the
renormalized vacuum is identified with the chiral-broken minimum at
\(\phi_0 = f_\pi\), \(\Delta_0 = 0\), and both \(P\) and
\(\varepsilon\) vanish there. The TOV integration therefore follows
the stable branch down to vacuum, and the resulting configurations
are gravitationally bound rather than self-bound. This distinction is
important for the interpretation of the mass--radius curves: low-mass
QMD stars extend to large radii, in
contrast to the topology of compact self-bound quark stars.

Overall, the neutral QMD equation of state for QMD~SET~A is a single-parameter
relation \(P(\varepsilon)\) describing a system that is in the
chirally-broken vacuum at low chemical potential and in the 2SC phase
above \(\mu_q = 278.8~\mathrm{MeV}\). The 2SC phase is conventional
BCS over the full density range relevant to compact stars, with no
gapless modes. The speed of sound peaks above the conformal limit
and relaxes toward it at high density. The EoS terminates at zero
energy density at the surface; the resulting stars are
gravitationally bound. This stable EoS table is the input for the TOV
calculation below.

\subsection*{Mass--radius relations}

The stable neutral equation of state constructed above is now passed to the TOV
equations to obtain the corresponding sequence of compact-star
configurations. This produces the principal numerical result of the
QMD calculation: the maximum mass and radius
of a 2SC quark star in the QMD model at the QMD~SET~A baseline.

The hydrostatic structure of a static, spherically symmetric,
non-rotating compact star is governed by the
Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff equations,
\begin{align}
\frac{dm}{dr} &= 4\pi r^2 \varepsilon(r),
\\
\frac{dP}{dr} &= -\frac{G\bigl[\varepsilon(r)+P(r)\bigr]
\bigl[m(r)+4\pi r^3 P(r)\bigr]}
{r\bigl[r - 2 G m(r)\bigr]},
\end{align}
with the boundary conditions \(m(0)=0\) and \(P(0)=P_c\), where \(P_c\)
is the central pressure. The integration proceeds outward until the
pressure vanishes, defining the stellar surface at \(r=R\) and the
gravitational mass \(M=m(R)\).

We use the same solver employed in the earlier two-flavor
Quark--Meson stellar calculation, with the only change being the
equation of state. The stable neutral QMD EoS is interpolated as a cubic
spline \(\varepsilon(P)\) with extrapolation at both ends. The
monotonicity of \(\varepsilon(P)\) is checked on the spline grid
before TOV integration. The central pressure is scanned
geometrically, with each step multiplying \(P_c\) by a factor of
\(1.08\), spanning the range of the stable EoS table. The radial
integration step is \(0.01~\mathrm{km}\), using a fourth-order
Runge-Kutta (RK4) scheme with a single forward-Euler bootstrap step
from the origin to avoid the \(1/r^2\) singularity in the TOV
equations. The resulting sequence
contains \(285\) configurations.

The stability of each configuration is determined by the sign of the
slope of the mass--central-density relation,
\begin{equation}
\frac{dM}{d\varepsilon_c} > 0
\qquad\text{(stable branch),}
\end{equation}
with the maximum mass corresponding to the turning point. Beyond this
point the sequence is unstable against radial perturbations and the
configurations would collapse in finite time. Of the \(285\)
configurations integrated, \(253\) are stable and \(32\) are
unstable.

The resulting mass--radius relation is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_mass_radius}. The stable branch is plotted as a
solid curve and the unstable branch as a dashed curve, with the
maximum-mass configuration marked by a filled circle.

\centering

\caption{Mass--radius relation for the neutral QMD equation of state
with 2SC pairing (QMD~SET~A, full one-loop potential). The solid curve
shows the stable branch, the dashed curve the unstable branch beyond

\(M_{\max} = 1.970~M_\odot\) at \(R = 12.16~\mathrm{km}\).}
\label{fig:qmd_mass_radius}
\end{figure}

The principal numerical results are
\begin{align}
M_{\max} &= 1.970~M_\odot,
&
R(M_{\max}) &= 12.16~\mathrm{km},
\end{align}
with central energy density \(\varepsilon_c = 1.196~\mathrm{GeV/fm^3}\)
at the maximum-mass configuration. At lower masses, representative
stars on the stable branch have
\begin{align}
R(1.4\,M_\odot) &= 14.80~\mathrm{km},
&
R(1.8\,M_\odot) &= 13.48~\mathrm{km},
\end{align}
with central energy densities \(\varepsilon_c(1.4\,M_\odot) =
0.433~\mathrm{GeV/fm^3}\) and \(\varepsilon_c(1.8\,M_\odot) =
0.650~\mathrm{GeV/fm^3}\). Across the stable branch, radii lie
between approximately \(12\) and \(17~\mathrm{km}\) for masses in the
range \(0.5\)--\(1.97~M_\odot\).

A notable feature of the QMD mass--radius curve is that the radius
grows without bound as the mass decreases. Stable configurations
with \(M \sim 10^{-3}\,M_\odot\) have radii of order
\(25\)--\(30~\mathrm{km}\), and the limit \(R \to \infty\) is reached
as \(M \to 0\). This is the topology of gravitationally-bound matter
and is qualitatively different from the mass--radius curves of
self-bound (MIT-bag-type) quark stars, where the radius shrinks
toward zero as the mass decreases. The difference is a direct
consequence of the surface condition established above: in the QMD model there is no
explicit bag constant, the renormalized vacuum has
\(\varepsilon = P = 0\), and the EoS terminates at zero energy
density rather than at a finite surface value.

The very-low-mass branch is a mathematical solution of the TOV
equations but not a physically realistic description of compact
stars. Observed neutron stars have masses in the range
\(1\)--\(2\,M_\odot\), and the present pure-quark calculation lacks
the nuclear-matter crust that would bind the outer regions of a real
star. The portion of the curve relevant for comparison with
astrophysical data is the band of radii \(R \approx 12\)--\(15~\mathrm{km}\)
spanning \(M \approx 1\)--\(1.96~\,M_\odot\).

To isolate the effect of the diquark condensate on stellar
properties, the QMD sequence is compared in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_vs_qm_mass_radius} with two Quark--Meson sequences
at the same sigma meson mass \(m_\sigma = 600~\mathrm{MeV}\). The two
QM sequences differ only in bound-state construction and serve as
complementary references.

The primary (matched) comparison uses the gravitationally-bound QM
sequence (\(B_{\min} = 0\)) discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:qm_grav_bound},
which shares the same vacuum structure as QMD: both models normalize
the vacuum pressure to zero via \(P(\mu_q=0)=0\) and produce stars whose
surface sits at zero baryon density. This matched comparison directly
isolates the contribution of the 2SC diquark condensate to the stellar
observables. The gravitationally-bound QM sequence gives
\(M_{\max} = 1.797~M_\odot\) at \(R = 11.8~\mathrm{km}\) and
\(R(1.4~M_\odot) = 14.1~\mathrm{km}\),
yielding
\begin{align}
\Delta M_{\max} &= +0.17~M_\odot,
&
\Delta R(1.4\,M_\odot) &= +0.70~\mathrm{km}.
\end{align}

The secondary reference uses the self-bound QM sequence with
\(B^{1/4} = B_{\min}^{1/4} = 27.8~\mathrm{MeV}\) (the absolute
stability criterion introduced in Sec.~\ref{subsec:qm_stellar_structure}).
This sequence gives \(M_{\max} = 1.795~M_\odot\) at
\(R = 11.1~\mathrm{km}\) and \(R(1.4~M_\odot) = 12.6~\mathrm{km}\),
so
\begin{align}
\Delta M_{\max} &= +0.18~M_\odot,
&
\Delta R(1.4\,M_\odot) &= +2.15~\mathrm{km}.
\end{align}

The two comparisons bracket the 2SC contribution to stellar
observables. The matched comparison (\(\Delta R = +0.70~\mathrm{km}\))
isolates the diquark effect on the radius; the self-bound comparison
(\(\Delta R = +2.15~\mathrm{km}\)) adds to this the effect of the bag
constant's radial stiffening, which shifts the QM radius inward by
\(\sim 1.5~\mathrm{km}\). The condensate \(\Delta_0\) raises the
pressure at fixed quark chemical potential through the 2SC pairing
energy, producing larger and more massive equilibrium configurations.
The topology difference visible in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_vs_qm_mass_radius} between the small-mass branches
of the QMD and gravitationally-bound QM sequences confirms the common
vacuum construction: both curves extend to large radii at low mass,
while the self-bound curve collapses to a compact island.

The maximum mass \(M_{\max} = 1.970~M_\odot\) lies just below the
central values inferred from the heaviest observed pulsars. PSR
J0740+6620 has a measured mass of \(2.08 \pm 0.07~M_\odot\)
\cite{Fonseca2021}, with a one-sigma lower bound of
\(2.01~M_\odot\). PSR J0348+0432 has a measured mass of
\(2.01 \pm 0.04~M_\odot\) \cite{Antoniadis_2013_J0348}. The QMD~SET~A
prediction is approximately \(0.04~M_\odot\) below the J0740+6620
one-sigma lower bound and essentially at the J0348+0432 one-sigma
lower bound of \(1.97~M_\odot\), leaving no comfortable margin.

The interpretation is therefore intermediate: the baseline QMD result
is not in direct conflict with the observational lower limit on the
maximum neutron-star mass, but it does not safely exceed it either.
This is the central motivation for the parameter sensitivity study in
Sec.~\ref{sec:qmd_parameter_sensitivity}: the diquark-sector
parameters are not fixed by vacuum observables, and modest variations
in them could push the predicted maximum mass either above or below
the observational constraint. Mapping this dependence is the
appropriate next step before drawing conclusions about whether the
QMD model with 2SC pairing can support the heaviest observed pulsars.

The baseline QMD~SET~A result, $M_{\max} = 1.970~M_\odot$ at $R = 12.16~\mathrm{km}$,
motivates the parameter sensitivity study in Sec.~\ref{sec:qmd_parameter_sensitivity}.

\section{Parameter sensitivity}
\label{sec:qmd_parameter_sensitivity}

\subsection*{Setup of the study}
\label{subsec:qmd_param_setup}

QMD~SET~A gives \(M_{\max}=1.970~M_\odot\), approximately
\(0.04~M_\odot\) below the J0740+6620 one-sigma lower bound and
essentially at the J0348+0432 lower bound. Whether the model can comfortably
support a two-solar-mass star therefore depends on how strongly the
stellar observables move under modest changes of the diquark sector.

The four free parameters of the diquark sector are the diquark mass
\(m_\Delta\), the Yukawa coupling \(g_\Delta\), the chiral--diquark
cross-coupling \(\lambda_3\), and the diquark self-coupling
\(\lambda_\Delta\). None of these is fixed by the on-shell matching
to vacuum observables described above, so they are genuinely free at
this stage. We take a one-at-a-time
approach: each parameter is varied individually while the others are
held at their QMD~SET~A values. For each parameter we run two
variations, one below and one above the baseline, giving eight new
configurations in addition to the baseline.

A full grid scan in four dimensions is not pursued here. Without an
external preference for any specific region of parameter space, such
a scan would produce a large and inconclusive result space. The
one-at-a-time approach isolates the marginal effect of each
parameter and makes the physical interpretation direct. The
correlations and degeneracies between parameters are not addressed in
the present analysis.

The eight new configurations are summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:qmd_param_variations}. The baseline values of the
diquark-sector parameters are
\begin{equation}
m_\Delta = 500~\mathrm{MeV},
\qquad
g_\Delta = 2g,
\qquad
\lambda_3 = \lambda_0,
\qquad
\lambda_\Delta = \lambda_0/4,
\end{equation}
where \(g = M_q/f_\pi \approx 3.23\) and
\(\lambda_0 \approx 118.1\) follow from the on-shell matching of the
chiral sector.

The physical motivation for each variation is straightforward. The
Yukawa coupling \(g_\Delta\) enters the asymptotic gap formula
\eqref{eq:qmd_asymptotic_gap} exponentially, so this parameter has
the largest nonlinear effect on the 2SC phase. The diquark mass
\(m_\Delta\) sets the tree-level threshold for 2SC formation at
\(\bar{\mu} = m_\Delta/2\), so variations in \(m_\Delta\) shift the
onset density. The self-coupling \(\lambda_\Delta\) controls the
curvature of the potential in the diquark direction and bounds the
gap at saturation. The cross-coupling \(\lambda_3\) links the chiral
and diquark sectors at tree level; the choice \(\lambda_3 = 0\) is
particularly informative, since it decouples the two condensate
sectors entirely.

For each of the eight variations we repeat the full stellar pipeline
defined above. This includes the common-\(\mu_q\) benchmark, the
neutral stellar equation of state with the coupled equilibrium
conditions for \(\phi_0\), \(\Delta_0\), \(\mu_e\), and \(\mu_8\), the
Maxwell construction, and the TOV integration. The full one-loop
potential is used throughout, with the same minimizer settings and EoS
interpolation as in the baseline calculation. The detailed results
included below focus on \(g_\Delta\), because this parameter produces
the largest changes in the mass--radius sequence and in the 2SC gap.

\subsection*{Variation of the Yukawa coupling \(g_\Delta\)}
\label{subsec:qmd_param_gdelta}

The Yukawa coupling \(g_\Delta\) controls the strength of the
quark--diquark interaction and is the dominant parameter for stellar
observables in the diquark sector. We consider two variations from
the baseline value \(g_\Delta = 2g\): a weaker coupling
\(g_\Delta = 1.5g\) (Run~A) and a stronger coupling \(g_\Delta = 2.5g\)
(Run~B). All other parameters are held at the QMD~SET~A values.

The mass--radius curves for the three values of \(g_\Delta\) are
compared in Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_MR_gdelta}. The maximum mass and the
radius at the maximum-mass configuration are
\begin{align}
\mathrm{Run~A}\;(1.5g): & \qquad M_{\max} = 1.793~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 11.04~\mathrm{km},\\
\mathrm{Baseline}\;(2g): & \qquad M_{\max} = 1.970~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 12.16~\mathrm{km},\\
\mathrm{Run~B}\;(2.5g): & \qquad M_{\max} = 2.047~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 12.72~\mathrm{km}.
\end{align}
The range \(\Delta M_{\max} = 0.254~M_\odot\) is the largest produced
by any of the parameter variations in this section. The radius at
fixed mass varies by a comparable margin: at \(M = 1.4~M_\odot\) the
radius spans \(13.00~\mathrm{km}\) (Run~A) to \(16.09~\mathrm{km}\)
(Run~B), with the baseline at \(14.80~\mathrm{km}\).

Only Run~B crosses the \(2~M_\odot\) line. Run~A falls
\(0.22~M_\odot\) below this benchmark, which is well outside the
observational uncertainty band for either PSR~J0740+6620 or
PSR~J0348+0432. The baseline is marginally below the threshold, as
found above. Among the three values explored, Run~B is the only
configuration that comfortably exceeds the maximum-mass constraint,
though it comes with the caveats discussed below.

Before discussing the physical mechanism behind these mass--radius
shifts, it is useful to address a feature of the model that may
seem at odds with intuition. The asymptotic gap, derived from the
gap equation at large \(\bar{\mu}\) in Sec.~\ref{subsec:qmd_benchmark}
and given by Eq.~\eqref{eq:qmd_asymptotic_gap}, has the simplified
form
\begin{equation}
g_\Delta \bar{\Delta}_0
=
M_q\,
\exp\!\left[
\frac{(4\pi)^2}{8\,g_\Delta^2} - \frac{1}{2}
\right]
\quad\text{(chiral limit).}
\label{eq:gd_asymptotic_simplified}
\end{equation}
This function is monotonically \emph{decreasing} in \(g_\Delta\):
the exponent dominates the prefactor, and the derivative
\(d(g_\Delta \bar{\Delta}_0)/dg_\Delta\) is strictly negative for all
positive \(g_\Delta\). Stronger Yukawa coupling therefore produces a
\emph{smaller} asymptotic gap, opposite to the naive expectation
that stronger pair binding should produce a larger condensate. This
behavior is a BCS-type signature: in the asymptotic limit the
condensate scale is set by the quark mass \(M_q\), and increasing
\(g_\Delta\) reduces the exponential enhancement of the gap relative
to that scale.

The numerical values confirm this trend. At \(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\)
the gap takes the values
\(g_\Delta \Delta_0 = 352~\mathrm{MeV}\) (Run~A),
\(252~\mathrm{MeV}\) (baseline), and
\(216~\mathrm{MeV}\) (Run~B), monotonically decreasing with
increasing \(g_\Delta\). These numerical values lie at
\(86\)--\(91\%\) of the analytic asymptote, reflecting that the
system has not yet fully saturated at the upper end of the
\(\mu_q\) grid.

This trend has an important physical consequence. The
Kurkela--Rajagopal--Steinhorst (KRS) analysis of
Ref.~\cite{Kurkela2024} constrains the CFL pairing gap rather than the
two-flavor 2SC gap considered here, so the comparison should be read as
an indicative high-density scale rather than a direct bound on this
model. Using the reference scale adopted here,
\(g_\Delta \bar{\Delta}_0 \lesssim 268~\mathrm{MeV}\) at asymptotic
density. Inverting Eq.~\eqref{eq:gd_asymptotic_simplified} shows
that this bound corresponds to \(g_\Delta \gtrsim 2.21\,g\) in our
parameterization. Run~A (\(g_\Delta = 1.5g\)) violates this bound,
while the baseline and Run~B satisfy it. The KRS bound therefore
favors larger values of \(g_\Delta\) within the range we explore.

If larger \(g_\Delta\) reduces the asymptotic gap, how does it
produce a larger maximum mass? The answer is that the asymptotic
gap is not the relevant quantity for stellar structure. At the
central densities reached in the maximum-mass configurations
(\(\varepsilon_c \sim 1.2~\mathrm{GeV/fm^3}\), corresponding to
\(\mu_q \sim 500~\mathrm{MeV}\)) the system is far from the
asymptotic regime. The direct effect of \(g_\Delta\) on the
finite-density 2SC binding energy dominates over its effect on the
asymptotic gap.

The mechanism is visible in the condensate profiles, shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_condensates_gdelta}. The chiral condensate
\(\phi_0\) is largely insensitive to \(g_\Delta\): all three runs
show the same first-order onset and similar rates of chiral
restoration. The diquark gap, however, differs substantially. At
low densities (\(\mu_q \lesssim 330~\mathrm{MeV}\)) the gap is
larger for stronger coupling, while at high densities the ordering
reverses, with weaker coupling producing the larger gap. The
crossover reflects the competition between the two effects: at
finite density, where the system is far from the BCS asymptote, the
direct \(g_\Delta\Delta_0\) coupling dominates and stronger
coupling gives a larger gap; at very high density, the asymptotic
formula takes over.

The effect on the equation of state is reflected most clearly in the
speed of sound, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_cs2_gdelta}. All three
runs produce a peak above the conformal limit \(c_s^2 = \frac{1}{3}\), but
the peak's location and height shift systematically with
\(g_\Delta\). In the smoothed curves, Run~A has the highest broad
peak, \(c_s^2 \approx 0.41\), the baseline reaches
\(c_s^2 \approx 0.40\), and Run~B peaks at
\(c_s^2 \approx 0.39\). The raw finite-difference tables contain
narrower local spikes, so the exact peak values should not be
overinterpreted. The robust trend is that stronger coupling shifts
the EoS stiffening to lower chemical potential and reduces its peak
amplitude, while broadening the stiffer region across a wider density
range. The combined effect is that Run~B has more pressure at the
stellar central densities than Run~A, even though Run~B's speed of
sound at any single \(\mu_q\) may be lower. It is this integrated
stiffness that drives the larger maximum mass.

Run~B is the most observationally favorable of the three values
considered, but the model shows signs of strain at this coupling
that should be flagged. Two features merit explicit comment.

First, the common-\(\mu_q\) benchmark for Run~B contains a region
near \(\mu_q = 0\) where the minimizer locates a 2SC-like state in
the vacuum, with \(\phi_0 \approx 86~\mathrm{MeV}\) and a nonzero
\(\Delta_0\). This state persists up to \(\mu_q \approx
69~\mathrm{MeV}\) before the normal phase is recovered, and the
physical 2SC onset reappears at \(\mu_q = 263.6~\mathrm{MeV}\).
This spurious vacuum 2SC region is unphysical: a true diquark
condensate at \(\mu_q = 0\) would violate baryon number
conservation. It indicates that the QMD potential at
\(g_\Delta = 2.5g\) is close to the boundary of the model's region
of validity. The stellar calculation is not affected by this
feature, since the neutrality conditions and the chemical-potential
range of the stellar scan exclude the spurious region. The
mass--radius result \(M_{\max} = 2.047~M_\odot\) is therefore
robust, but it should be quoted with the caveat that the underlying
potential is near a stability limit.

Second, the 2SC phase in Run~B becomes gapless at high density. The
condition for gapless 2SC, \(\delta\mu > g_\Delta \Delta_0\) with
\(\delta\mu = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu_e\), is satisfied for
\(\mu_q > 728~\mathrm{MeV}\), covering approximately one quarter of
the 2SC range. By \(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\) the mismatch exceeds
the gap by \(21~\mathrm{MeV}\). The gapless 2SC phase introduces
zero modes in the quasiparticle spectrum and the resulting
finite-difference speed of sound becomes numerically unreliable in
this region. The maximum-mass configuration of Run~B has central
chemical potential well below the onset of the gapless regime, so
the headline result is unaffected; configurations on the unstable
branch beyond \(M_{\max}\) are within the gapless region but are
not physically relevant. The baseline and Run~A do not enter the
gapless regime at the densities reached in the stellar
calculation.

These caveats do not invalidate Run~B, but they indicate that
\(g_\Delta = 2.5g\) sits near the upper edge of the model's
quantitative reliability. Pushing \(g_\Delta\) substantially higher
would likely require either an extension of the QMD model to
accommodate the vacuum stability or a more careful treatment of the
gapless 2SC phase.

Overall, the Yukawa coupling \(g_\Delta\) is by far the most influential
parameter in the diquark sector. Across the range explored, the
maximum mass varies by \(0.254~M_\odot\), the radius at maximum
mass by \(1.68~\mathrm{km}\), and the radius at fixed
\(M = 1.4~M_\odot\) by \(3.1~\mathrm{km}\). The shift in
\(M_{\max}\) is driven not by the asymptotic gap, which decreases
with \(g_\Delta\), but by the integrated stiffening of the EoS at
stellar densities, which increases with \(g_\Delta\). Both the
\(2~M_\odot\) observational constraint and the KRS bound on the
asymptotic gap favor values \(g_\Delta \gtrsim 2.2g\), within the
range we explore. Run~B at \(g_\Delta = 2.5g\) is the only
configuration that comfortably exceeds \(2~M_\odot\), but the
emergence of a spurious vacuum 2SC region and a sizeable gapless
regime at high density suggests that the QMD model is approaching
the boundary of its quantitative validity at this coupling.

\subsection*{Variation of the diquark mass \(m_\Delta\)}
\label{subsec:qmd_param_mdelta}

The diquark mass \(m_\Delta\) is the second most influential parameter
in the diquark sector. Unlike \(g_\Delta\), which alters the strength
of pair binding at every density above the 2SC threshold, \(m_\Delta\)
acts primarily by shifting the location of the onset itself. The
asymptotic gap formula \eqref{eq:qmd_asymptotic_gap} does not depend
on \(m_\Delta\), and the local features of the 2SC equation of state
once pairing has set in are nearly identical across the variations
considered here. The mechanism by which \(m_\Delta\) influences
stellar properties is therefore geometric rather than local: a lower
onset places a larger fraction of the stellar interior in the 2SC
phase, accumulating more of the pairing-driven pressure within the
star, while a higher onset confines the paired region to the innermost
densities and softens the integrated equation of state. We consider
the variations \(m_\Delta = 400~\mathrm{MeV}\) (Run~C) and
\(m_\Delta = 700~\mathrm{MeV}\) (Run~D), with all other parameters
held at the QMD~SET~A values.

The mass--radius sequences for the three values of \(m_\Delta\) are
shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_MR_mdelta} and produce the maximum-mass
configurations
\begin{align}
\mathrm{Run~C}\;(400~\mathrm{MeV}): & \qquad M_{\max} = 2.058~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 12.59~\mathrm{km},\\
\mathrm{Baseline}\;(500~\mathrm{MeV}): & \qquad M_{\max} = 1.970~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 12.16~\mathrm{km},\\
\mathrm{Run~D}\;(700~\mathrm{MeV}): & \qquad M_{\max} = 1.829~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 11.28~\mathrm{km}.
\end{align}
The range \(\Delta M_{\max} = 0.229~M_\odot\) is the second largest
among the parameter variations, exceeded only by \(g_\Delta\). The
three curves are systematically ordered: a lighter diquark produces a
larger maximum mass and a larger radius at every fixed mass on the
stable branch. At \(M = 1.4~M_\odot\), the radius spans from
\(13.32~\mathrm{km}\) (Run~D) to \(15.80~\mathrm{km}\) (Run~C), with
the baseline at \(14.80~\mathrm{km}\); the same ordering holds at
\(M = 1.8~M_\odot\) and at the maximum mass itself.

The location of the 2SC onset in neutral matter follows the same
ordering: \(\mu_q^{\,\mathrm{2SC}} = 271.4~\mathrm{MeV}\) for Run~C,
\(278.8~\mathrm{MeV}\) for the baseline, and \(293.7~\mathrm{MeV}\)
for Run~D. The size of this shift is, however, much smaller than the
tree-level estimate \(\bar{\mu} = m_\Delta/2\) would suggest. At tree
level, the three values of \(m_\Delta\) correspond to onsets of
\(200\), \(250\), and \(350~\mathrm{MeV}\) respectively, spanning a
range of \(150~\mathrm{MeV}\). The actual neutral-matter onsets span
only \(22.3~\mathrm{MeV}\): the one-loop contributions to the diquark
sector compress the sensitivity of the onset to \(m_\Delta\) by
nearly an order of magnitude. This compression is the main
quantitative surprise of the present variation. It reflects the fact
that the loop-level binding term that stabilizes the 2SC condensate
(the same term that was shown earlier to be essential for the
existence of the phase) depends only weakly on \(m_\Delta\) and acts
as a partial restoring force against the tree-level shift.

Despite the modest range of the onset, the integrated effect on
stellar structure is substantial. The condensate profiles are
qualitatively identical across the three runs: \(\phi_0\) drops
slightly at the onset and falls continuously through the 2SC phase,
and the gap grows toward a common asymptotic value of order
\(200\)--\(240~\mathrm{MeV}\) at the upper end of the density grid.
The three profiles are essentially translates of one another along
the \(\mu_q\) axis, set apart by the different onset locations. By
\(\mu_q \approx 500~\mathrm{MeV}\) the condensates have converged to
within a few MeV of each other, and the high-density EoS is
indistinguishable between runs at the working precision. The
asymptotic gap at \(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\) varies only by
approximately \(12~\mathrm{MeV}\) across the three runs, in line
with the expectation that \(m_\Delta\) does not enter the asymptotic
formula directly.

The integrated effect therefore acts at intermediate densities,
where one run is already in the 2SC phase while another is still
near the transition. At \(\mu_q = 300~\mathrm{MeV}\), Run~C has a
gap of \(138~\mathrm{MeV}\) and a substantially restored chiral
condensate, the baseline has a gap of \(103~\mathrm{MeV}\), and
Run~D has just entered the 2SC phase with a gap of
\(21~\mathrm{MeV}\). At a typical maximum-mass central density,
which corresponds to \(\mu_q \sim 500~\mathrm{MeV}\), the three runs
have nearly identical local equations of state, but Run~C has
sampled the 2SC region over a wider density range during the radial
integration. The resulting stiffening of the integrated equation of
state, accumulated over the volume of the star, is what produces the
ordering in \(M_{\max}\) and the systematic radius shifts at fixed
mass. The mechanism is therefore distinct from that of \(g_\Delta\):
where \(g_\Delta\) modifies the local EoS at every density,
\(m_\Delta\) modifies the range of densities over which the 2SC
contribution acts.

A brief remark is needed on the common-\(\mu_q\) benchmark for Run~D.
The very rapid 2SC condensation associated with a heavier diquark
mass — the gap grows from less than \(1~\mathrm{MeV}\) to more than
\(20~\mathrm{MeV}\) over a fraction of an MeV in \(\mu_q\) — produces
isolated numerical artifacts in the finite-difference speed of sound
near the onset, where the optimizer jumps briefly between local
minima of the potential. These appear as single-grid-point spikes
of unphysically large \(c_s^2\) followed by points where the
derivative is undefined. The stellar calculation is unaffected: the
Maxwell construction handles the first-order onset cleanly, and the
mass--radius result \(M_{\max} = 1.829~M_\odot\) is robust. The same
kind of artifact appears, more mildly, in Run~C. For both runs, the
high-density behavior of \(c_s^2\) is well behaved and qualitatively
similar to the baseline, with peaks slightly shifted in location.

Run~C is the second of the configurations considered in this section
that comfortably exceeds the \(2~M_\odot\) benchmark, after Run~B of
the previous variation. Unlike Run~B, the lighter-diquark
configuration shows no signs of model strain. The vacuum minimum
remains at \(\phi_0 = f_\pi\), \(\Delta_0 = 0\); the 2SC phase is
fully gapped over essentially the entire stellar density range, with
only the upper few percent of the EoS table marginally entering the
gapless regime; and the speed of sound retains the characteristic
peak above the conformal limit followed by a smooth relaxation toward
\(c_s^2 = \frac{1}{3}\). Run~D, by contrast, is clean and well-behaved
numerically but observationally disfavored: at
\(M_{\max} = 1.829~M_\odot\) it falls roughly \(0.18~M_\odot\) below
the central values of the heaviest measured pulsars, and outside the
observational uncertainty band of either of them.

Two directions in the diquark parameter space therefore lead to
compliance with the \(2~M_\odot\) constraint: a stronger Yukawa
coupling, as in Run~B, or a lighter diquark mass, as in Run~C. The
model has flexibility in both. A natural follow-up would be to
explore whether the two directions reinforce each other, but
combinations of this kind lie outside the one-at-a-time approach
adopted here and would require a joint scan that we do not undertake.

\subsection*{Variation of the diquark self-coupling \(\lambda_\Delta\)}
\label{subsec:qmd_param_lamdelta}

The diquark self-coupling \(\lambda_\Delta\) enters the potential
through the quartic term \((\lambda_\Delta/6)\Delta_0^4\). It bounds
the gap at saturation and sets the stiffness of the diquark
direction of the potential against large-amplitude fluctuations. The
remaining terms in the diquark sector are quadratic in \(\Delta_0\),
either through the tree-level mass term or through the loop-induced
binding term proportional to \(\bar{\mu}^2 \Delta_0^2\). The quartic
\(\lambda_\Delta\) therefore has no leverage at the 2SC onset, where
\(\Delta_0\) is small, and only becomes important once the
condensate has grown to a magnitude where the quartic competes with
the quadratic terms. At stellar densities, where the gap is still
approaching its asymptotic value, the quartic remains subleading.
The expectation is therefore that \(\lambda_\Delta\) should be a
relatively inert parameter for stellar observables. We consider the
variations \(\lambda_\Delta = \lambda_0/8\) (Run~E) and
\(\lambda_\Delta = \lambda_0/2\) (Run~F), bracketing the baseline by
a factor of two on each side and spanning a factor of four overall.

The numerical results bear out the expectation in stark form. The
neutral 2SC onset is at \(\mu_q = 278.8~\mathrm{MeV}\) for both
Run~E and the baseline, and shifts only to
\(\mu_q = 277.9~\mathrm{MeV}\) for Run~F: a total spread of less
than one MeV across a factor-of-four change in the parameter. The
asymptotic gap at \(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\) takes the values
\(g_\Delta \Delta_0 = 252.6\), \(252.4\), and \(251.8~\mathrm{MeV}\)
respectively, a range of less than one MeV against the
\(135~\mathrm{MeV}\) range produced by the \(g_\Delta\) variation
in the previous subsections. The maximum mass is similarly
insensitive: Run~E gives \(M_{\max} = 1.9707~M_\odot\), the baseline
gives \(1.9703~M_\odot\), and Run~F gives \(1.9701~M_\odot\). The
total spread is \(0.0006~M_\odot\), smaller than the \(g_\Delta\)
range by a factor of approximately three hundred and smaller than
the \(m_\Delta\) range by a similar margin. The corresponding radii
at the maximum-mass configuration vary by only
\(0.08~\mathrm{km}\).

Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_MR_lamdelta} shows the three mass--radius
sequences overlaid. The three curves are indistinguishable at the
working resolution; a reader without the legend would see a single
trajectory. This visual coincidence is the central content of the
section.

The result has a clear interpretation. The 2SC onset is set by the
balance between the tree-level mass term \((m_\Delta^2 - 4\bar{\mu}^2)\)
and the loop-level binding contribution at small \(\Delta_0\); the
quartic enters only at higher order in \(\Delta_0\) and is therefore
absent from the onset condition to leading order. Once the
condensate has grown into the 2SC phase, \(\lambda_\Delta\) reappears
in the equilibrium condition through the term
\((2\lambda_\Delta/3)\Delta_0^3\), but this contribution is subleading
relative to the loop binding term throughout the stellar density range.
Only deep in the saturation regime, where \(\Delta_0\) becomes
comparable to \(\bar{\mu}\), does the quartic term begin to dominate. At the densities reached in the maximum-mass
configurations, \(\mu_q \sim 500~\mathrm{MeV}\), the system is still
far from saturation, and the quartic remains a subleading correction.

The speed of sound shows a modest spread across the three runs
that does not follow a clean monotonic pattern: Run~E and Run~F give
raw benchmark peaks of \(c_s^2 = 0.40\), compared with \(0.41\) for
the baseline. This small scatter carries no systematic dependence on
\(\lambda_\Delta\); the overall shape of \(c_s^2(\mu_q)\) is essentially
identical across the three runs, and the asymptotic relaxation toward
the conformal limit is unaffected. The peak height difference does not
propagate into any detectable shift in the mass--radius sequence.

The practical consequence is that the effective dimensionality of
the diquark parameter space is reduced. Within the range of
\(\lambda_\Delta\) considered here, the parameter can be treated as
fixed at its baseline value without loss of accuracy for stellar
observables; the diquark sector reduces, for the purposes of
neutron-star modeling, from four free parameters to three. The
remaining three parameters, the chiral--diquark cross-coupling
\(\lambda_3\) considered next, the diquark mass \(m_\Delta\), and
the Yukawa coupling \(g_\Delta\), each have qualitatively different
phenomenological roles and account between them for essentially the
entire variation in the predicted maximum mass.

\subsection*{Variation of the chiral--diquark cross-coupling \(\lambda_3\)}
\label{subsec:qmd_param_lam3}

The cross-coupling \(\lambda_3\) enters the potential through the
term \((\lambda_3/12)\,\phi_0^2 \Delta_0^2\). It is the only term in
the tree-level potential that couples the chiral and diquark
condensates directly, and its role is therefore qualitatively
different from the self-couplings discussed above. For positive
\(\lambda_3\), the term raises the energy of any state in which both
condensates are simultaneously nonzero: the growth of \(\Delta_0\)
contributes to the effective mass of \(\phi_0\), and conversely. The
two sectors compete. At \(\lambda_3 = 0\) the tree-level potential
factorizes into independent chiral and diquark pieces, and the two
sectors are coupled only through the one-loop integral
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\). We consider the variations
\(\lambda_3 = 0\) (Run~G) and \(\lambda_3 = 2\lambda_0\) (Run~H),
bracketing the baseline \(\lambda_3 = \lambda_0 \approx 118.1\) by
factors of two.

The mass--radius sequences for the three values of \(\lambda_3\) are
shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_MR_lam3} and produce maximum-mass
configurations
\begin{align}
\mathrm{Run~G}\;(\lambda_3 = 0): & \qquad M_{\max} = 2.004~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 13.03~\mathrm{km},\\
\mathrm{Baseline}\;(\lambda_0): & \qquad M_{\max} = 1.970~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 12.16~\mathrm{km},\\
\mathrm{Run~H}\;(2\lambda_0): & \qquad M_{\max} = 1.950~M_\odot,
\quad R(M_{\max}) = 11.46~\mathrm{km}.
\end{align}
The range \(\Delta M_{\max} = 0.054~M_\odot\) is intermediate
between the negligible effect of \(\lambda_\Delta\) and the
substantial effects of \(g_\Delta\) and \(m_\Delta\). The ordering
is monotonic: weaker cross-coupling produces the larger maximum mass
and the larger radius at every fixed mass.

The spread is more visible at intermediate masses than at the
maximum. At \(M = 1.4~M_\odot\) the three sequences give radii of
approximately \(18.2\), \(14.8\), and \(13.1~\mathrm{km}\)
respectively, a range of more than five kilometers. The three
curves separate substantially at low masses and converge as they
approach the maximum-mass configuration. For an observational
comparison to neutron-star radius measurements, this intermediate-mass
spread is the more useful signal than the comparatively modest shift
in \(M_{\max}\).

The 2SC onset in neutral stellar matter follows the same monotonic
ordering: \(\mu_q^{\,\mathrm{2SC}} = 270.5\), \(278.8\), and
\(285.3~\mathrm{MeV}\) for Runs~G, baseline, and H respectively,
spanning a range of \(14.8~\mathrm{MeV}\) that is comparable to the
range produced by \(m_\Delta\). This is consistent with the
expected mechanism: stronger cross-coupling raises the energy cost
of forming \(\Delta_0\) in the presence of a still-nonzero
\(\phi_0\), and the system requires a higher chemical potential to
overcome this cost. The condensate jump at the first-order onset
itself is small in all three cases: \(\phi_0\) drops from its
vacuum value by less than \(0.2~\mathrm{MeV}\) across the transition
in every run, and in Run~G the chiral condensate remains exactly at
\(\phi_0 = f_\pi\) through the onset, since the tree-level
decoupling removes any direct feedback from the appearing
\(\Delta_0\). The dominant \(\lambda_3\) signature lies not in the
onset jump but in the subsequent rate at which \(\phi_0\) is
suppressed inside the 2SC phase. At \(\mu_q \approx 317~\mathrm{MeV}\),
\(\phi_0\) takes the values \(20.0\), \(17.5\), and
\(15.6~\mathrm{MeV}\) for the three runs: stronger cross-coupling
drives the chiral condensate down faster.

The diquark gap itself, in contrast, is only weakly sensitive to
\(\lambda_3\). At \(\mu_q = 400~\mathrm{MeV}\) all three runs give
\(g_\Delta \Delta_0 \approx 188~\mathrm{MeV}\) within a spread of
less than two MeV. The small differences in the gap profile at
lower densities are largely a consequence of the runs reaching their
onsets at different \(\mu_q\); at fixed displacement above the onset,
the gap is nearly identical. By \(\mu_q = 900~\mathrm{MeV}\) the
asymptotic gap is \(g_\Delta \Delta_0 = 252.4 \pm 0.05~\mathrm{MeV}\)
across the three runs. This near-perfect coincidence at high density
is the cleanest confirmation of the underlying mechanism: once
\(\phi_0\) has decayed, the \(\phi_0^2 \Delta_0^2\) term vanishes
regardless of \(\lambda_3\), and the asymptotic gap is set by the
BCS structure in \(g_\Delta\) and \(M_q\) alone.

Run~G is the most conceptually informative of the three. With
\(\lambda_3 = 0\) the tree-level potential factorizes into
independent chiral and diquark sectors. The 2SC condensate
appears at \(\mu_q = 270.5~\mathrm{MeV}\) without any tree-level
feedback on \(\phi_0\): the chiral condensate stays at its vacuum
value through the transition and begins to fall only afterwards,
driven entirely by the one-loop medium contribution
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\). Chiral restoration and 2SC pairing
therefore occur as separate phenomena in this run, in contrast to
the baseline calculation where the two are partially locked by the
tree-level coupling. Despite the qualitatively different evolution
of \(\phi_0\), the first-order character of the 2SC onset survives:
\(\Delta_0\) still appears discontinuously, and there is no
indication that decoupling the sectors converts the transition to
second order at this level of approximation.

Run~G also crosses the \(2~M_\odot\) benchmark, reaching
\(M_{\max} = 2.004~M_\odot\) and becoming the third configuration
in the scan to satisfy the observational constraint, after Run~C
(\(m_\Delta = 400~\mathrm{MeV}\)) and Run~B (\(g_\Delta = 2.5g\)).
The margin above the threshold is only \(0.004~M_\odot\), the
narrowest of the three crossing configurations, indicating that
\(\lambda_3\) is a second-tier parameter: its variation alone moves
the model into compliance but only marginally. The conditions under
which the QMD model comfortably exceeds the observational maximum
mass remain those identified in the preceding subsections: stronger
\(g_\Delta\) or lighter \(m_\Delta\). The \(\lambda_3 \to 0\)
direction would reinforce compliance when combined with shifts in
the dominant parameters, but provides negligible margin by itself.

With this variation the four-parameter scan is complete. The
relative importance of the four diquark-sector parameters, their
distinct phenomenological roles, and the implications for the
\(2~M_\odot\) constraint are summarized in the closing subsection
below.

In decreasing order of influence on $M_{\max}$: $g_\Delta$ produces the largest spread
($\Delta M_{\max} = 0.254~M_\odot$) through EoS stiffening at stellar densities and is
the only parameter that substantially affects the asymptotic gap; $m_\Delta$ produces a
comparable spread ($\Delta M_{\max} = 0.229~M_\odot$) through the onset threshold

from four to three for compact-star modeling.

The observational benchmark of greatest interest is the maximum
mass of two solar masses, set by the heaviest measured pulsars. Of
the eight new configurations, three cross this threshold. Run~C
(\(m_\Delta = 400~\mathrm{MeV}\)) gives \(M_{\max} = 2.058~M_\odot\)
without any sign of model strain: the vacuum minimum sits at the
physical chiral-broken state, the 2SC phase is fully gapped over
essentially the entire stellar density range, and the speed of
sound retains the qualitative features of the baseline. Run~B
(\(g_\Delta = 2.5g\)) gives \(M_{\max} = 2.047~M_\odot\) but
carries two caveats. First, the common-\(\mu_q\) benchmark
exhibits a spurious 2SC condensate near \(\mu_q = 0\), indicating
that the QMD potential at this coupling is close to the boundary of
its region of validity. Second, the asymptotic gap is
\(g_\Delta \Delta_0 = 216~\mathrm{MeV}\), below the
Kurkela--Rajagopal--Steinhorst bound of \(268~\mathrm{MeV}\)
inferred from CFL-phase considerations, although the
applicability of that bound to the present 2SC calculation is only
indicative. Neither feature invalidates the stellar result, but
they signal that Run~B is operating near the edge of the model's
quantitative reliability. The lighter-diquark route of Run~C is by
contrast unambiguously clean.

These three configurations identify three distinct directions in the
diquark parameter space along which the QMD model can satisfy the
\(2~M_\odot\) constraint: a stronger Yukawa coupling (Run~B), a
lighter diquark mass (Run~C), or removal of the chiral--diquark
cross-coupling (Run~G). The first two require parameter changes of
roughly twenty to twenty-five percent from the baseline, which is a
modest variation for a phenomenological model with parameters that
are not fixed by vacuum observables. Run~G achieves compliance with
\(\lambda_3 \to 0\), but by only \(0.004~M_\odot\)~---~a
negligible margin compared with the \(0.058~M_\odot\) and
\(0.047~M_\odot\) provided by Runs~C and~B respectively. A joint
scan in two or more parameters is not pursued here, but the
one-dimensional results suggest that an extended region of
parameter space exists in which the model comfortably reproduces
the observed maximum mass.

Several qualitative features of the model are reproduced across
all nine configurations and represent robust predictions of the
QMD framework rather than parameter-specific accidents. The 2SC
onset is first-order in every run: the diquark condensate appears
discontinuously, and the Maxwell construction applied to the raw
equation of state always removes a small unstable segment in the
onset region. The speed of sound exceeds the conformal limit in
every run, with peaks ranging from \(0.39\) to \(0.44\) in the
clean cases, and approaches \(c_s^2 = \frac{1}{3}\) from above at the
upper end of the density grid. The 2SC phase remains conventional
BCS (fully gapped) over the relevant stellar density range in all
runs, with only marginal entries into the gapless regime at the
highest \(\mu_q\). All sequences terminate at vanishing energy
density at the surface, producing gravitationally bound rather
than self-bound configurations. These features are structural
consequences of the model and not artifacts of the particular
baseline chosen in Sec.~\ref{sec:qmd_section1}.

Set against observational constraints, the picture is mixed. On
mass, the QMD model is viable: with parameter choices within a
narrow but plausible window, it produces stellar configurations
that exceed the heaviest measured pulsar masses. On radius, the
picture is less favorable. The baseline predicts
\(R(1.4\,M_\odot) \approx 14.8~\mathrm{km}\), and the
configurations that satisfy the mass constraint predict still
larger radii: \(R(1.4\,M_\odot) \approx 16.1~\mathrm{km}\) for
Run~B, \(15.8~\mathrm{km}\) for Run~C, and \(18.2~\mathrm{km}\)
for Run~G. Recent NICER measurements
favor smaller radii in the range \(11\)--\(13~\mathrm{km}\) for a
\(1.4~M_\odot\) star. This tension is a generic feature of pure
quark-matter models without a hadronic crust: the QMD equation of
state extends from the centre of the star to the surface without
the soft low-density nuclear EoS that would otherwise produce a
more compact configuration. Hybrid models, in which a QMD quark
core is matched to a nuclear-matter envelope, would naturally
reduce the predicted radii while retaining the stiffening from
the 2SC phase at high density. Constructing and analyzing such
hybrid configurations is a natural extension of the present work
but lies outside its scope.

The limitations of the analysis are stated directly. The
one-at-a-time scan captures the marginal effect of each parameter
but cannot resolve correlations or identify optimal joint
configurations. No formal optimization or Bayesian inference has
been performed. Tidal deformability constraints from binary
neutron-star mergers, such as those derived from GW170817, would
require a separate calculation that we have not undertaken.
Finite-temperature effects, rotation, and magnetic fields are
absent, and the matter content is restricted to pure quark phases
throughout. Each of these is a meaningful extension of the
present framework, and each can be tackled with the same
underlying pipeline.

Across the nine QMD configurations, the central baryon density at
maximum mass spans \(n_{B,c}/n_0 \in [6.30, 8.06]\), with all but
one run falling in the range \(6.3\)--\(7.5\,n_0\). The outlier is
Run~A (\(g_\Delta = 1.5g\)), which produces the softest equation of
state and the lowest maximum mass (\(1.793~M_\odot\)); a softer EoS
requires higher central compression to support the star against
gravity. Conversely, Runs~B and~C, which yield the stiffest
equations of state and the highest maximum masses, have the lowest
central densities (\(n_{B,c}/n_0 \approx 6.30\) and
\(6.48\) respectively). This inverse correlation between maximum
mass and central density at maximum mass is a generic consequence
of EoS stiffness and holds consistently across all nine
runs.\footnote{The anticorrelation between \(M_{\max}\) and
\(n_{B,c}(M_{\max})\) reflects the fact that a stiffer EoS
reaches the mass turning-point at a lower central pressure.
It does not imply that the central density of any individual star
decreases as more matter is added; along the stable branch, central
density increases monotonically with mass.}

The central chemical potentials lie in the range
\(\mu_{q,c} \in [477, 509]~\mathrm{MeV}\) across the nine QMD
runs, consistent with the rough estimate \(\mu_q \sim
500~\mathrm{MeV}\) given in the preceding section. At these
chemical potentials the quark matter is substantially denser than
nuclear saturation, and the 2SC condensate \(\Delta_0\) is fully
established in every run.

The three QM reference configurations yield central densities
\(n_{B,c}/n_0 \approx 6.7\)--\(6.9\), closely bracketing the QMD
baseline value of \(6.64\,n_0\), indicating that the 2SC pairing
does not substantially shift the central density at maximum mass
relative to the unpaired model at comparable stellar mass.

The thermodynamic identity \(\varepsilon = -P + \mu_q n_q\) was
verified at the central point for all twelve configurations,
yielding relative residuals below \(0.005\%\) in every case. This
constitutes an independent cross-check that the EoS tables and
stellar-integration pipeline are thermodynamically consistent
throughout the density range probed by the maximum-mass star.

The QMD model with the full one-loop potential, neutrality
imposed, and the Maxwell construction applied produces a
thermodynamically consistent and numerically robust description of
two-flavor quark matter with 2SC pairing. The baseline parameter
choice gives a maximum mass that lies just below the heaviest
observed pulsars, with quantitatively well-controlled mechanisms
governing the deviations under parameter variation. The model is
constrained but not excluded by current observations: it can
support a two-solar-mass star within a plausible region of the
diquark parameter space, while predicting radii that are
characteristic of pure quark configurations and would be reduced
in a hybrid construction. The qualitative predictions of the
framework, a first-order 2SC onset, a peak in the speed of sound
above the conformal limit, asymptotic gap saturation, and
gravitationally-bound stellar configurations, are robust across
the parameter scan and represent genuine consequences of the QMD
description of dense matter.
