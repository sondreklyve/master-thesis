\chapter{Quark Matter and Compact Stars}

The primary goal of this chapter is to construct an equation of state
for cold and dense quark matter and to investigate its implications
for compact stars. In contrast to the hadronic models considered in
Part~I, the matter described here consists entirely
of deconfined quarks. The resulting stellar configurations therefore
represent idealized quark stars. While such objects provide a useful
theoretical framework for isolating the effects of quark degrees of
freedom, they are not expected to give a complete description of
realistic compact stars. A more realistic scenario involves a
transition between hadronic and quark matter, leading to so-called
hybrid stars. This possibility will be considered in a later chapter,
where the present equation of state is combined with a hadronic
description.

Within the QM model, the thermodynamic properties of the system are
determined from the grand canonical potential evaluated in the
mean-field approximation. In this framework, the scalar meson field
develops a non-vanishing expectation value, which acts as an order
parameter for chiral symmetry breaking. The constituent quark mass is
then generated dynamically through the relation
\begin{equation}
M_q = g \bar{\sigma} ,
\end{equation}
and becomes a function of the thermodynamic state of the system. This
feature distinguishes the model from free fermion descriptions and is
crucial for obtaining a realistic equation of state.

A first objective of this chapter is to evaluate the grand potential
numerically and determine the equilibrium value of the scalar
condensate by minimizing the thermodynamic potential at each value of
the quark chemical potential. This yields a function
$\bar{\sigma}(\mu)$, and therefore a density-dependent constituent
quark mass $M_q = g\bar{\sigma}(\mu)$.

At low chemical potential, the condensate remains close to its vacuum
value, corresponding to a phase with spontaneously broken chiral
symmetry and large constituent quark masses. As the chemical
potential increases, the minimum of the thermodynamic potential shifts
toward smaller values of the condensate, leading to a reduction of the
quark mass. This behavior reflects the gradual restoration of chiral
symmetry and, depending on the model parameters, may occur through a
proper (first-order) phase transition or a smooth crossover
\cite{Folkestad2018,Buballa2005,Schmitt2010}.

Once the thermodynamic quantities have been determined, we impose the
physical conditions relevant for compact stars, namely charge
neutrality and weak equilibrium. These constraints fix the relative
abundances of the different particle species and ensure that the
resulting equation of state is suitable for astrophysical
applications.

In addition to the basic QM model, we also consider extensions that
include additional interaction channels relevant at high density. In
particular, we investigate the possibility of diquark condensation,
which leads to color-superconducting phases of quark matter. These
phases arise from attractive interactions in the quark--quark channel
and are expected to play an important role in the phase structure of
dense QCD \cite{Alford2008,Schmitt2010}. We restrict the analysis to
the two-flavor superconducting (2SC) phase, which represents the
simplest realization of color superconductivity. The inclusion of such
effects modifies the thermodynamic potential and can have a
significant impact on the resulting equation of state.

Finally, the equation of state obtained from the QM model is used as
input to the TOV equations, which determine the structure of
non-rotating compact stars in general relativity. By solving these
equations, we obtain mass--radius relations for quark stars and
analyze how the microscopic properties of the model influence
macroscopic observables.

\section{A Simple Quark--Meson Equation of State}

\subsection*{Thermodynamic framework}

Having established the two-flavor Quark--Meson model in vacuum, the
next step is to use it as a model for cold and dense matter. The
central quantity in this construction is the grand potential density
$\Omega$, since all thermodynamic observables may be obtained from it.
In the present work we restrict ourselves to zero temperature,
\begin{equation}
T=0,
\end{equation}
which is the regime relevant for compact stars to a very good
approximation.

The thermodynamic treatment is carried out in the mean-field
approximation. In practice, this means that the mesonic fields are
replaced by their expectation values, while the quark fields are kept
as dynamical degrees of freedom and integrated out. This is the same
large-$N_c$ logic used in the derivation of the effective potential in
the previous chapter: quark-loop contributions are retained, whereas
mesonic fluctuations are neglected at this stage. 

With this approximation, the thermodynamics is governed by the grand
potential
\begin{equation}
\Omega = \Omega(\bar{\sigma}; \mu_u,\mu_d,\ldots),
\end{equation}
viewed as a function of the mean scalar field and of the relevant
chemical potentials. In the simplest case considered first below, the
medium is parameterized by a common quark chemical potential. Later,
when the model is adapted to quark-star matter, additional chemical
potentials are introduced in order to impose weak equilibrium and
charge neutrality. The basic thermodynamic structure is, however, the
same in both cases: one first constructs $\Omega$ as a function of the
mean fields and chemical potentials, and then determines the physical
state by minimizing it.

Since we consider isospin-symmetric matter with
\(\mu_u = \mu_d\), the isospin chemical potential vanishes and
pion condensation does not occur. The thermodynamic potential
therefore depends only on the scalar condensate, and the
stationarity condition reduces to
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \bar{\sigma}} = 0.
\label{eq:qs_gap_eq}
\end{equation}
This equation is often referred to as the \emph{gap equation}. Its
solution gives the medium-dependent condensate
$\bar{\sigma}(\mu_u,\mu_d,\ldots)$ and thereby the constituent quark
mass,
\begin{equation}
M_q = g\bar{\sigma}.
\label{eq:qs_constituent_mass}
\end{equation}
The importance of this relation is that the quark mass is not fixed,
but generated dynamically through the chiral condensate. The equation
of state is therefore determined self-consistently rather than by
assuming a prescribed fermion mass. This dynamical mass generation is a
central feature of the Quark--Meson model and is precisely what makes
it qualitatively different from models in which quark mass is treated as
a fixed parameter \cite{Buballa2005,Schmitt2010,sletmoen2022}.

The vacuum expectation value \(\sigma_0=f_\pi\) is fixed by the pion
decay constant, whereas the sigma mass is less sharply constrained and
is therefore commonly treated as a model parameter 
\cite{SchaeferWambach2008,SchaeferPawlowskiWambach2007,Buballa2005}.
In the simple case considered here, the model is therefore
evaluated for the representative choices
\(m_\sigma=500,600,\) and \(700\,\mathrm{MeV}\). This
variation changes the curvature of the effective potential and thereby
the detailed thermodynamic response, while leaving the underlying
two-flavor mean-field setup unchanged \cite{Folkestad2018,Andersen2024}.

Once the stationary point of $\Omega$ has been found, the pressure is
identified as minus the grand potential evaluated at the minimum,
\begin{equation}
P = -\Omega_{\min}.
\label{eq:qs_pressure_def}
\end{equation}
The particle number densities follow from derivatives with respect to
the corresponding chemical potentials,
\begin{equation}
n_f = -\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_f},
\label{eq:qs_density_def}
\end{equation}
and the energy density is obtained from the thermodynamic relation
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon = -P + \sum_f \mu_f n_f.
\label{eq:qs_energy_def}
\end{equation}
In the constrained stellar calculation, the sum is extended to include
the electron contribution as well.

It is useful to keep in mind that this thermodynamic framework serves
two distinct purposes in the chapter. First, it allows us to study the
Quark--Meson model in a simplified setting, where the role of the
chiral condensate and the onset of dense matter can be seen clearly.
Second, the same framework can be extended by imposing the additional
conditions required for stellar matter, namely local charge neutrality
and $\beta$-equilibrium. The unconstrained and constrained equations of
state are therefore not based on different formalisms, but on the same
grand-potential construction with different choices of thermodynamic
constraints.

\subsection*{Medium contribution at zero temperature}

At zero temperature, the thermodynamic potential separates into a
vacuum contribution and a medium-dependent part associated with the
filled Fermi sea. The latter can be expressed in terms of the Fermi
momentum
\begin{equation}
k_{F,f} = \sqrt{\mu_f^2 - M_q^2},
\end{equation}
where $M_q = g\bar{\sigma}$ is the constituent quark mass.

The medium contribution is nonvanishing only when the chemical
potential exceeds the constituent quark mass, $\mu_f > M_q$. In this
case, a Fermi surface is formed and quark states are occupied up to
the Fermi momentum. For $\mu_f < M_q$, the Fermi momentum becomes
imaginary, indicating that no states are occupied and the medium
contribution vanishes. The system therefore remains in the vacuum
state, with zero pressure and vanishing number density. This behavior
is characteristic of zero-temperature fermionic systems and is also
found in the Quark--Meson model in the mean-field approximation
\cite{andresen2023,sletmoen2022}.

In this limit, the Fermi--Dirac distribution reduces to a unit
step function,
\begin{equation}
n_F(E) = \theta(\mu - E),
\end{equation}
so that momentum integrals over fermionic states are restricted to
the region below the Fermi surface. As a result, the medium
contribution can be evaluated by integrating single-particle
quantities up to the Fermi momentum.

The resulting expressions for the pressure, number density, and energy
density are obtained from standard phase-space integrals over the
occupied states. In particular, the number density is proportional to
$k_{F,f}^3$, while the pressure and energy density involve integrals
over the single-particle energy. For each flavor one has
\begin{equation}
n_f = \frac{N_c}{3\pi^2} k_{F,f}^3.
\end{equation}
These quantities depend explicitly on
the chemical potentials and on the dynamically generated mass, and
therefore inherit the medium dependence of the chiral condensate. The
equation of state is thus determined self-consistently once the
condensate has been computed from the gap equation. The behavior of
the condensate together with the onset of finite density is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_sigma_density_mu}.

As the chemical potential increases, the solution to the gap equation
changes, leading to a reduction of the condensate and therefore of the
constituent quark mass. This reflects the gradual restoration of
chiral symmetry in dense matter. At a critical chemical potential, the
preferred solution of the gap equation changes abruptly, resulting in
a discontinuous change in the condensate. Within the present
mean-field treatment, this corresponds to a first-order phase
transition between the vacuum and a dense quark-matter phase 
\cite{andresen2023,sletmoen2022}.

Changing \(m_\sigma\) modifies the stiffness of the effective
potential and thereby the detailed evolution of the condensate through
the transition region. This shifts the onset of finite density and
produces a corresponding spread in the density curves, but the overall
structure remains the same for the three representative parameter
choices.

Beyond the transition, the Fermi momentum grows with increasing
chemical potential, and the pressure and energy density increase
monotonically. The behavior of these quantities as functions of the
chemical potential forms the basis for the numerical construction of
the equation of state presented in the following subsection.

\subsection*{Vacuum normalization}

The thermodynamic potential obtained from the Quark--Meson model
contains a vacuum contribution which, after renormalization, is
generally nonzero. As a consequence, the pressure defined by Eq.~(\ref{eq:qs_pressure_def})
does not vanish in the vacuum state, i.e.\ at vanishing chemical
potentials. This is in contrast to the physical expectation that the
vacuum should correspond to zero pressure.

For applications to compact stars, it is therefore necessary to
normalize the pressure such that
\begin{equation}
P(\mu_f = 0) = 0.
\end{equation}
This is achieved by introducing a constant shift in the thermodynamic
potential,
\begin{equation}
\Omega \rightarrow \Omega + B,
\end{equation}
which corresponds to the transformation
\begin{equation}
P \rightarrow P - B, \qquad
\varepsilon \rightarrow \varepsilon + B.
\end{equation}
The constant $B$ is commonly referred to as a \emph{bag constant}, by
analogy with the MIT bag model \cite{Chodos1974,Chodos1974b}.

In the present framework, this shift has a clear interpretation. The
renormalized vacuum effective potential determines the absolute value
of the energy density of the vacuum, but this value is not fixed by
the theory itself. Only differences in the thermodynamic potential are
physically meaningful. The constant $B$ therefore fixes the reference
point of the energy density and ensures that the physical vacuum
corresponds to zero pressure.

In practice, it is convenient to separate this normalization into two
steps. First, a vacuum subtraction is performed such that the pressure
vanishes in the true vacuum configuration,
\begin{equation}
B_0 = \Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}(\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{vac}}),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{vac}}$ denotes the solution of the gap
equation at zero chemical potential. This guarantees that the vacuum
state is correctly normalized.

In addition, one may introduce a further constant shift $B$, which can
be used to modify the stiffness of the equation of state. While $B_0$
is fixed by the requirement of vacuum normalization, the additional
shift $B$ acts as a phenomenological parameter that effectively
controls the onset of the high-density phase. This is analogous to the
role of the bag constant in phenomenological quark-matter models
\cite{sletmoen2022}.

It is important to emphasize that constant shifts of the pressure do
not affect thermodynamic derivatives such as the number density or the
speed of sound. However, they do change the relation between pressure
and energy density,
\begin{equation}
P = P(\varepsilon),
\end{equation}
which is the quantity entering the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff
equations. The choice of $B$ therefore has a direct impact on the
resulting mass--radius relations of compact stars.

From a numerical perspective, the effect of the vacuum normalization
can be seen as a vertical shift of the pressure curve. In particular,
the condition $P=0$ defines the surface of the star, and the
normalization ensures that this point corresponds to the physical
vacuum rather than an unphysical offset in the thermodynamic
potential.

\subsection*{Construction of the equation of state}

The full equation of state is obtained by solving the gap equation for
$\bar{\sigma}$ at each value of the chemical potential and evaluating
the thermodynamic quantities at the corresponding minimum of the grand
potential. In practice, this procedure defines a mapping from the
chemical potentials to the thermodynamic observables,
\begin{equation}
\mu_f \;\longrightarrow\; \{\bar{\sigma},\, M_q,\, n_f,\, P,\, \varepsilon\}.
\end{equation}

For each value of the chemical potential, the gap equation
\eqref{eq:qs_gap_eq} is solved to determine the condensate
$\bar{\sigma}$. This fixes the constituent quark mass through
$M_q = g\bar{\sigma}$, which in turn determines the Fermi momenta and
the medium contribution to the thermodynamic potential. The pressure,
number densities, and energy density are then evaluated using
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:qs_pressure_def}--\eqref{eq:qs_energy_def}, including
the vacuum normalization discussed above. Numerically, the global
minimum of the grand potential is selected at each value of the
chemical potential, so that the thermodynamically preferred phase is
obtained.

This relation is obtained numerically by tabulating $P$ and
$\varepsilon$ as functions of the chemical potential and performing a
parametric representation of the curve. The resulting function
$\varepsilon(P)$ serves as input for the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff
equations governing the structure of compact stars \cite{sletmoen2022}.
In the numerical construction, only the branch with non-negative
pressure is retained in the plotted equation of state.
The corresponding pressure as a function of chemical potential, together
with the resulting equation of state, is displayed in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_pressure_panels}.

It is important to note that the structure of the equation of state is
directly influenced by the behavior of the condensate. In particular,
a discontinuous change in $\bar{\sigma}$ as a function of the chemical
potential leads to a corresponding discontinuity in the thermodynamic
quantities, signaling a first-order phase transition. In the numerical
equation of state, this manifests itself as a discontinuous change, or
a very rapid variation, in the thermodynamic quantities.

The multi-curve comparison in Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_pressure_panels} shows
that the pressure and the resulting equation of state retain the same
qualitative form across the chosen sigma masses, while exhibiting a
moderate sensitivity to the value of \(m_\sigma\). In this sense, the
simple model is best viewed as a controlled baseline that illustrates
how the parameter choice affects the thermodynamics before the
additional stellar constraints are imposed.

The equation of state is constructed by eliminating the chemical
potential and expressing the pressure as a function of the energy

\begin{equation}
P = P(\varepsilon).
\end{equation}

From a numerical perspective, the construction of the equation of
state therefore proceeds in three steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Solve the gap equation for $\bar{\sigma}$ at fixed chemical
      potential.
\item Evaluate $P$ and $\varepsilon$ at the corresponding minimum of
      the grand potential, including vacuum normalization.
\item Construct the relation $P(\varepsilon)$ from the resulting
      dataset.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Speed of sound}

An additional characterization of the equation of state is provided by
the speed of sound,
\begin{equation}
c_s^2 = \frac{dP}{d\varepsilon},
\end{equation}
where \(P\) is the pressure and \(\varepsilon\) is the energy density.
This quantity measures the response of the pressure to compression and
therefore reflects the stiffness of the equation of state.

In the present calculation, both \(P\) and \(\varepsilon\) are obtained
as functions of the quark chemical potential \(\mu_q\). The speed of
sound can therefore be evaluated parametrically as
\begin{equation}
c_s^2
=
\frac{dP/d\mu_q}{d\varepsilon/d\mu_q}.
\end{equation}
This form is convenient in numerical implementations, since the
equation of state is constructed as a table in \(\mu_q\), from which
derivatives can be computed directly.

The behavior of \(c_s^2\) provides information beyond the relation
\(P(\varepsilon)\). In particular, rapid variations indicate regions
where the response of the system changes significantly, such as near
the onset of finite density. In the high-density regime, where the
constituent quark mass becomes small compared with \(\mu_q\), the
system approaches an ultrarelativistic limit in which
\begin{equation}
c_s^2 \to \frac{1}{3}.
\end{equation}
This limit serves as a consistency check on the numerical results.

The numerical results for the two-flavor Quark--Meson model are shown
in Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_speed_of_sound_mu}. For low values of \(\mu_q\),
the speed of sound is approximately zero. This reflects that the
system remains in the vacuum state up to the onset of finite density.
At the transition to quark matter, \(c_s^2\) increases rapidly,
indicating a sudden change in the stiffness of the equation of state.

At larger chemical potentials, \(c_s^2\) approaches the
ultrarelativistic limit \(1/3\) from below. No pronounced overshoot is
observed within the present mean-field setup. This is consistent with
the absence of additional interaction channels, such as diquark
pairing, which in more elaborate models can modify the high-density
behavior \cite{Andersen2024}.

The dependence on the sigma mass \(m_\sigma\) is moderate. Varying
\(m_\sigma\) shifts the location of the transition and slightly
changes the slope of \(c_s^2\), but the qualitative behavior remains
the same: a vanishing speed of sound in the vacuum phase, a rapid
increase at onset, and a smooth approach to the ultrarelativistic
limit at large \(\mu_q\).

\section{Quark stars from the Quark--Meson model}

In the previous section, the Quark--Meson model was used to construct
an equation of state for cold quark matter in a simplified setting,
where the system was characterized by a single quark chemical
potential. While this provides a useful baseline for understanding the
thermodynamic structure of the model, it does not yet correspond to
the conditions realized in compact stars.

In the present section, the same framework is extended to describe
electrically neutral matter in weak equilibrium. The resulting
equation of state is then used as input to the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff
equations in order to construct quark-star configurations and study
their mass--radius relations.

The main objective is to investigate how the stellar properties depend
on the microscopic inputs of the model. In particular, we analyze
the sensitivity to the sigma mass \(m_\sigma\), which
controls the shape of the effective potential, and to the bag
constant \(B\), which fixes the vacuum normalization and shifts the
equation of state.

\subsection*{Stellar matter and construction of the equation of state}

We now construct the equation of state for quark matter under the
conditions relevant for compact stars. As discussed in Part~I, the
matter inside a mature neutron star can be treated to a very good
approximation as cold and in weak-interaction equilibrium. In the
present two-flavor framework, the relevant degrees of freedom are up
quarks, down quarks, and electrons, while neutrinos are assumed to
escape from the star and therefore do not contribute to the
thermodynamics.

The composition of the system is determined by the requirement of
$\beta$-equilibrium. Weak processes of the form
\[
d \leftrightarrow u + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e
\]
convert down quarks into up quarks and electrons, and vice versa.
In chemical equilibrium, the sum of chemical potentials must be
conserved in such reactions. This leads to the condition
\begin{equation}
\mu_d = \mu_u + \mu_e + \mu_{\bar{\nu}_e}.
\end{equation}

In the present context, neutrinos and antineutrinos are not trapped
inside the star but escape on timescales much shorter than the
evolution timescale of the system. As a result, they do not contribute
to the thermodynamic equilibrium and their chemical potential can be
set to zero,
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\bar{\nu}_e} = 0.
\end{equation}
The condition for $\beta$-equilibrium therefore reduces to
\begin{equation}
\mu_d = \mu_u + \mu_e.
\label{eq:qs_beta_eq}
\end{equation}

In addition to weak equilibrium, bulk matter must satisfy local
electric charge neutrality. For two-flavor quark matter, this condition
reads
\begin{equation}
\frac{2}{3} n_u - \frac{1}{3} n_d - n_e = 0,
\label{eq:qs_charge_neutrality_sub}
\end{equation}
where \(n_u\), \(n_d\), and \(n_e\) denote the number densities of
up quarks, down quarks, and electrons, respectively. This ensures that
the net electric charge of the system vanishes, which is required to
avoid large Coulomb energies in macroscopic matter.

A further constraint in quark matter is color neutrality. In general,
this requires that the densities associated with the color charges
corresponding to the diagonal generators of SU(3), commonly denoted
by \(\phi_3\) and \(\phi_8\), vanish. In the present two-flavor
Quark--Meson model, however, all color degrees of freedom are treated
symmetrically, and the quark distributions are identical for each
color. As a consequence, the net color charge vanishes automatically,
and no additional chemical potentials are required to enforce color
neutrality.
This situation changes in more general phases of quark matter, such as
color-superconducting phases, where pairing patterns can distinguish
between different colors. In such cases, color neutrality must be
imposed explicitly through additional constraints.

The thermodynamic potential is therefore generalized to include both
the quark and electron contributions. Before imposing the constraints,
it may be written schematically as
\begin{equation}
\Omega = \Omega_q(\bar{\sigma};\mu_u,\mu_d) + \Omega_e(\mu_e),
\end{equation}
where \(\Omega_q\) is the Quark--Meson contribution and \(\Omega_e\)
is the contribution from the electron gas. The mean-field structure of
the quark sector remains unchanged. In particular, the equilibrium
value of the scalar condensate is still obtained from the gap equation
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \bar{\sigma}} = 0,
\label{eq:qs_gap_eq_stellar}
\end{equation}
which determines the medium-dependent condensate \(\bar{\sigma}\) and
thereby the constituent quark mass,
\begin{equation}
M_q = g\bar{\sigma}.
\end{equation}

It is useful to impose \(\beta\)-equilibrium by changing variables from
the individual quark chemical potentials to a quark-number chemical
potential \(\mu\) and the electron chemical potential \(\mu_e\). Since
\(\mu_e\) couples to negative electric charge, while the electric
charges of the quarks are \(q_u = 2/3\) and \(q_d = -1/3\), the quark
chemical potentials are written as
\begin{equation}
\mu_u = \mu - \frac{2}{3}\mu_e,
\qquad
\mu_d = \mu + \frac{1}{3}\mu_e.
\label{eq:qs_mu_u_mu_d}
\end{equation}
This parametrization automatically satisfies the condition for
\(\beta\)-equilibrium, since
\begin{equation}
\mu_d - \mu_u = \mu_e.
\end{equation}
The remaining independent composition variable is therefore
\(\mu_e\), which is fixed by imposing electric charge neutrality.

With this parametrization, the thermodynamic potential becomes
\begin{equation}
\Omega(\bar{\sigma};\mu,\mu_e)
=
\Omega_q\!\left(\bar{\sigma};
\mu - \frac{2}{3}\mu_e,
\mu + \frac{1}{3}\mu_e
\right)
+
\Omega_e(\mu_e).
\end{equation}
The electron term is included because the electrons required by charge
neutrality are part of the thermodynamic system. In the present model
they are not coupled to the chiral condensate and are treated as a free
relativistic Fermi gas. At zero temperature, the grand potential of a
fermion species is obtained by filling all momentum states up to the
Fermi momentum. For electrons, the corresponding contribution is
therefore the standard degenerate Fermi-gas result
\cite{shapiro_teukolsky,glendenning2000,Schmitt2010}.

The numerical calculation uses the standard massive degenerate
Fermi-gas expressions with the physical electron mass
\(m_e = 0.511~\mathrm{MeV}\). For reference, in the ultrarelativistic
limit \(m_e \ll \mu_e\), these expressions reduce to
\begin{equation}
\Omega_e(\mu_e)
=
-\frac{\mu_e^4}{12\pi^2}.
\label{eq:qs_electron_omega}
\end{equation}
The corresponding limiting expressions for the pressure and number
density are
\begin{equation}
P_e = -\Omega_e = \frac{\mu_e^4}{12\pi^2},
\qquad
n_e = -\frac{\partial \Omega_e}{\partial \mu_e}
= \frac{\mu_e^3}{3\pi^2}.
\end{equation}
The electron energy density is then
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_e
=
-P_e + \mu_e n_e
=
\frac{\mu_e^4}{4\pi^2}.
\end{equation}
Although the electron contribution is usually small compared with the
quark contribution, it must be included in the total pressure and
energy density in order to maintain thermodynamic consistency.

For each value of the control chemical potential \(\mu\), the physical
state is obtained by solving the gap equation together with the
neutrality condition. The latter can be written as stationarity with
respect to \(\mu_e\),
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_e} = 0.
\end{equation}
Using Eq.~(\ref{eq:qs_mu_u_mu_d}) together with
\(n_i = -\partial\Omega/\partial\mu_i\), this gives
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_e}
=
\frac{2}{3}n_u
-
\frac{1}{3}n_d
-
n_e,
\end{equation}
and therefore reproduces the electric charge neutrality condition
\begin{equation}
\frac{2}{3}n_u
-
\frac{1}{3}n_d
-
n_e
=
0.
\end{equation}
Thus, the coupled problem is reduced to solving for the condensate and
the chemical potentials subject to the gap equation and electric charge
neutrality.

Once the stationary point has been found, the total pressure is given
by
\begin{equation}
P = -\Omega_{\min},
\end{equation}
where both the quark and electron contributions are included. The
particle number densities follow from
\begin{equation}
n_f = -\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_f},
\end{equation}
and the energy density is obtained from
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon
=
-P
+
\mu_u n_u
+
\mu_d n_d
+
\mu_e n_e .
\end{equation}

The construction of the equation of state therefore proceeds
parametrically. For each point in the chemical-potential grid, the gap
equation and the neutrality condition are solved, with
\(\beta\)-equilibrium imposed through
\(\mu_e=\mu_d-\mu_u\). This yields
\begin{equation}
\{\mu_u,\,\mu_d,\,\mu_e\}
\;\longrightarrow\;
\{\bar{\sigma},\, M_q,\,
n_u,\, n_d,\, n_e,\, P,\, \varepsilon\}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the result may be viewed as a parametrization in terms
of the quark-number chemical potential \(\mu\). By eliminating the
chemical potentials, one obtains the relation
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon = \varepsilon(P),
\end{equation}
which defines the equation of state required for the stellar structure
calculation.

The resulting composition reflects the requirement of electric charge
neutrality. Since electrons carry negative charge, the system contains
a larger density of down quarks than up quarks, \(n_d > n_u\), while
the electron density remains much smaller than the quark densities.
This behavior is characteristic of cold, two-flavor quark matter in
\(\beta\)-equilibrium and is consistent with previous studies of dense
QCD matter in effective models
\cite{Buballa2005,Schmitt2010,Alford2008}.

\subsection*{Vacuum structure, normalization, and phase construction}

Before constructing the stellar equation of state, it is necessary to
ensure that the underlying model provides a physically consistent
description of the vacuum. In the Quark--Meson model, the vacuum is
determined by the effective potential \(\Omega(\sigma)\) evaluated at
vanishing temperature and chemical potentials. The physical vacuum
corresponds to the global minimum of this potential and fixes the
vacuum value of the chiral condensate.

As discussed previously, the vacuum expectation value is required to
reproduce the pion decay constant, \(\sigma_0 = f_\pi\). This imposes
constraints on the model parameters, in particular on the sigma mass
\(m_\sigma\), which controls the curvature of the effective potential.
If \(m_\sigma\) is chosen too small, the potential may fail to develop
a stable minimum at \(\sigma \simeq f_\pi\), and the model no longer
provides a consistent description of the vacuum.

To determine the range of acceptable parameter values, the vacuum
effective potential \(\Omega(\sigma)\) is evaluated for different
choices of \(m_\sigma\). The results are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_vacuum_scan}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/vacuum_potential_sigma_scan.pdf}
\caption{Vacuum effective potential \(\Omega(\sigma)\), shown in units of
\(f_\pi^4\), as a function of the chiral condensate for several values of
the sigma mass \(m_\sigma = 300, 350, 400, 500, 600, 700~\mathrm{MeV}\).
The vertical dashed line indicates the physical vacuum value
\(\sigma = f_\pi = 93~\mathrm{MeV}\). Curves shown with dotted lines
correspond to parameter choices that do not exhibit a stable minimum at
\(\sigma = f_\pi\) and are therefore excluded.}
\label{fig:qs_vacuum_scan}
\end{figure}

The figure shows that the existence of a physically acceptable vacuum
depends sensitively on the value of \(m_\sigma\). For sufficiently small
values, the point \(\sigma=f_\pi\) is no longer a stable minimum of the
effective potential. Although the stationarity condition may still be
satisfied at this point, the curvature is not positive and the
potential is not bounded from below in the present truncation. The
corresponding parameter choices therefore do not define a stable vacuum
state and are excluded from the stellar calculation. In the present
setup, this occurs for
\(m_\sigma \lesssim 395~\mathrm{MeV}\).

Based on this analysis, we restrict the parameter space to values of
\(m_\sigma\) above this threshold. In the following, we consider the
representative choices
\begin{equation}
m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV},
\end{equation}
which correspond to the lowest values for which a stable vacuum solution
is obtained. These values provide a controlled range for studying the
impact of the effective potential on the thermodynamic and stellar
properties of the model.

Having established the parameter range, we turn to the normalization
of the thermodynamic potential. After renormalization, the vacuum
contribution to \(\Omega\) is generally nonzero, which implies that the
pressure does not vanish in the vacuum state. Since the physical
vacuum should correspond to zero pressure, a constant shift is
introduced,
\begin{equation}
\Omega \rightarrow \Omega + B_0,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
B_0 = \Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}(\sigma_0).
\end{equation}
This ensures that \(P = 0\) in the vacuum configuration.

In addition to this vacuum subtraction, one may introduce an
additional constant shift \(B\), leading to the transformation
\begin{equation}
P \rightarrow P - B, \qquad
\varepsilon \rightarrow \varepsilon + B.
\end{equation}
This parameter plays a role analogous to the bag constant in
phenomenological quark-matter models \cite{Chodos1974,FarhiJaffe1984}.
While \(B_0\) is fixed by the requirement of vacuum normalization, the
additional constant \(B\) can be used to control the onset and
stiffness of the equation of state. In particular, increasing \(B\)
shifts the equation of state toward higher energy densities and
delays the point at which the pressure becomes positive.

A further important aspect is the treatment of the phase transition.
Within the mean-field approximation, the thermodynamic potential may
develop multiple local minima as a function of the chemical
potential. Selecting the global minimum ensures thermodynamic
consistency, but the resulting equation of state may still contain
regions where
\begin{equation}
\frac{dP}{d\mu} < 0,
\end{equation}
which corresponds to a mechanically unstable branch.

To construct a physically meaningful equation of state, this unstable
region is removed by performing a Maxwell construction. This replaces
the non-monotonic segment by a constant-pressure interval,
corresponding to a first-order phase transition between two phases at
equal pressure and chemical potential. The resulting equation of state
is thermodynamically stable and can be used as input for the stellar
structure calculation \cite{glendenning2000,Schmitt2010}.

\subsection*{Equation of state and parameter dependence}

With the constraints of $\beta$-equilibrium and charge neutrality
imposed, and the vacuum structure fixed as discussed above, the
equation of state can now be constructed. The procedure follows
directly from the thermodynamic framework: for each point in the
chemical-potential grid, the gap equation and the neutrality condition
are solved, and the pressure and energy density are evaluated at the
minimum of the grand potential.

The resulting equation of state is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_eos_zoom} for 
\(m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV}\).

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/pressure_vs_energy_density.pdf}
\caption{Equation of state for charge-neutral, $\beta$-equilibrated
two-flavor quark matter. The curves are shown for
\(m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV}\). The dotted segments
represent the direct mean-field result, while the solid curves show
the equation of state after applying the Maxwell construction.}
\label{fig:qs_eos_zoom}
\end{figure}

The dotted curves correspond to the direct mean-field solution,
obtained by selecting the global minimum of the thermodynamic
potential at each value of the chemical potential. As discussed in the
previous subsection, this construction may contain regions where the
pressure decreases with increasing chemical potential, indicating a
mechanically unstable branch. In the \(\varepsilon(P)\) representation,
this manifests as a non-monotonic behavior that cannot be used in a
consistent stellar calculation.

The physically relevant equation of state is obtained by performing a
Maxwell construction, shown by the solid curves in the figure. In this
procedure, the unstable region is replaced by a constant-pressure
segment, corresponding to a first-order phase transition between two
phases at equal pressure and chemical potential. This ensures that the
resulting equation of state is thermodynamically stable and
single-valued.

The dependence on the sigma mass is clearly visible. Lower values of
\(m_\sigma\) lead to a stiffer equation of state, with a more rapid
increase of pressure with energy density, while higher values produce
a softer response. This behavior can be traced back to the shape of
the effective potential and its influence on the evolution of the
chiral condensate with increasing density.

At higher pressures, the curves approach a common behavior, reflecting
that the system becomes increasingly dominated by relativistic quark
degrees of freedom. In this regime, the constituent quark masses become
small compared with the chemical potentials, and the equation of state
approaches that of an ultrarelativistic ideal Fermi gas. Correspondingly,
the different curves tend toward the limiting relation
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon = 3P,
\end{equation}
or equivalently \(c_s^2=dP/d\varepsilon = 1/3\). This is the standard
high-density behavior of a gas of weakly interacting relativistic
fermions \cite{Buballa2005,Schmitt2010}.

\subsection*{Stellar structure and mass--radius relations}

The macroscopic properties of the quark-star configurations are
obtained by solving the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff (TOV) equations,
as introduced in Part~I. Given an equation of state, the TOV equations
are integrated outward from a chosen central pressure until the
pressure vanishes, which defines the radius of the star. Repeating
this procedure for a range of central pressures yields a sequence of
equilibrium configurations characterized by their mass and radius.

In the present calculation, this procedure is applied to the
equations of state constructed for the selected sigma masses
\(m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV}\). For each value of
\(m_\sigma\), a reference bag constant \(B_{\min}\) is first determined
from a stability condition at zero pressure. More precisely, the
shifted equation of state is written as
\begin{equation}
P = P_{B_0} - B,
\qquad
\varepsilon = \varepsilon_{B_0} + B,
\end{equation}
where \(B_0\) denotes the vacuum subtraction discussed above. The value
\(B_{\min}\) is chosen such that the energy per baryon at the
zero-pressure surface satisfies
\begin{equation}
\frac{E}{A} = 931~\mathrm{MeV}.
\end{equation}
This reference value is used as a conventional
stability benchmark, obtained by comparing the energy per baryon of
bulk quark matter at zero pressure with that of ordinary nuclear
matter, represented by iron. This criterion is commonly used in
discussions of absolutely stable quark matter and the Bodmer--Witten
hypothesis \cite{Bodmer1971,Witten1984,FarhiJaffe1984}. In the present
two-flavor calculation, it should be understood as a reference scale
for fixing \(B_{\min}\), rather than as a claim that the model realizes
absolutely stable strange matter.

To test the sensitivity of the stellar solutions to this
phenomenological shift, two additional equations of state are generated
around the reference value. The variation is made in fourth-root units,
\begin{equation}
B^{1/4} = B_{\min}^{1/4} - 10~\mathrm{MeV},
\qquad
B^{1/4} = B_{\min}^{1/4},
\qquad
B^{1/4} = B_{\min}^{1/4} + 10~\mathrm{MeV}.
\end{equation}
The two shifted values are not independent physical constraints, but
are included to show how the mass--radius sequence responds to a
smaller or larger effective bag constant.

For each resulting equation of state, the TOV equations are solved
numerically using the tabulated relation \(\varepsilon(P)\). The
integration produces a family of stellar configurations, which are
represented as mass--radius relations. Along each sequence, the
maximum mass marks the onset of instability, separating stable and
unstable configurations.
The resulting mass--radius relations are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_mass_radius_clean}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering

\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/mass_radius_combined.pdf}

\caption{Mass--radius relations for quark stars obtained from the
Quark--Meson model for \(m_\sigma = 400,\; 500,\; 600~\mathrm{MeV}\)
(top to bottom). 
For each value of \(m_\sigma\), three curves are shown
corresponding to \(B^{1/4}=B_{\min}^{1/4}-10~\mathrm{MeV}\),

Solid lines indicate stable configurations, while dashed lines
denote unstable branches beyond the maximum mass.}
\label{fig:qs_mass_radius_clean}
\end{figure}

It is important to note that the low-mass part of these sequences
should not be interpreted as a realistic description of ordinary
compact stars. In the present calculation, the quark-matter equation of
state is used throughout the entire star, down to the surface where
\(P=0\). This corresponds to an idealized self-bound quark-star
construction. For configurations with small masses and low central
pressures, the densities are not necessarily high enough for deconfined
quark matter to be the appropriate description. In a more realistic
stellar model, one would expect the low-density region to be described
by hadronic matter, possibly with a crust or with a transition to quark
matter only above some critical density. The low-mass, small-radius
segments of the curves should therefore be regarded mainly as a
consequence of extending the pure quark-matter equation of state down
to zero pressure.

The results exhibit a clear dependence on the bag constant. Increasing
\(B\) shifts the equation of state toward higher energy densities and
effectively softens it. As a consequence, the corresponding stellar
sequences support lower maximum masses and typically smaller radii.
Conversely, smaller values of \(B\) lead to a stiffer equation of
state and allow for more massive stars. This behavior is consistent
with the interpretation of the bag constant as a parameter controlling
the onset and stiffness of the quark-matter phase
\cite{FarhiJaffe1984,Buballa2005}.

The dependence on the sigma mass reflects the underlying chiral
dynamics of the model. Lower values of \(m_\sigma\) correspond to a
stiffer effective potential and therefore to a stiffer equation of
state, resulting in more massive and more compact stellar
configurations. Higher values of \(m_\sigma\) lead to softer equations
of state and reduced maximum masses.

Quantitatively, the maximum masses obtained for the different
parameter sets are found to lie in the range
\begin{equation}
M_{\max} \simeq 1.78\text{--}2.12~M_\odot,
\end{equation}
depending on the choice of \(m_\sigma\) and \(B\). For
\(m_\sigma = 400~\mathrm{MeV}\), the lowest bag constant considered
yields a maximum mass of approximately \(2.12~M_\odot\), while
increasing \(B\) reduces the maximum mass to below
\(1.9~M_\odot\). A similar trend is observed for
\(m_\sigma = 500~\mathrm{MeV}\), where the maximum mass remains close
to \(2~M_\odot\) for moderate values of the bag constant but decreases
for larger shifts. For \(m_\sigma = 600~\mathrm{MeV}\), all considered
configurations yield maximum masses below \(1.8~M_\odot\), indicating
that the corresponding equations of state are too soft to support the
most massive observed neutron stars.

Overall, the results demonstrate that both the sigma mass and the bag
constant play a central role in determining the stellar properties.
The sigma mass controls the underlying stiffness through the chiral
dynamics of the model, while the bag constant provides an additional
phenomenological shift that can significantly modify the mass scale of
the solutions. Only a subset of the parameter space is capable of
supporting stars with masses of order \(2~M_\odot\), which provides an
important constraint when comparing the model to astrophysical
observations.

\section{Color-superconducting quark matter}

The quark-matter equations of state constructed above were based on the
ordinary two-flavor Quark--Meson model. In that description the only
mean field was the chiral condensate, and the main microscopic effect was
the density dependence of the constituent quark mass. At sufficiently high
density, however, another instability may become relevant. Quarks occupy
states up to a Fermi surface, and an attractive interaction between quarks
near this surface can lead to the formation of Cooper pairs. This is the
same basic mechanism as in BCS superconductivity, but the pairs are now
formed from quarks rather than electrons. Since quarks carry color charge,
the corresponding phase is called a color-superconducting phase
\cite{Shovkovy2005}.

The microscopic origin of the pairing can be illustrated by quark--quark
scattering through one-gluon exchange, shown schematically in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qq_one_gluon_exchange}. In QCD, this interaction is
attractive in the antisymmetric color-antitriplet channel. This makes the
normal quark Fermi surface unstable toward diquark condensation
\cite{Shovkovy2005}. In this sense, the pairing mechanism is more direct
than in ordinary electronic superconductivity: for electrons, single-photon
exchange is repulsive, and the effective attraction responsible for Cooper
pairing arises from the interaction with the medium, usually through
phonon exchange.

      -- [anti fermion] b

\label{fig:qq_one_gluon_exchange}
\end{figure}

A diquark condensate corresponds to a nonzero expectation value of a
quark--quark operator. Since such an operator carries color, it is not
gauge invariant in QCD. By Elitzur's theorem, a local gauge symmetry
cannot be spontaneously broken in a gauge-invariant formulation
\cite{Elitzur1975}. The condensate should therefore not be interpreted as
a gauge-invariant order parameter in the same sense as the chiral
condensate. In an effective model, or after fixing a color orientation, the
diquark mean field can instead be used as a convenient representation of
the pairing pattern and of the resulting quasiparticle spectrum.

In the present work we restrict the discussion to two flavors, \(u\) and
\(d\). The relevant color-superconducting phase is then the two-flavor
color-superconducting phase, usually denoted by 2SC. In this phase, quarks
pair in a scalar channel that is antisymmetric in both color and flavor.
With a conventional choice of color orientation, the condensate pairs red
and green quarks, while the blue quarks remain unpaired, as shown
schematically in Fig.~\ref{fig:2sc_pairing_schematic}. This choice fixes
the orientation of the condensate in color space and is made for
mathematical convenience. An equivalent description could be obtained by a
color rotation, so the choice of which color remains unpaired should not
be interpreted as a physical preference for blue quarks. In the fixed color
basis used by the effective model, the corresponding pairing pattern
leaves an \(SU(2)_c\) subgroup of the original color structure unchanged
\cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2024}.

This distinction is important when using the Quark--Meson--Diquark model.
The model contains quarks, mesons, and diquark fields, but no dynamical
gluons. As a result, the color symmetry appearing in the model is treated
as a global symmetry. A nonzero value of \(\Delta_0\) therefore signals
spontaneous breaking of this global symmetry within the effective model.
This should be understood as a model representation of the 2SC pairing
structure, not as a gauge-invariant statement about symmetry breaking in
QCD. With this caveat, the effective model used below follows the formulation of
Andersen and Nødtvedt~\cite{Andersen2024}. The full renormalized one-loop
thermodynamic potential is not rederived in the main text.

\subsection*{Diquark degrees of freedom}

Following Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, the superconducting channel is
represented by a complex diquark field \(\Delta_a\), with color index
\(a=1,2,3\). Its schematic quark--quark structure may be written as
\begin{equation}
\Delta_a
\sim
\epsilon_{abc}\epsilon_{ij}
\bar{\psi}^{C\,i}_b \gamma_5 \psi^{\,j}_c ,
\label{eq:2sc_diquark_operator}
\end{equation}
where \(b,c\) are color indices and \(i,j\) are flavor indices. The field
\(\psi^C=C\bar{\psi}^T\) is the charge-conjugate quark field. The tensor
\(\epsilon_{abc}\) is antisymmetric in color space, while
\(\epsilon_{ij}\) is antisymmetric in the two-flavor space. Equivalently,
the flavor structure may be written using \(\tau_2\), up to a conventional
factor.

The structure of Eq.~\eqref{eq:2sc_diquark_operator} fixes the quantum
numbers of the diquark field. The antisymmetric tensor
\(\epsilon_{abc}\) makes the pair a color antitriplet, while
\(\epsilon_{ij}\) gives the antisymmetric flavor-singlet combination. The
factor \(\gamma_5\) selects the scalar spin-zero channel. This is the
standard diquark degree of freedom used in the two-flavor
Quark--Meson--Diquark model \cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2024}.

With the color orientation fixed as in
Fig.~\ref{fig:2sc_pairing_schematic}, we allow the third component of the
diquark field to develop a nonzero expectation value,
\begin{equation}
\Delta_3 \rightarrow \Delta_0 + \tilde{\Delta}_3 ,
\label{eq:2sc_condensate_choice}
\end{equation}
where \(\Delta_0\) is taken to be real and
\(\langle \tilde{\Delta}_3\rangle=0\). This choice fixes the orientation
of the condensate in color space. The other components of \(\Delta_a\) are
not set to zero as fields; rather, their expectation values vanish in this
chosen color basis.

The quantity \(\Delta_0\) is the mean field that appears in the
thermodynamic potential. The quasiparticle gap is proportional to
\begin{equation}
g_\Delta \Delta_0 ,
\end{equation}
where \(g_\Delta\) is the quark--diquark coupling introduced explicitly in
the Lagrangian below.

\subsection*{The Quark--Meson--Diquark Lagrangian}

The two-flavor Quark--Meson--Diquark model is obtained by adding the
diquark field to the Quark--Meson degrees of freedom. Following
Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, we separate the Lagrangian into three parts,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QMD}}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QM}}
+
\mathcal{L}_{\Delta}
+
\mathcal{L}_{q\Delta},
\label{eq:qmd_lagrangian_split}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QM}}\) is the previously introduced
Quark--Meson Lagrangian, \(\mathcal{L}_{\Delta}\) contains the diquark
sector, and \(\mathcal{L}_{q\Delta}\) couples quarks to the diquark field.

The Quark--Meson part is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QM}}
&=
\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\sigma)(\partial^\mu\sigma)
+
\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\pi_0)(\partial^\mu\pi_0)
+
(\partial_\mu+i\mu_I\delta_{\mu0})\pi^+
(\partial^\mu-i\mu_I\delta^{\mu0})\pi^-
\nonumber \\
&\quad
-
\frac{1}{2}m^2(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})
-
\frac{\lambda}{24}(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})^2
+
h\sigma
+
\bar{\psi}(i\slashed{\partial}+\gamma^0\hat{\mu})\psi
-
g\bar{\psi}
\left[
\sigma+i\gamma^5\vec{\pi}\cdot\vec{\tau}
\right]\psi .
\label{eq:qmd_qm_part}
\end{align}
This is the same chiral quark--meson sector used in the previous sections,
now with the chemical-potential matrix \(\hat{\mu}\) allowing different
color and flavor components.

The diquark part is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\Delta}
&=
(\partial_\mu+2i\mu_a\delta_{\mu0})\Delta_a^\dagger
(\partial^\mu-2i\mu_a\delta^{\mu0})\Delta_a
-
m_\Delta^2\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a
\nonumber \\
&\quad
-
\frac{\lambda_3}{12}
(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a
-
\frac{\lambda_\Delta}{6}
(\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a)^2 .
\label{eq:qmd_diquark_part}
\end{align}
Here \(m_\Delta\) is the diquark mass parameter. The quantity \(\mu_a\)
denotes the chemical potential associated with the diquark field
\(\Delta_a\). With the convention used in Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, it
enters the covariant derivative as \(2\mu_a\). For the condensate chosen
in the third color direction, the relevant chemical potential is denoted
by \(\mu_3\). Its relation to the quark chemical potentials is specified
below when the thermodynamic potential is constructed. The coupling
\(\lambda_3\) links the chiral and diquark sectors, and
\(\lambda_\Delta\) is the diquark self-coupling that stabilizes the
potential in the diquark direction.

The quark--diquark interaction is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{q\Delta}
&=
\frac{1}{2}g_\Delta
\bar{\psi}_b^C
\Delta_a\gamma_5\tau_2\epsilon_{abc}\psi_c
+
\frac{1}{2}g_\Delta
\bar{\psi}_b
\Delta_a^\dagger\gamma_5\tau_2\epsilon_{abc}\psi_c^C .
\label{eq:qmd_quark_diquark_part}
\end{align}
The first term couples two quarks to the diquark field, while the second
term is its Hermitian conjugate. The coupling \(g_\Delta\) controls the
pairing strength; when \(\Delta_0\neq0\), the product
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\) is the gap scale in the paired quasiparticle
spectrum. The factors \(\tau_2\) and \(\epsilon_{abc}\) implement the same
antisymmetric flavor and color structure shown explicitly in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:2sc_diquark_operator}. The interaction is therefore in the
color-antitriplet, flavor-singlet scalar channel.

Combining Eqs.~\eqref{eq:qmd_qm_part}--\eqref{eq:qmd_quark_diquark_part},
the full Lagrangian is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}
&=
\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\sigma)(\partial^\mu\sigma)
+
\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\pi_0)(\partial^\mu\pi_0)
+
(\partial_\mu+i\mu_I\delta_{\mu0})\pi^+
(\partial^\mu-i\mu_I\delta^{\mu0})\pi^-
\nonumber \\
&\quad
-
\frac{1}{2}m^2(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})
-
\frac{\lambda}{24}(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})^2
+
h\sigma
\nonumber \\
&\quad
+
(\partial_\mu+2i\mu_a\delta_{\mu0})\Delta_a^\dagger
(\partial^\mu-2i\mu_a\delta^{\mu0})\Delta_a
-
m_\Delta^2\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a
\nonumber \\
&\quad
-
\frac{\lambda_3}{12}
(\sigma^2+\vec{\pi}^{\,2})\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a
-
\frac{\lambda_\Delta}{6}
(\Delta_a^\dagger\Delta_a)^2
\nonumber \\
&\quad
+
\bar{\psi}(i\slashed{\partial}+\gamma^0\hat{\mu})\psi
-
g\bar{\psi}
\left[
\sigma+i\gamma^5\vec{\pi}\cdot\vec{\tau}
\right]\psi
\nonumber \\
&\quad
+
\frac{1}{2}g_\Delta
\bar{\psi}_b^C
\Delta_a\gamma_5\tau_2\epsilon_{abc}\psi_c
+
\frac{1}{2}g_\Delta
\bar{\psi}_b
\Delta_a^\dagger\gamma_5\tau_2\epsilon_{abc}\psi_c^C .
\label{eq:qmd_full_lagrangian}
\end{align}

Compared with the ordinary Quark--Meson model, the superconducting
extension introduces the four new parameters
\begin{equation}
m_\Delta,\qquad
g_\Delta,\qquad
\lambda_3,\qquad
\lambda_\Delta .
\end{equation}
They are not fixed by the vacuum pion, sigma, and constituent-quark input
that determines the mesonic sector. They must therefore be treated as
additional phenomenological parameters of the effective model
\cite{Andersen2024}, and the superconducting equation of state depends on
their chosen values.

\subsection*{Mean-field thermodynamic potential}

Using the mean-field shifts introduced above for \(\sigma\) and
\(\Delta_3\), the fields \(\phi_0\) and \(\Delta_0\) are treated as real
background fields. The chiral mean field determines the constituent quark
mass,
\begin{equation}
M_q = g\phi_0 ,
\end{equation}
while the diquark mean field determines the superconducting gap scale
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\). The mean-field problem is therefore a coupled
minimization in \((\phi_0,\Delta_0)\), as in standard treatments of
color-superconducting quark matter \cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2024}.

The field shifts give the tree-level thermodynamic potential
\begin{align}
\Omega_0
&=
\frac{1}{2}m^2\phi_0^2
+
\left(m_\Delta^2-4\mu_3^2\right)\Delta_0^2
+
\frac{\lambda}{24}\phi_0^4
-
h\phi_0
\nonumber \\
&\quad
+
\frac{\lambda_3}{12}\phi_0^2\Delta_0^2
+
\frac{\lambda_\Delta}{6}\Delta_0^4 ,
\label{eq:qmd_tree_potential}
\end{align}
where
\begin{equation}
\mu_3=\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{ur}+\mu_{dg}\right).
\end{equation}
Here \(\mu_{ur}\) denotes the chemical potential of an up quark with red
color, while \(\mu_{dg}\) denotes the chemical potential of a down quark
with green color. These are two of the quarks that participate in the 2SC
pairing pattern. The diquark field \(\Delta_3\) represents a Cooper pair
made from two quarks, so the chemical potential associated with the pair is
\(\mu_{ur}+\mu_{dg}\). With the convention used in the Lagrangian, this
sum is written as \(2\mu_3\), which gives the term
\(-4\mu_3^2\Delta_0^2\) in Eq.~\eqref{eq:qmd_tree_potential}.

The term proportional to \(\lambda_3\) couples the chiral and diquark
sectors already at tree level, while the term proportional to
\(\lambda_\Delta\) is the diquark self-interaction.

The full thermodynamic potential also contains the quark one-loop
contribution. In the approximation used here, quark fluctuations are
included, while mesonic and diquark fluctuations are neglected. Because the
diquark condensate couples quarks to charge-conjugate quarks, the
fermionic determinant is evaluated in the Nambu--Gorkov form introduced
below.

Following Ref.~\cite{Andersen2024}, the resulting renormalized
thermodynamic potential is denoted by
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{0+1}
=
\Omega_{0+1}(\phi_0,\Delta_0;\bar{\mu},T).
\end{equation}
The full expression is lengthy and is therefore given in
Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential}. In the main text we only need its
structure: for each value of the chemical potential, the equilibrium state
is found by minimizing \(\Omega_{0+1}\) with respect to both mean fields,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial\Omega_{0+1}}{\partial\phi_0}=0,
\qquad
\frac{\partial\Omega_{0+1}}{\partial\Delta_0}=0 .
\label{eq:qmd_gap_equations}
\end{equation}
The first equation is the chiral gap equation, while the second is the
diquark gap equation. A solution with \(\Delta_0=0\) corresponds to
normal quark matter, whereas a solution with \(\Delta_0\neq0\)
corresponds to a color-superconducting state.

The physical pressure is obtained from the value of the thermodynamic
potential at the global minimum,
\begin{equation}
P=-\Omega_{0+1,\min}.
\end{equation}
Thus the superconducting equation of state is determined by the same
thermodynamic principle as in the ordinary Quark--Meson model, but with an
additional order parameter and a modified quasiparticle spectrum. When the
model is later adapted to compact-star matter, the same potential must also
be supplemented by the neutrality constraints appropriate for electrically
and color-neutral matter \cite{Andersen2025}.

\subsection*{Quasiparticle spectrum}

The main effect of the diquark condensate is to modify the fermionic
spectrum. Once the field \(\Delta_3\) develops the expectation value
\(\Delta_0\), the quark--diquark interaction in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qmd_quark_diquark_part} generates terms that mix quarks with
charge-conjugate quarks. This is written in the Nambu--Gorkov basis,
originally introduced for superconductivity \cite{Gorkov1958,Nambu1960}
and standard in color-superconducting mean-field calculations
\cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2024}.

For the present 2SC condensate, it is convenient to introduce the
Nambu--Gorkov spinor
\begin{equation}
\Psi =
\begin{pmatrix}
\psi \\
\psi^C
\end{pmatrix}.
\label{eq:qmd_nambu_gorkov_spinor}
\end{equation}
In this basis the inverse quark propagator is
\begin{equation}
D^{-1}(p)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\slashed{p}-g\phi_0+\gamma^0\hat{\mu}
&
i g_\Delta \tau_2\lambda_2\gamma^5\Delta_0
\\
i g_\Delta \tau_2\lambda_2\gamma^5\Delta_0
&
\slashed{p}-g\phi_0-\gamma^0\hat{\mu}
\end{pmatrix}.
\label{eq:qmd_inverse_propagator}
\end{equation}
The diagonal entries describe quarks and charge-conjugate quarks with
constituent mass \(g\phi_0\). The off-diagonal entries are proportional to
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\) and encode the pairing induced by the diquark
condensate. The tensor \(\epsilon_{3ab}\) associated with the chosen
condensate direction has been written as the Gell-Mann matrix
\(i\lambda_2\), which acts in the red--green color subspace
\cite{Andersen2024}.

The quasiparticle spectrum is obtained from the zeros of
\begin{equation}
\det D^{-1}(p)=0 .
\end{equation}
Because the condensate pairs only red and green quarks in the chosen color
basis, the blue quarks remain unpaired. Their dispersion relations are
\begin{align}
E_{ub}^{\pm}(p) &= E(p)\pm\mu_{ub}, \\
E_{db}^{\pm}(p) &= E(p)\pm\mu_{db},
\end{align}
where
\begin{equation}
E(p)=\sqrt{p^2+g^2\phi_0^2}.
\end{equation}
These modes have the same form as ordinary massive quark excitations.

The paired red-green quasiparticles have dispersion relations
\begin{equation}
E_{\Delta^\pm}^{\pm}(p)
=
E_\Delta^\pm(p)\pm\delta\mu ,
\label{eq:qmd_paired_spectrum}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{align}
E_\Delta^\pm(p)
&=
\sqrt{
\left(E(p)\pm\bar{\mu}\right)^2
+
g_\Delta^2\Delta_0^2
},
\\
\bar{\mu}
&=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{ur}+\mu_{dg}\right)
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{ug}+\mu_{dr}\right),
\\
\delta\mu
&=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{dg}-\mu_{ur}\right)
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{dr}-\mu_{ug}\right).
\end{align}
The quantity \(\bar{\mu}\) is the average chemical potential of the paired
quarks, while \(\delta\mu\) measures the mismatch between their Fermi
surfaces. When \(\delta\mu=0\), the paired modes have the standard
BCS-like form, with gap \(g_\Delta\Delta_0\).

The fermionic one-loop contribution to the thermodynamic potential is
obtained by summing over these quasiparticle energies. This completes the
theoretical definition of the QMD framework used here. The full
renormalized potential is collected in Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential},
including the finite residual integral \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\), whose
treatment is part of the numerical implementation. The next section uses
this framework to construct the equation of state for the 2SC phase.

\section{Numerical QMD equation of state with 2SC pairing}

The previous section introduced the Quark--Meson--Diquark model and the
2SC pairing structure at the level of the Lagrangian, mean fields, and
quasiparticle spectrum. We now use this framework to construct the
corresponding equation of state numerically. The main difference from the
ordinary Quark--Meson calculation is that the thermodynamic potential depends
on two mean fields: the chiral condensate \(\phi_0\) and the diquark
condensate \(\Delta_0\). The equilibrium state must therefore be found by a
two-dimensional minimization at each value of the relevant chemical
potential.

The purpose of this section is not to rederive the QMD thermodynamic
potential, which was given in Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential}~\cite{Andersen2024,Andersen2025}, but to
describe how it is used in practice. We first define the truncation used in
the numerical implementation and specify the diquark-sector parameter sets.
We then consider a common-chemical-potential calculation as a benchmark for
the onset of 2SC pairing. Finally, we impose the neutrality conditions
required for stellar matter and construct the corresponding neutral QMD
equation of state.

Throughout this section, the finite residual integral
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is set to zero. This approximation reduces the
computational cost and allows the full stellar pipeline to be developed. It
should be interpreted as a numerical truncation of the one-loop potential,
not as a modification of the underlying QMD model. The consequences of this
truncation are discussed together with the resulting equation of state.

\subsection*{Numerical setup and parameter choices}

The numerical calculation uses the truncated QMD thermodynamic potential
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\mathrm{trunc}}
=
\Omega_{0+1}\big|_{\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}=0}.
\label{eq:qmd_truncated_potential}
\end{equation}
Here \(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) is the finite residual integral given in
Appendix~\ref{app:qmd_potential}~\cite{Andersen2024}. Setting this term to zero reduces the
computational cost of the stellar calculation. This should be understood as
a numerical truncation of the one-loop potential, not as a modification of
the QMD Lagrangian.

At fixed chemical potentials, the equilibrium state is obtained by selecting
the global minimum of \(\Omega_{\mathrm{trunc}}\) with respect to
\(\phi_0\) and \(\Delta_0\). The chiral condensate determines the
constituent quark mass \(M_q=g\phi_0\), while the diquark condensate
determines the superconducting gap scale \(g_\Delta\Delta_0\).

The diquark-sector parameters used in the calculation are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:qmd_parameter_sets}. Set A is used for the full neutral
stellar equation of state and the mass--radius sequence. Set B is included
as an exploratory comparison in the common-chemical-potential calculation~\cite{Andersen2024,Andersen2025}.

\subsection*{Common-chemical-potential benchmark}

Before imposing the stellar neutrality constraints, we first consider a
simpler benchmark calculation. In this case all quark flavors and colors are
assigned the same quark chemical potential,
\begin{equation}
\mu_{ur}=\mu_{ug}=\mu_{ub}
=
\mu_{dr}=\mu_{dg}=\mu_{db}
=
\mu_q .
\end{equation}
The average chemical potential of the paired quarks is then
\begin{equation}
\bar{\mu}=\mu_q,
\end{equation}
and the mismatch between the paired Fermi surfaces vanishes,
\begin{equation}
\delta\mu=0.
\end{equation}
This limit is not the physical compact-star case, since electric and color
neutrality are not imposed. Its purpose is instead to test the numerical QMD
implementation in the simplest possible setting.

At each value of \(\mu_q\), the truncated potential
\(\Omega_{\mathrm{trunc}}\) is minimized with respect to \(\phi_0\) and
\(\Delta_0\). The global minimum determines whether the preferred state is
normal quark matter or 2SC matter. In this calculation the \(\Delta_0=0\)
limit was also used as a consistency check: when the diquark condensate
vanishes, the QMD potential must reduce to the ordinary two-flavor
Quark--Meson potential, up to the same vacuum normalization convention.

The resulting condensates are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_simple_condensates}. The chiral condensate remains close
to its vacuum value at low chemical potential. In this region the preferred
minimum has \(\Delta_0=0\), so the system is in the normal phase. At the
onset of 2SC pairing, the global minimum moves to a solution with nonzero
\(\Delta_0\). This is accompanied by a rapid decrease of \(\phi_0\), showing
that the onset of pairing is tied to the restoration of chiral symmetry in
this mean-field calculation.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/qmd_simple_condensates.pdf}
\caption{Chiral condensate \(\phi_0\) and superconducting gap
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\) as functions of the common quark chemical potential
\(\mu_q\) in the truncated QMD model.}
\label{fig:qmd_simple_condensates}
\end{figure}

The equation of state obtained from the same common-\(\mu_q\) calculation is
shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_simple_eos}. The pressure is normalized by
subtracting the vacuum value of the thermodynamic potential,
\begin{equation}
P(\mu_q)
=
-\left[
\Omega_{\min}(\mu_q)-\Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}
\right].
\end{equation}
The quark number density is computed from
\begin{equation}
n_q=\frac{dP}{d\mu_q},
\end{equation}
and the energy density follows from the zero-temperature thermodynamic
relation
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon
=
-P+\mu_q n_q .
\end{equation}
The speed of sound is then evaluated parametrically as
\begin{equation}
c_s^2
=
\frac{dP/d\mu_q}{d\varepsilon/d\mu_q}.
\end{equation}

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/qmd_simple_eos.pdf}
\caption{Left panel: equation of state \(P(\varepsilon)\) for the
common-chemical-potential QMD calculation. Right panel: corresponding speed
of sound squared as a function of \(\mu_q\). The dashed line indicates the
conformal value \(c_s^2=1/3\).}
\label{fig:qmd_simple_eos}
\end{figure}

The common-\(\mu_q\) calculation shows the qualitative effect of including
2SC pairing. After the onset of the diquark condensate, the equation of state
becomes stiffer than in the normal phase. This is visible in the speed of
sound, which rises above the conformal value \(c_s^2=1/3\) before decreasing
at larger chemical potential. This behavior is consistent with the QMD
calculations of Refs.~\cite{Andersen2024,Andersen2025}, where the speed of
sound also develops a maximum and approaches the conformal limit from above.

For the present truncated calculation, the common-\(\mu_q\) benchmark should
mainly be viewed as a validation step. It confirms that the coupled
\((\phi_0,\Delta_0)\) minimization works, that the ordinary Quark--Meson
limit is recovered when \(\Delta_0=0\), and that the model produces the
expected qualitative 2SC stiffening. The physically relevant compact-star
equation of state requires the additional neutrality constraints introduced
in the next subsection.

\subsection*{Neutral stellar equation of state}

The common-chemical-potential calculation is useful as a benchmark, but it
does not describe matter in a compact star. Stellar matter must be in weak
equilibrium and must be neutral with respect to electric charge. In the 2SC
phase, the pairing pattern also distinguishes the paired red--green quarks
from the unpaired blue quarks. A color chemical potential is therefore needed
to impose color neutrality. Following the standard treatment of neutral 2SC
matter, we introduce the electron chemical potential \(\mu_e\) and the color
chemical potential \(\mu_8\) in addition to the quark chemical potential
\(\mu_q\) \cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2025}.

The individual quark chemical potentials are written as
\begin{align}
\mu_{ur}=\mu_{ug}
&=
\mu_q-\frac{2}{3}\mu_e+\frac{1}{3}\mu_8,
\\
\mu_{dr}=\mu_{dg}
&=
\mu_q+\frac{1}{3}\mu_e+\frac{1}{3}\mu_8,
\\
\mu_{ub}
&=
\mu_q-\frac{2}{3}\mu_e-\frac{2}{3}\mu_8,
\\
\mu_{db}
&=
\mu_q+\frac{1}{3}\mu_e-\frac{2}{3}\mu_8 .
\end{align}
Here \(\mu_e\) couples to electric charge, while \(\mu_8\) couples to the
diagonal color charge associated with the generator \(T_8\)~\cite{Shovkovy2005,Andersen2025}. With these
definitions, the average chemical potential of the paired quarks becomes
\begin{equation}
\bar{\mu}
=
\frac{1}{2}(\mu_{ur}+\mu_{dg})
=
\mu_q-\frac{1}{6}\mu_e+\frac{1}{3}\mu_8,
\end{equation}
and the mismatch between their Fermi surfaces is
\begin{equation}
\delta\mu
=
\frac{1}{2}(\mu_{dg}-\mu_{ur})
=
\frac{1}{2}\mu_e .
\end{equation}
The quantity \(\delta\mu\) is important because it measures the stress on
the Cooper pairs. If the mismatch becomes comparable to the gap
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\), gapless 2SC modes can appear~\cite{Shovkovy2005,Alford2008}. In the present
calculation this is monitored through the difference
\begin{equation}
g_\Delta\Delta_0-\delta\mu .
\end{equation}
A positive value means that the paired quasiparticle branch remains gapped.

The total thermodynamic potential used for neutral stellar matter is
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\mathrm{tot}}
=
\Omega_{\mathrm{trunc}}(\phi_0,\Delta_0;\mu_q,\mu_e,\mu_8)
+
\Omega_e(\mu_e),
\end{equation}
where \(\Omega_e\) is the free electron contribution. At each fixed value of
\(\mu_q\), the physical state is determined by solving the coupled system
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial\Omega_{\mathrm{tot}}}{\partial\phi_0} &= 0,
&
\frac{\partial\Omega_{\mathrm{tot}}}{\partial\Delta_0} &= 0,
\\
\frac{\partial\Omega_{\mathrm{tot}}}{\partial\mu_e} &= 0,
&
\frac{\partial\Omega_{\mathrm{tot}}}{\partial\mu_8} &= 0 .
\end{align}
The first two equations determine the chiral and diquark condensates. The
last two equations impose electric and color neutrality. Numerically, this
system is solved by minimizing the neutral thermodynamic potential in the
\((\phi_0,\Delta_0)\) plane while solving the two neutrality equations for
\((\mu_e,\mu_8)\) at each trial point. The solution at one value of
\(\mu_q\) is then used as the initial guess for the next value. This
continuation procedure is useful near first-order transitions, where the
minimum can move rapidly.

The resulting neutral condensates are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_stellar_condensates}. The 2SC phase appears at a
chemical potential close to that of the common-\(\mu_q\) benchmark. In
principle, electric neutrality introduces a nonzero \(\mu_e\), which creates a
mismatch between the paired up and down quark Fermi surfaces and shifts the
onset. In practice, \(\mu_e\) remains very small in the normal phase and jumps
discontinuously to a large value at the first-order transition, so the
apparent onset is nearly the same as in the common-\(\mu_q\) case. The color
chemical potential \(\mu_8\) remains approximately zero in the normal phase,
where all colors are treated symmetrically. Once the 2SC condensate forms,
\(\mu_8\) becomes negative in order to restore color neutrality between
the paired red--green sector and the unpaired blue sector.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/qmd_stellar_condensates.pdf}
\caption{Left panel: chiral condensate \(\phi_0\) and superconducting gap
\(g_\Delta\Delta_0\) as functions of the quark chemical potential for
neutral QMD matter. Right panel: electron chemical potential \(\mu_e\) and
color chemical potential \(\mu_8\), determined by the neutrality conditions.}
\label{fig:qmd_stellar_condensates}
\end{figure}

After the neutral minimum has been found, the pressure is defined by
subtracting the vacuum value of the thermodynamic potential,
\begin{equation}
P(\mu_q)
=
-\left[
\Omega_{\min}(\mu_q)-\Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}
\right].
\end{equation}
The quark number density is obtained from
\begin{equation}
n_q = \frac{dP}{d\mu_q},
\end{equation}
and the baryon density is
\begin{equation}
n_B=\frac{1}{3}n_q .
\end{equation}
The energy density is then
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon
=
-P+\mu_q n_q
=
-P+\mu_B n_B,
\qquad
\mu_B=3\mu_q .
\end{equation}
Finally, the speed of sound is evaluated as
\begin{equation}
c_s^2
=
\frac{dP/d\mu_q}{d\varepsilon/d\mu_q}.
\end{equation}

The resulting neutral QMD equation of state is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_stellar_eos}. Compared with the common-\(\mu_q\)
benchmark, the speed of sound is reduced once neutrality is imposed. This is
consistent with the fact that the system has additional composition degrees
of freedom: changes in density can be partly accommodated by changes in
\(\mu_e\), \(\mu_8\), and the relative color--flavor densities, rather than
only by increasing the pressure.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/quark_stars/qmd_stellar_eos.pdf}
\caption{Left panel: neutral QMD equation of state \(P(\varepsilon)\)
obtained from the truncated thermodynamic potential. Right panel: speed of
sound squared as a function of \(\mu_q\) for the same equation of state.
The dashed reference line indicates the conformal value \(c_s^2=1/3\).}
\label{fig:qmd_stellar_eos}
\end{figure}

The speed of sound shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_stellar_eos} is obtained by
numerically differentiating the tabulated equation of state. It is therefore
more sensitive to rapid changes in the numerical solution than the pressure
itself. This is most visible for Set~B near the onset of the 2SC solution,
where a narrow upward spike appears.

This feature occurs in the transition region, where the condensates, and in
neutral matter also the neutrality chemical potentials, can change rapidly
or discontinuously. Since \(c_s^2\) is evaluated from derivatives of \(P\)
and \(\varepsilon\) with respect to \(\mu_q\), it is not well defined at a
discontinuous transition and is numerically delicate near a sharp transition.
The spike should therefore be interpreted as a derivative artifact of the
phase transition, rather than as a regular finite value of the sound speed.
Away from this transition region, the neutral QMD curves remain smooth and
below the causal limit within the range shown.

The raw neutral equation of state contains a small low-pressure region where
\(P(\mu_q)\) is not monotonic. In the speed of sound this appears as a
negative pocket in \(c_s^2\). Such a region is mechanically unstable and
cannot be used directly as input to the TOV equations. As in the ordinary
Quark--Meson calculation, a Maxwell construction or an equivalent stability
prescription is required before constructing stellar sequences~\cite{glendenning2000,Schmitt2010}.

In the present implementation this unstable segment is removed before the
TOV calculation. The stable branch satisfies \(P>0\), \(\varepsilon>0\), and
has monotonic \(\varepsilon(P)\). This stable equation-of-state table is used
as input to the mass--radius calculation in the next subsection.

\subsection*{Mass--radius relations}

After constructing the stable neutral QMD equation of state, the final step is
to solve the TOV equations. The same stellar-structure solver used for the
ordinary Quark--Meson equation of state is used here. The input is the stable
relation \(\varepsilon(P)\) obtained after removing the mechanically unstable
low-pressure segment of the raw QMD equation of state.

Since the present calculation describes pure quark matter throughout the
star, configurations with a finite surface energy density are self-bound quark
stars~\cite{Witten1984,FarhiJaffe1984}. The stellar surface is therefore reached at
\begin{equation}
P=0
\end{equation}
while the energy density remains finite. In the present QMD calculation, the
surface energy density is
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon(P=0)
\simeq
0.20~\mathrm{GeV/fm^3}.
\end{equation}
This is above the characteristic nuclear saturation energy density, and
confirms that the Set~A sequence corresponds to compact self-bound quark-matter
configurations. Set~B, by contrast, has a vanishing surface energy density in
the truncated calculation, meaning it does not produce self-bound stars with
the present parameter set and is therefore not used for the stellar sequence.

The resulting mass--radius relation is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_mass_radius}. The sequence contains both stable and
unstable configurations. As in the previous stellar calculations, stability is
identified along the sequence by the sign of the change in mass with central
pressure. The stable branch extends up to the maximum mass, while
configurations beyond this point are unstable against radial collapse.

\caption{Mass--radius relation for the neutral QMD equation of state with
2SC pairing (Set~A). The equation of state is constructed using the truncated

branch and the dashed curve shows configurations beyond the maximum mass.
The filled circle marks \(M_{\max}\).}
\label{fig:qmd_mass_radius}
\end{figure}

For Set A, the maximum mass is found to be
\begin{equation}
M_{\max}
=
1.89\,M_\odot,
\end{equation}
with corresponding radius
\begin{equation}
R(M_{\max})
=
9.91~\mathrm{km}.
\end{equation}
The radius is relatively small compared with typical hadronic neutron-star
models, which is consistent with the compactness expected for self-bound
quark stars. The maximum mass lies slightly below the observed \(2M_\odot\) mass scale
set by massive pulsars~\cite{Demorest_2010_J1614,Antoniadis_2013_J0348,Cromartie2020,Fonseca2021}, but
remains close enough that changes in the diquark-sector parameters, the bag
shift, or the omitted residual integral could modify this conclusion.

A comparison with the ordinary Quark--Meson stellar sequences is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qmd_vs_qm_mass_radius}. The QMD sequence is more compact for
the chosen parameters, reflecting the combined effect of neutrality, the 2SC
condensate, and the specific vacuum normalization used in the truncated QMD
equation of state. This comparison should not be interpreted as a universal
prediction of the QMD model, since the diquark-sector parameters are not
fixed by vacuum observables.

The main result of this section is the construction of a complete stellar
pipeline for neutral two-flavor QMD matter with 2SC pairing,
\[
\Omega_{\mathrm{trunc}}
\rightarrow
(\phi_0,\Delta_0,\mu_e,\mu_8)
\rightarrow
P(\varepsilon)
\rightarrow
M(R).
\]
The calculation shows explicitly how the diquark condensate enters the
equation of state and how electric and color neutrality modify the
superconducting solution. Quantitatively, the result should still be
interpreted with the truncation in mind: the finite residual integral
\(\Omega_{1,\mathrm{num}}\) has been set to zero throughout. Including this
term and extending the calculation to a broader scan of the diquark-sector
parameters are natural next steps~\cite{Andersen2024,Andersen2025}.
