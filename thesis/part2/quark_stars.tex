\chapter*{Quark Matter and Compact Stars}

The primary goal of this chapter is to constructtion an equation of state
for cold and dense quark matter and to investigate its implications
for compact stars. In contrast to the hadronic models considered in
the Part~I, the matter described here consists entirely
of deconfined quarks. The resulting stellar configurations therefore
represent idealized quark stars. While such objects provide a useful
theoretical framework for isolating the effects of quark degrees of
freedom, they are not expected to give a complete description of
realistic compact stars. A more realistic scenario involves a
transition between hadronic and quark matter, leading to so-called
hybrid stars. This possibility will be considered in a later chapter,
where the present equation of state is combined with a hadronic
description.

Within the QM model, the thermodynamic properties of the system are
determined from the grand canonical potential evaluated in the
mean-field approximation. In this framework, the scalar meson field
develops a non-vanishing expectation value, which acts as an order
parameter for chiral symmetry breaking. The constituent quark mass is
then generated dynamically through the relation
\begin{equation}
m_q = g \langle \sigma \rangle ,
\end{equation}
and becomes a function of the thermodynamic state of the system. This
feature distinguishes the model from free fermion descriptions and is
crucial for obtaining a realistic equation of state.

A first objective of this chapter is to evaluate the grand potential
numerically and determine the equilibrium value of the scalar
condensate by minimizing the thermodynamic potential at each value of
the quark chemical potential. This yields a function
$\bar{\sigma}(\mu)$, and therefore a density-dependent constituent
quark mass $m_q = g\bar{\sigma}(\mu)$.

At low chemical potential, the condensate remains close to its vacuum
value, corresponding to a phase with spontaneously broken chiral
symmetry and large constituent quark masses. As the chemical
potential increases, the minimum of the thermodynamic potential shifts
toward smaller values of the condensate, leading to a reduction of the
quark mass. This behavior reflects the gradual restoration of chiral
symmetry and, depending on the model parameters, may occur through a
chiral phase transition or a smooth crossover
\cite{Folkestad2018,Buballa2005,Schmitt2010}.

Once the thermodynamic quantities have been determined, we impose the
physical conditions relevant for compact stars, namely charge
neutrality and weak equilibrium. These constraints fix the relative
abundances of the different particle species and ensure that the
resulting equation of state is suitable for astrophysical
applications.

In addition to the basic QM model, we also consider extensions that
include additional interaction channels relevant at high density. In
particular, we investigate the possibility of diquark condensation,
which leads to color-superconducting phases of quark matter. These
phases arise from attractive interactions in the quark--quark channel
and are expected to play an important role in the phase structure of
dense QCD \cite{Alford2008,Schmitt2010}. We restrict the analysis to
the two-flavor superconducting (2SC) phase, which represents the
simplest realization of color superconductivity. The inclusion of such
effects modifies the thermodynamic potential and can have a
significant impact on the resulting equation of state.

Finally, the equation of state obtained from the QM model is used as
input to the TOV equations, which determine the structure of
non-rotating compact stars in general relativity. By solving these
equations, we obtain mass--radius relations for quark stars and
analyze how the microscopic properties of the model influence
macroscopic observables.

\section{A Simple Quark--Meson Equation of State}

\subsection*{Thermodynamic framework}

Having established the two-flavor Quark--Meson model in vacuum, the
next step is to use it as a model for cold and dense matter. The
central quantity in this construction is the grand potential density
$\Omega$, since all thermodynamic observables may be obtained from it.
In the present work we restrict attention to zero temperature,
\begin{equation}
T=0,
\end{equation}
which is the regime relevant for compact stars to a very good
approximation.

The thermodynamic treatment is carried out in the mean-field
approximation. In practice, this means that the mesonic fields are
replaced by their expectation values, while the quark fields are kept
as dynamical degrees of freedom and integrated out. This is the same
large-$N_c$ logic used in the derivation of the effective potential in
the previous chapter: quark-loop contributions are retained, whereas
mesonic fluctuations are neglected at this stage. 

With this approximation, the thermodynamics is governed by the grand
potential
\begin{equation}
\Omega = \Omega(\bar{\sigma}; \mu_u,\mu_d,\ldots),
\end{equation}
viewed as a function of the mean scalar field and of the relevant
chemical potentials. In the simplest case considered first below, the
medium is parameterized by a common quark chemical potential. Later,
when the model is adapted to quark-star matter, additional chemical
potentials are introduced in order to impose weak equilibrium and
charge neutrality. The basic thermodynamic structure is, however, the
same in both cases: one first constructs $\Omega$ as a function of the
mean fields and chemical potentials, and then determines the physical
state by minimizing it.

Since the pion condensate is neglected in the present baseline
treatment, only the scalar condensate needs to be determined. The
stationarity condition therefore reduces to
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \bar{\sigma}} = 0.
\label{eq:qs_gap_eq}
\end{equation}
This equation is often referred to as the \emph{gap equation}. Its
solution gives the medium-dependent condensate
$\bar{\sigma}(\mu_u,\mu_d,\ldots)$ and thereby the constituent quark
mass,
\begin{equation}
M_q = g\bar{\sigma}.
\label{eq:qs_constituent_mass}
\end{equation}
The importance of this relation is that the quark mass is not fixed,
but generated dynamically through the chiral condensate. The equation
of state is therefore determined self-consistently rather than by
assuming a prescribed fermion mass. This dynamical mass generation is a
central feature of the Quark--Meson model and is precisely what makes
it qualitatively different from models in which quark mass is treated as
a fixed parameter \cite{Buballa2005,Schmitt2010,sletmoen2022}.

The vacuum expectation value \(\sigma_0=f_\pi\) is fixed by the pion
decay constant, whereas the sigma mass is less sharply constrained and
is therefore commonly treated as a model parameter 
\cite{SchaeferWambach2008,SchaeferPawlowskiWambach2007,Buballa2005}.
In the simple baseline considered here, the model is therefore
evaluated for the representative choices
\(m_\sigma=500,600,\) and \(700\,\mathrm{MeV}\). This
variation changes the curvature of the effective potential and thereby
the detailed thermodynamic response, while leaving the underlying
two-flavor mean-field setup unchanged \cite{Folkestad2018,Andersen2024}.

Once the stationary point of $\Omega$ has been found, the pressure is
identified as minus the grand potential evaluated at the minimum,
\begin{equation}
P = -\Omega_{\min}.
\label{eq:qs_pressure_def}
\end{equation}
The particle number densities follow from derivatives with respect to
the corresponding chemical potentials,
\begin{equation}
n_f = -\frac{\partial \Omega}{\partial \mu_f},
\label{eq:qs_density_def}
\end{equation}
and the energy density is obtained from the thermodynamic relation
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon = -P + \sum_f \mu_f n_f.
\label{eq:qs_energy_def}
\end{equation}
In the constrained stellar calculation, the sum is extended to include
the electron contribution as well.

It is useful to keep in mind that this thermodynamic framework serves
two distinct purposes in the chapter. First, it allows us to study the
Quark--Meson model in a simplified setting, where the role of the
chiral condensate and the onset of dense matter can be seen clearly.
Second, the same framework can be extended by imposing the additional
conditions required for stellar matter, namely local charge neutrality
and $\beta$-equilibrium. The unconstrained and constrained equations of
state are therefore not based on different formalisms, but on the same
grand-potential construction with different choices of thermodynamic
constraints.

\subsection*{Medium contribution at zero temperature}

At zero temperature, the thermodynamic potential separates into a
vacuum contribution and a medium-dependent part associated with the
filled Fermi sea. The latter can be expressed in terms of the Fermi
momentum
\begin{equation}
k_{F,f} = \sqrt{\mu_f^2 - M_q^2},
\end{equation}
where $M_q = g\bar{\sigma}$ is the constituent quark mass.

The medium contribution is nonvanishing only when the chemical
potential exceeds the constituent quark mass, $\mu_f > M_q$. In this
case, a Fermi surface is formed and quark states are occupied up to
the Fermi momentum. For $\mu_f < M_q$, the Fermi momentum becomes
imaginary, indicating that no states are occupied and the medium
contribution vanishes. The system therefore remains in the vacuum
state, with zero pressure and vanishing number density. This behavior
is characteristic of zero-temperature fermionic systems and is also
found in the Quark--Meson model in the mean-field approximation
\cite{andresen2023,sletmoen2022}.

The resulting expressions for the pressure, number density, and energy
density are obtained from standard phase-space integrals over the
occupied states. In particular, the number density is proportional to
$k_{F,f}^3$, while the pressure and energy density involve integrals
over the single-particle energy. For each flavor one has
\begin{equation}
n_f = \frac{N_c}{3\pi^2} k_{F,f}^3.
\end{equation}
These quantities depend explicitly on
the chemical potentials and on the dynamically generated mass, and
therefore inherit the medium dependence of the chiral condensate. The
equation of state is thus determined self-consistently once the
condensate has been computed from the gap equation. The behavior of
the condensate together with the onset of finite density is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_sigma_density_mu}.

As the chemical potential increases, the solution to the gap equation
changes, leading to a reduction of the condensate and therefore of the
constituent quark mass. This reflects the gradual restoration of
chiral symmetry in dense matter. At a critical chemical potential, the
preferred solution of the gap equation changes abruptly, resulting in
a discontinuous change in the condensate. Within the present
mean-field treatment, this corresponds to a first-order phase
transition between the vacuum and a dense quark-matter phase 
\cite{andresen2023,sletmoen2022}.

Changing \(m_\sigma\) modifies the stiffness of the effective
potential and thereby the detailed evolution of the condensate through
the transition region. This shifts the onset of finite density and
produces a corresponding spread in the density curves, but the overall
structure remains the same for the three representative parameter
choices.

Beyond the transition, the Fermi momentum grows with increasing
chemical potential, and the pressure and energy density increase
monotonically. The behavior of these quantities as functions of the
chemical potential forms the basis for the numerical construction of
the equation of state presented in the following subsection.

\subsection*{Vacuum normalization}

The thermodynamic potential obtained from the Quark--Meson model
contains a vacuum contribution which, after renormalization, is
generally nonzero. As a consequence, the pressure defined by Eq.~(\ref{eq:qs_pressure_def})
does not vanish in the vacuum state, i.e.\ at vanishing chemical
potentials. This is in contrast to the physical expectation that the
vacuum should correspond to zero pressure.

For applications to compact stars, it is therefore necessary to
normalize the pressure such that
\begin{equation}
P(\mu_f = 0) = 0.
\end{equation}
This is achieved by introducing a constant shift in the thermodynamic
potential,
\begin{equation}
\Omega \rightarrow \Omega + B,
\end{equation}
which corresponds to the transformation
\begin{equation}
P \rightarrow P - B, \qquad
\varepsilon \rightarrow \varepsilon + B.
\end{equation}
The constant $B$ is commonly referred to as a \emph{bag constant}, by
analogy with the MIT bag model \cite{Chodos1974,Chodos1974b}.

In the present framework, this shift has a clear interpretation. The
renormalized vacuum effective potential determines the absolute value
of the energy density of the vacuum, but this value is not fixed by
the theory itself. Only differences in the thermodynamic potential are
physically meaningful. The constant $B$ therefore fixes the reference
point of the energy density and ensures that the physical vacuum
corresponds to zero pressure.

In practice, it is convenient to separate this normalization into two
steps. First, a vacuum subtraction is performed such that the pressure
vanishes in the true vacuum configuration,
\begin{equation}
B_0 = \Omega_{\mathrm{vac}}(\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{vac}}),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\sigma}_{\mathrm{vac}}$ denotes the solution of the gap
equation at zero chemical potential. This guarantees that the vacuum
state is correctly normalized.

In addition, one may introduce a further constant shift $B$, which can
be used to modify the stiffness of the equation of state. While $B_0$
is fixed by the requirement of vacuum normalization, the additional
shift $B$ acts as a phenomenological parameter that effectively
controls the onset of the high-density phase. This is analogous to the
role of the bag constant in phenomenological quark-matter models
\cite{sletmoen2022}.

It is important to emphasize that constant shifts of the pressure do
not affect thermodynamic derivatives such as the number density or the
speed of sound. However, they do change the relation between pressure
and energy density,
\begin{equation}
P = P(\varepsilon),
\end{equation}
which is the quantity entering the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff
equations. The choice of $B$ therefore has a direct impact on the
resulting mass--radius relations of compact stars.

From a numerical perspective, the effect of the vacuum normalization
can be seen as a vertical shift of the pressure curve. In particular,
the condition $P=0$ defines the surface of the star, and the
normalization ensures that this point corresponds to the physical
vacuum rather than an unphysical offset in the thermodynamic
potential.

\subsection*{Construction of the equation of state}

The full equation of state is obtained by solving the gap equation for
$\bar{\sigma}$ at each value of the chemical potential and evaluating
the thermodynamic quantities at the corresponding minimum of the grand
potential. In practice, this procedure defines a mapping from the
chemical potentials to the thermodynamic observables,
\begin{equation}
\mu_f \;\longrightarrow\; \{\bar{\sigma},\, M_q,\, n_f,\, P,\, \varepsilon\}.
\end{equation}

For each value of the chemical potential, the gap equation
\eqref{eq:qs_gap_eq} is solved to determine the condensate
$\bar{\sigma}$. This fixes the constituent quark mass through
$M_q = g\bar{\sigma}$, which in turn determines the Fermi momenta and
the medium contribution to the thermodynamic potential. The pressure,
number densities, and energy density are then evaluated using
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:qs_pressure_def}--\eqref{eq:qs_energy_def}, including
the vacuum normalization discussed above. Numerically, the global
minimum of the grand potential is selected at each value of the
chemical potential, so that the thermodynamically preferred phase is
obtained.

This relation is obtained numerically by tabulating $P$ and
$\varepsilon$ as functions of the chemical potential and performing a
parametric representation of the curve. The resulting function
$P(\varepsilon)$ serves as input for the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff
equations governing the structure of compact stars \cite{sletmoen2022}.
In the numerical construction, only the branch with non-negative
pressure is retained in the plotted equation of state.
The corresponding pressure as a function of chemical potential, together
with the resulting equation of state, is displayed in
Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_pressure_panels}.

It is important to note that the structure of the equation of state is
directly influenced by the behavior of the condensate. In particular,
a discontinuous change in $\bar{\sigma}$ as a function of the chemical
potential leads to a corresponding discontinuity in the thermodynamic
quantities, signaling a first-order phase transition. In the numerical
equation of state, this manifests itself as a discontinuous change, or
a very rapid variation, in the thermodynamic quantities.

The multi-curve comparison in Fig.~\ref{fig:qs_pressure_panels} shows
that the pressure and the resulting equation of state retain the same
qualitative form across the chosen sigma masses, while exhibiting a
moderate sensitivity to the value of \(m_\sigma\). In this sense, the
simple model is best viewed as a controlled baseline that illustrates
how the parameter choice affects the thermodynamics before the
additional stellar constraints are imposed.

The equation of state is constructed by eliminating the chemical
potential and expressing the pressure as a function of the energy
density,
\begin{equation}
P = P(\varepsilon).
\end{equation}

From a numerical perspective, the construction of the equation of
state therefore proceeds in three steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Solve the gap equation for $\bar{\sigma}$ at fixed chemical
      potential.
\item Evaluate $P$ and $\varepsilon$ at the corresponding minimum of
      the grand potential, including vacuum normalization.
\item Construct the relation $P(\varepsilon)$ from the resulting
      dataset.
\end{enumerate}
