\chapter{Quantum Field Theory}
\label{chap:qft}

Quantum field theory (QFT) provides the modern theoretical framework for
describing elementary particles and their interactions. It underlies the
Standard Model of particle physics and plays a central role in both
high-energy physics and condensed matter theory. 
In the context of dense matter and compact stars, QFT offers
a natural language for describing interacting particles at high densities and
for connecting microscopic physics to macroscopic quantities
\cite{PeskinSchroeder1995,Weinberg1995}.

The need for QFT arises from the limitations of
non-relativistic quantum mechanics. While quantum mechanics successfully
describes a wide range of phenomena at atomic and molecular scales, it is not
compatible with special relativity in a fundamental way. In particular, the
Schrödinger equation treats time and space asymmetrically and does not enforce
relativistic causality. When relativistic effects become important, the notion
of a fixed number of particles breaks down, and attempts to combine quantum
mechanics with special relativity at the level of single-particle wavefunctions
lead to conceptual and physical inconsistencies
\cite{BjorkenDrell1964}.

These issues can be illustrated with simple thought experiments that expose a
tension between quantum mechanics and special relativity. One such
example is provided by a particle confined to a box that is observed from a
highly boosted reference frame \cite{Vutha_2013}. In the rest frame of the box,
the ground state has a well-defined energy, even though the particle’s momentum
fluctuates due to confinement. When the system is viewed from a relativistically
boosted frame, these momentum fluctuations are converted into fluctuations in
the particle’s energy. As a result, a state that is an energy eigenstate in one
inertial frame acquires an energy uncertainty in another. Within ordinary
quantum mechanics, this leads to a conceptual problem: energy fluctuations imply
a finite lifetime and an exchange of energy, yet the vacuum is not treated as a
dynamical object and cannot act as a reservoir. There is therefore no consistent
way to interpret these fluctuations within the Schrödinger framework alone.

Quantum field theory resolves this difficulty by allowing particle number to
change and by treating the vacuum as an active part of the theory. In QFT,
particles are not fundamental objects but appear as excitations of underlying
quantum fields that extend throughout space and time. This framework naturally
incorporates special relativity and provides a consistent description of
processes involving particle creation and annihilation. It also offers a simple
explanation for the existence of identical particles: particles of the same type
are identical because they arise as excitations of the same underlying field
\cite{Weinberg1995}. In this way, QFT provides a coherent resolution of the
conflict encountered in relativistic quantum mechanics.

One of the most successful realizations of quantum field theory is quantum
electrodynamics (QED), the relativistic field theory of electrons and photons.
QED is among the most precisely tested theories in physics, with experimental
measurements agreeing with theoretical predictions to remarkable accuracy.
This level of agreement illustrates the effectiveness of quantum field theory
as a framework for describing fundamental interactions beyond a purely
particle-based picture \cite{PeskinSchroeder1995}. More generally, QFT provides
a unified framework in which quantum mechanics and special relativity are
combined in a consistent manner.

For the study of dense matter, QFT is particularly useful
because it allows interactions, symmetries, and conservation laws to be treated
systematically. Thermodynamic quantities such as pressure and energy density can
be derived directly from an underlying Lagrangian, making it possible to
construct equations of state from microscopic models. In the following
sections, the basic concepts of QFT relevant for this work will
be introduced, with an emphasis on those needed to model strongly interacting
matter at finite density and to prepare for the QCD-inspired descriptions used
later in the thesis.

\section{Bridging quantum mechanics and field theory}
\label{sec:bridging_qm_qft}

In this section we briefly introduce the conceptual and mathematical frameworks
that connect ordinary quantum mechanics to QFT. 
The emphasis is on the path-integral formulation. This approach generalizes
naturally from systems with a fixed number of degrees of freedom to
relativistic fields and provides a convenient starting point for describing
interacting matter.

\subsection*{Action principle and path integrals in quantum mechanics}

In classical mechanics, the dynamics of a system with generalized coordinate
$q(t)$ is determined by the action
\begin{equation}
S[q] = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} \! dt \, L\bigl(q(t), \dot{q}(t)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $L$ is the Lagrangian of the system. The classical equations of motion
follow from the principle of stationary action, $\delta S = 0$.

In quantum mechanics, an alternative formulation of dynamics is provided by the
path-integral approach. Instead of describing time evolution in terms of wave
functions and operators, transition amplitudes between initial and final states
are expressed as sums over all possible trajectories connecting them. Formally,
the transition amplitude can be written as
\begin{equation}
\langle q_f, t_f | q_i, t_i \rangle
= \int \mathcal{D}q(t)\,
\exp\!\left(\frac{i}{\hbar} S[q]\right),
\end{equation}
where the functional integral runs over all paths $q(t)$ satisfying the
boundary conditions $q(t_i)=q_i$ and $q(t_f)=q_f$
\cite{FeynmanHibbs1965,Schulman1981}.

Physical observables in this formulation are expressed in terms of correlation
functions, which measure how the value of a dynamical variable at one time is
related to its value at another time. Correlation functions therefore encode
information about the system’s dynamics. In quantum mechanics, the dynamical 
variables are represented by operators, and expectation values are taken with
respect to a given quantum state. As an example, the time-ordered two-point function
\(\langle T\, q(t)\, q(t') \rangle\) describes how fluctuations of the quantum
degree of freedom \(q\) at time \(t'\) are correlated with fluctuations at time
\(t\). Here \(T\) denotes the time-ordering operator, which arranges operators
according to their time arguments and ensures a causal interpretation of the
correlation function. This two-point function therefore plays the role of a
propagator, characterizing how disturbances of the system evolve in time, and
this viewpoint generalizes directly to QFT, where the dynamical
variables are fields defined throughout spacetime.

\subsection*{Generating functionals}

A convenient way to handle correlation functions is to introduce an external
source that couples to the system. In quantum mechanics, this is done by adding
a term of the form $J(t)\,q(t)$ to the action. The source $J(t)$ is a classical,
prescribed function that does not describe a physical force, but is introduced
as a mathematical tool. It allows us to probe how the system responds to small
disturbances and to extract correlation functions in a systematic way.

With the source included, one defines the generating functional
\begin{equation}
Z[J] = \int \mathcal{D}q(t)\,
\exp\!\left[\frac{i}{\hbar}
\left(S[q] + \int dt\, J(t)\, q(t)\right)\right].
\end{equation}
By taking functional derivatives of $Z[J]$ with respect to the source and then
setting $J=0$, one generates time-ordered correlation functions of the dynamical
variable $q(t)$,
\begin{equation}
\langle T\, q(t_1)\cdots q(t_n)\rangle
= \left. \frac{1}{i^n}
\frac{\delta^n Z[J]}{\delta J(t_1)\cdots \delta J(t_n)}
\right|_{J=0}.
\end{equation}
In this sense, the generating functional acts as a compact object from which all
correlation functions of the theory can be obtained. This idea extends directly
to QFT, where the dynamical variables are fields and the source
becomes a function of spacetime.


\subsection*{From particles to fields}

The transition from quantum mechanics to QFT amounts to
replacing a finite set of dynamical variables by fields defined at every point
in spacetime. A real scalar field $\phi(x)$ assigns a single real number to each
spacetime point $x=(t,\mathbf{x})$. The dynamics of the field are specified by
a Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L}(\phi,\partial_\mu\phi)$, and the action takes
the form
\begin{equation}
S[\phi] = \int d^4x \, \mathcal{L}(\phi,\partial_\mu\phi).
\end{equation}
The classical equations of motion follow from the condition $\delta S=0$ and are
given by the Euler–Lagrange equations for fields.

In the path-integral formulation of QFT, the generating
functional is defined as
\begin{equation}
Z[J] = \int \mathcal{D}\phi \,
\exp\!\left[i\left(S[\phi] + \int d^4x\, J(x)\,\phi(x)\right)\right],
\end{equation}
where $J(x)$ is a spacetime-dependent source. As in quantum mechanics, all
time-ordered correlation functions of the field can be obtained by functional
differentiation with respect to $J(x)$. In particular, the two-point function
plays the role of the propagator and describes how field fluctuations propagate
in spacetime.

The path-integral formulation provides a unified and flexible framework for
QFT. It treats space and time on an equal footing, makes the
role of spacetime symmetries transparent, and generalizes naturally to systems with many
interacting degrees of freedom. For these reasons, it will serve as the primary
language for the scalar field theories discussed in the following sections and
for the effective models of dense matter introduced later in this thesis
\cite{PeskinSchroeder1995,Weinberg1995}.

\section{Free scalar field}
\label{sec:free_scalar_field}

We now turn to the simplest example of a relativistic quantum field theory: a
real scalar field. Despite its simplicity, this theory already illustrates
many of the structural features common to more realistic models, including the
role of symmetries, conserved currents, and propagators. It also provides a
natural starting point for introducing interactions in a controlled way.

\subsection*{Classical scalar field}

A real scalar field $\phi(x)$ is a real-valued function defined on spacetime,
where $x=(t,\mathbf{x})$. The dynamics of a free relativistic scalar field are
specified by the Lagrangian density
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_0
= \frac{1}{2}\partial_\mu \phi\, \partial^\mu \phi
- \frac{1}{2} m^2 \phi^2 ,
\end{equation}
where $m$ is a mass parameter. The action
is obtained by integrating the Lagrangian density over spacetime,
\begin{equation}
S_0[\phi] = \int d^4x \, \mathcal{L}_0 .
\end{equation}

Varying the action with respect to the field yields the Euler--Lagrange equation
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_0}{\partial \phi}
- \partial_\mu
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_0}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi)} = 0 ,
\end{equation}
which leads to the Klein--Gordon equation
\begin{equation}
(\Box + m^2)\phi(x) = 0 ,
\end{equation}
where $\Box = \partial_\mu\partial^\mu$ is the d'Alembert operator.

The Klein--Gordon equation is the relativistic wave equation for a
scalar field. Due to the linearity of the free theory, the field can be
decomposed into independent modes, each of which evolves separately. At the
classical level, each such mode behaves like a harmonic oscillator with a
well-defined frequency. Since the quantum harmonic oscillator is a standard and
well-understood system, this structure provides a direct link between the
classical field description and its quantum counterpart. In this way, the free
scalar field can be viewed as a collection of independent quantum oscillators,
and particles emerge naturally as excitations of the field \cite{Kachelriess2017}.

\subsection*{Symmetries and Noether's theorem}

A central organizing principle in field theory is the role of symmetries.
Symmetries of the action constrain the dynamics and lead directly to conserved
quantities. For the free scalar field, the most important symmetries are those
associated with spacetime itself.

The action $S_0[\phi]$ is invariant under spacetime translations,
\begin{equation}
x^\mu \rightarrow x^\mu + a^\mu ,
\end{equation}
where $a^\mu$ is a constant four-vector. Physically, this symmetry expresses the
homogeneity of spacetime: the laws of physics are the same at all points in
space and time. Under an infinitesimal translation, the field transforms as
\begin{equation}
\phi(x) \rightarrow \phi(x-a)
\qquad \Rightarrow \qquad
\delta\phi(x) = -a^\nu \partial_\nu \phi(x) .
\end{equation}
Since the Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L}_0$ depends on the field and its
derivatives but not explicitly on the spacetime coordinates $x^\mu$, the action
remains unchanged under this transformation.

According to Noether's theorem, every continuous symmetry of the action gives
rise to a conserved current \cite{Noether1918,Weinberg1995}. Applied to spacetime
translations, this theorem leads to the conservation of energy and momentum. The
corresponding conserved current is the energy--momentum tensor,
\begin{equation}
T^{\mu\nu}
= \partial^\mu \phi\, \partial^\nu \phi
- g^{\mu\nu}\mathcal{L}_0 .
\end{equation}
Using the equations of motion, one finds that this tensor satisfies the local
conservation law
\begin{equation}
\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0 .
\end{equation}

The energy--momentum tensor $T^{\mu\nu}$ has a clear physical interpretation.
Its components describe how energy and momentum are distributed in spacetime
and how they flow through space. In particular, the components $T^{0\nu}$ play a
special role. Integrating them over space defines conserved quantities,
\begin{equation}
P^\nu = \int d^3x \, T^{0\nu} ,
\end{equation}
which are constant in time as a consequence of the conservation law
$\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu}=0$.

The quantity $P^0$ corresponds to the total energy of the field configuration,
while the spatial components $P^i$ correspond to the total momentum. These
conserved quantities are directly associated with spacetime translation
symmetry: conservation of energy follows from invariance under time
translations, and conservation of momentum follows from invariance under spatial
translations. In this way, the energy--momentum tensor provides the concrete link
between spacetime symmetries and conserved physical quantities
\cite{Pauli1941}.

Noether's theorem thus provides a direct link between symmetry and conservation
laws in QFT. While spacetime symmetries lead to conservation of
energy and momentum, the same reasoning applies to internal symmetries, which
give rise to conserved charges that play a crucial role in interacting field
theories and in the description of many-body systems.

\subsection*{Free scalar quantum field and generating functional}

The quantum theory of a free scalar field is defined through the path
integral. Introducing a spacetime-dependent source $J(x)$, the generating
functional is given by
\begin{equation}
Z_0[J] =
\int \mathcal{D}\phi\,
\exp\!\left[i\left(
S_0[\phi] + \int d^4x\, J(x)\phi(x)
\right)\right].
\end{equation}
The source $J(x)$ is a classical auxiliary field that allows correlation
functions of $\phi(x)$ to be calculated by functional differentiation. In this
sense, the generating functional provides a compact way of organizing the
dynamical content of the theory \cite{Schwinger1951,FeynmanHibbs1965}.

Because the action is quadratic in the field, the functional integral is
Gaussian and can be evaluated exactly. While the explicit calculation will not
be carried out here, its structure implies that all correlation functions of the
free theory can be reduced to combinations of the two-point function
\cite{Kachelriess2017}.

The time-ordered two-point correlation function,
\begin{equation}
D_F(x-y)
= \langle T\,\phi(x)\phi(y)\rangle ,
\end{equation}
is known as the Feynman propagator. Here, $T$ denotes the time-ordering
operator, which arranges the field operators such that those evaluated at later
times appear to the left. Time ordering ensures a causal definition of
correlation functions and is essential in QFT,
where field operators at different spacetime points do not generally commute.
The propagator describes the propagation of field fluctuations between
spacetime points $x$ and $y$ and plays a central role in perturbative
calculations. Formally, it is the Green's function of the Klein--Gordon
operator,
\begin{equation}
(\Box_x + m^2) D_F(x-y) = -i\,\delta^{(4)}(x-y) .
\end{equation}
All higher-order correlation functions of the free theory can be obtained by
functional differentiation of $Z_0[J]$ with respect to the source $J(x)$.

\section{Interacting scalar fields: the \texorpdfstring{$\phi^4$}{phi4} theory}
\label{sec:phi4_theory}

Having established the basic framework for free scalar fields, we now introduce
interactions in the simplest possible setting. The interacting scalar field
theory with a quartic self-interaction, commonly referred to as $\phi^4$ theory,
provides a minimal extension of the free theory in which interaction
effects appear. Even in this simple model, interactions lead to nonlinear
dynamics, a modification of propagators, and a nontrivial vacuum structure.
Moreover, $\phi^4$ theory offers a transparent and controlled setting in which
spontaneous symmetry breaking can be examined explicitly. Concepts such as
degenerate vacuum states and fluctuations around a chosen ground state arise
naturally in this context. These ideas reappear in more realistic descriptions
of strongly interacting matter and motivate the use of $\phi^4$ theory as a
starting point for developing intuition and establishing notation before
turning to more complex field-theoretic models.

\subsection*{Lagrangian and symmetries}

The Lagrangian density of the interacting scalar field is given by
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}
= \frac{1}{2}\partial_\mu \phi\,\partial^\mu \phi
- \frac{1}{2} m^2 \phi^2
- \frac{\lambda}{4!}\,\phi^4 ,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a real coupling constant. For $\lambda>0$, the interaction
term ensures that the energy of the system is bounded from below.

The theory is invariant under the discrete transformation
\begin{equation}
\phi(x) \rightarrow -\phi(x) ,
\end{equation}
which corresponds to a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry of the action. This symmetry
reflects the fact that the Lagrangian depends only on even powers of the field
and therefore treats positive and negative values of $\phi$ equally.

\subsection*{Classical potential, vacuum structure, and symmetry breaking}

For static and spatially uniform field configurations, the dynamics of the
theory are governed by the classical potential
\begin{equation}
V(\phi)
= \frac{1}{2} m^2 \phi^2
+ \frac{\lambda}{4!}\,\phi^4 .
\label{eq:classic_potential}
\end{equation}
The vacuum of the theory corresponds to the field configuration that minimizes
this potential. Restricting to uniform fields is sufficient for determining the
vacuum structure. The gradient terms increase the energy and therefore do not
contribute to the lowest-energy state.

If $m^2>0$, the potential has a single minimum at $\phi=0$, and the
$\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry of the Lagrangian is realized trivially. In contrast, if
$m^2<0$, the potential takes the form of a double well with two degenerate minima
located at
\begin{equation}
\phi = \pm v, \qquad
v = \sqrt{\frac{-6m^2}{\lambda}} .
\end{equation}
Although the Lagrangian remains invariant under the transformation
$\phi \rightarrow -\phi$, the ground state of the theory is not. In this case,
the system must select one of the two minima as its vacuum state, and the
$\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry is said to be spontaneously broken.

Fig.~\ref{fig:phi4_potential} illustrates this qualitative change in the vacuum
structure: for $m^2>0$ the potential has a single minimum at $\phi=0$, while for
$m^2<0$ it develops two degenerate minima at $\phi=\pm v$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/qft/symmetry_breaking.pdf}
  \caption{Classical $\phi^4$ potential
  $V(\phi)=\frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2+\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4$ for $\lambda>0$.
  For $m^2>0$ the vacuum is at $\phi=0$. For $m^2<0$ the potential becomes a
  double well with degenerate minima at $\phi=\pm v$, where
  $v=\sqrt{-6m^2/\lambda}$.}
  \label{fig:phi4_potential}
\end{figure}

To study quantum fluctuations around a chosen vacuum, the field is written as
\begin{equation}
\phi(x) = v + \eta(x) ,
\end{equation}
where $v$ is the vacuum expectation value that minimizes the classical
potential and $\eta(x)$ describes small fluctuations about this vacuum. The
Lagrangian can then be expanded in powers of $\eta$.

Inserting $\phi=v+\eta$ into the potential in \eqref{eq:classic_potential},
and expanding about $\phi=v$ gives
\begin{equation}
V(v+\eta)
= V(v)
+ \left.\frac{dV}{d\phi}\right|_{\phi=v}\eta
+ \frac{1}{2}\left.\frac{d^2V}{d\phi^2}\right|_{\phi=v}\eta^2
+ \mathcal{O}(\eta^3).
\end{equation}
Since $v$ is a minimum of the potential, the first derivative vanishes,
\begin{equation}
\left.\frac{dV}{d\phi}\right|_{\phi=v} = 0 ,
\end{equation}
and the linear term in $\eta$ drops out. The quadratic term determines the mass
of the fluctuation field. Evaluating the second derivative yields
\begin{equation}
\left.\frac{d^2V}{d\phi^2}\right|_{\phi=v}
= m^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}v^2 = -2m^2 ,
\end{equation}
where in the last step the relation $v^2=-6m^2/\lambda$ has been used.
The expanded Lagrangian therefore contains a quadratic term of the form
\begin{equation}
-\frac{1}{2} m_\eta^2\,\eta^2 ,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
m_\eta^2 = -2m^2 = 2|m^2| .
\end{equation}

This shows explicitly that fluctuations around the chosen vacuum correspond to
massive scalar excitations. The appearance of this mass is a direct consequence
of the curvature of the potential at the minimum and illustrates how a
nontrivial vacuum structure dynamically determines the properties of physical
excitations.

No massless mode appears in this example, since the broken symmetry is discrete
rather than continuous. The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry relates the two degenerate
vacuums but does not generate a continuous family of equivalent ground states.
This distinguishes the present case from theories with continuous symmetries,
where spontaneous symmetry breaking leads to massless excitations.

\section{On-shell renormalization in \texorpdfstring{$\phi^4$}{phi4} theory}
\label{sec:phi4_onshell_example}

We now illustrate dimensional regularization and on-shell
renormalization in a concrete example.
For a general introduction to the concepts used in the following
we refer to Appendix~\ref{app:renormalization}.
In the present section we focus exclusively on the explicit
one-loop computation and the practical implementation of the
on-shell conditions.

We consider real scalar $\phi^4$ theory and work to first order in the
coupling constant $\lambda$.
At this order in perturbation theory, the only correction to the
two-point function arises from the one-loop tadpole diagram shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:tadpole}.
This diagram contributes to the 1PI
self-energy $\Sigma(p^2)$, which encodes loop corrections to the
inverse propagator.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}
  \begin{feynman}
    \diagram [large, horizontal=a to c, layered layout] {
      a -- [edge label=\(p\)]
      b [dot] -- [edge label=\(p\)]
      c
    };
    \def\R{0.8}
    \path (b) ++(90:\R) coordinate (loopcenter);
    \draw (loopcenter) circle (\R);
    \node at ($(loopcenter)+(0,\R+0.25)$) {\(k\)};
  \end{feynman}
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{One-loop tadpole contribution to the 1PI two-point function
in $\phi^4$ theory. The loop momentum is denoted $k$, while $p$
is the external momentum.}
\label{fig:tadpole}
\end{figure}

Using the momentum-space Feynman rules summarized in
Appendix~\ref{app:renormalization}, the amputated 1PI contribution reads
\begin{equation}
-i\,\Sigma_{\text{1-loop}}(p^2)
=
\left(-i\lambda\right)\frac{1}{2}
\int\!\frac{d^4k}{(2\pi)^4}
\frac{i}{k^2 - m^2 + i\epsilon}.
\label{eq:tadpole_selfenergy_start}
\end{equation}
The factor $-i\lambda$ arises from the quartic interaction vertex,
and the propagator inside the loop contributes the factor
$i/(k^2-m^2+i\epsilon)$.
The overall factor $1/2$ is the symmetry factor of the diagram,
reflecting the indistinguishability of the two internal lines attached
to the vertex.

An important feature of the tadpole diagram is that no external
momentum flows through the loop.
The loop integral therefore depends only on the integration momentum $k$,
and not on the external momentum $p$.
Consequently, at this order the self-energy is independent of $p^2$,
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\text{1-loop}}(p^2) = \Sigma_{\text{1-loop}} .
\end{equation}
This momentum independence will play a central role when imposing the
on-shell renormalization conditions below.

The integral in \eqref{eq:tadpole_selfenergy_start} is ultraviolet
divergent.
The divergence originates from the region of large loop momentum,
$k^2 \to \infty$.
To make the expression well defined, we regulate it using dimensional
regularization, analytically continuing the integral to
$d=4-2\epsilon$ spacetime dimensions.
A renormalization scale $\Lambda$ is introduced so that the coupling
remains dimensionless away from four dimensions.

Employing the standard result quoted in
Appendix~\ref{app:renormalization} (see Eq.~\eqref{eq:I1_pole}),
\begin{equation}
\Lambda^{2\epsilon}
\int\!\frac{d^dk}{(2\pi)^d}
\frac{i}{k^2 - m^2 + i\epsilon}
=
\frac{i m^2}{16\pi^2}
\left[
\frac{1}{\epsilon}
+ \ln\!\left(\frac{\Lambda^2}{m^2}\right)
+ \text{finite}
\right],
\end{equation}
we obtain
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\text{1-loop}}
=
\frac{\lambda m^2}{32\pi^2}
\left[
\frac{1}{\epsilon}
+ \ln\!\left(\frac{\Lambda^2}{m^2}\right)
+ \text{finite}
\right].
\end{equation}
The pole term $1/\epsilon$ signals the ultraviolet divergence of the
loop integral.
The logarithmic term depends on the renormalization scale $\Lambda$,
while the remaining finite part depends on the precise subtraction
convention adopted.
At this stage the theory still contains a divergence, which must be
removed by renormalization.

The bare and renormalized quantities are related by
\begin{equation}
\phi_0 = Z^{1/2}\phi,
\qquad
m_0^2 = m^2 + \delta m^2.
\end{equation}
Here $Z = 1 + \delta Z$ defines the field-strength renormalization
constant, and $\delta m^2$ is the mass counterterm.
Inserting these relations into the Lagrangian and expanding to first
order in the counterterms generates additional quadratic interaction
terms.
In momentum space these contributions modify the two-point function
according to
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\text{ct}}(p^2)
=
\delta Z\,p^2
-
\delta m^2.
\label{eq:selfenergy_counterterm_stripped}
\end{equation}
The term proportional to $p^2$ originates from the kinetic
counterterm, while the constant term arises from the mass
counterterm.

The renormalized self-energy is therefore given by
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\text{ren}}(p^2)
=
\Sigma_{\text{1-loop}}
+
\delta Z\,p^2
-
\delta m^2.
\end{equation}
The counterterms are now chosen so that physical conditions on the
propagator are satisfied.

In the on-shell scheme, the physical mass is defined as the location
of the pole of the full propagator, and the residue at that pole is
required to be unity.
These requirements ensure that the parameter $m$ is identified with
the pole mass and that the field is canonically normalized.
They translate into the conditions
\begin{align}
\Sigma_{\text{ren}}(m^2) &= 0, \\
\left.\frac{d\Sigma_{\text{ren}}(p^2)}{dp^2}\right|_{p^2=m^2} &= 0.
\end{align}

Since the tadpole contribution is independent of $p^2$,
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\Sigma_{\text{1-loop}}}{dp^2}=0,
\end{equation}
and therefore the residue condition immediately implies
\begin{equation}
\delta Z = 0
\qquad \text{at } \mathcal{O}(\lambda).
\end{equation}
Thus, at one-loop order in $\phi^4$ theory the field-strength does
not receive a correction in the on-shell scheme.

The mass condition then fixes the remaining counterterm,
\begin{equation}
\delta m^2
=
\Sigma_{\text{1-loop}}.
\end{equation}
The mass counterterm exactly cancels the entire one-loop contribution.

Thus, at one-loop order the on-shell scheme subtracts the complete
tadpole correction to the two-point function.
The ultraviolet divergence contained in the $1/\epsilon$ term is
absorbed into the redefinition of the mass parameter,
and the propagator retains its pole at $p^2 = m^2$ with unit residue.
This explicit example shows in practice how divergences arising from
loop integrals are removed by renormalization while preserving the
physical interpretation of the mass and the normalization of the
field.
