\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:introduction2}

Neutron stars are compact stellar remnants formed in core-collapse supernovae,
with typical masses of order one to two solar masses and radii of roughly ten
kilometres. Their interiors reach densities far above those of ordinary nuclei,
making them natural laboratories for strongly interacting matter at extreme
density. Observations from radio timing, X-ray pulse-profile modelling, and
gravitational-wave detections have therefore made neutron stars central probes
of dense-matter physics \cite{LattimerPrakash2004,glendenning2000,
Riley_2019_J0030,Miller_2019_J0030,Miller_2021_J0740,Riley2021,LIGO2018}.

The structure of a static, spherically symmetric compact star is determined by
the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff (TOV) equations \cite{tolman1939,oppenheimer1939}. These equations relate the
pressure, energy density, and enclosed mass of a star in hydrostatic
equilibrium. To solve them, one must provide an equation of state (EoS), which encodes the
microscopic degrees of freedom and interactions of dense matter. The EoS is
therefore the main source of theoretical uncertainty in compact-star modelling,
and different assumptions can lead to substantially different predictions for
maximum masses, radii, central densities, and stability.

The importance of the equation of state is already visible in the simplest
models. The classic Oppenheimer--Volkoff calculation, based on an ideal
degenerate neutron gas, gives a maximum mass of approximately
\(0.7\,M_\odot\) \cite{oppenheimer1939}. This is far below the masses of
observed neutron stars and shows that interactions between particles are
essential. More realistic hadronic models include interacting neutrons and
protons, leptons, charge neutrality, and $\beta$-equilibrium. Such models represent
a major improvement over the ideal Fermi-gas description. This progression was
studied in Part~\ref{part:neutron-stars}, where compact-star structure was developed
from ideal gases to interacting relativistic mean-field matter. The main lesson from that analysis was that
microphysical assumptions are directly reflected in macroscopic mass--radius
relations.

Even with interacting hadronic matter, however, the equation of state remains
uncertain at the highest densities. Models based on nucleonic degrees of
freedom, such as relativistic mean-field descriptions or microscopic hadronic
equations of state, can reproduce important aspects of neutron-star
phenomenology, but their extrapolation to several times nuclear saturation
density is not unique. A sufficiently stiff equation of state is needed to
support massive compact stars, while radius measurements
restrict how stiff the matter can be at lower and intermediate
densities. This tension motivates the study of additional degrees of freedom and
new phases of strongly interacting matter in the cores of compact stars
\cite{Baym2018,Annala2020}.

At sufficiently high density, the relevant microscopic degrees of freedom are no
longer hadrons, but quarks and gluons. Quantum chromodynamics (QCD) is the
fundamental theory of the strong interaction and describes hadrons in terms of
colored quarks interacting through gluons \cite{GrossWilczek1973,
PeskinSchroeder1995}. Due to asymptotic freedom, QCD becomes weakly coupled at
very large energy or density scales. This suggests that matter at sufficiently
high baryon density may undergo a transition from confined hadronic matter to
deconfined quark matter. Compact stars are among the few physical systems in
which such densities may be realized, making them natural laboratories for
studying possible quark phases of QCD.

The possibility of quark matter in compact stars has a long history. Early work
considered stars containing deconfined quark matter, either as hybrid stars with
quark cores or as stars composed entirely of quark matter
\cite{Itoh1970,Bodmer1971,Witten1984,glendenning2000}. The strange matter
hypothesis, proposed by Bodmer and later developed by Witten, suggests that bulk
three-flavor quark matter could be the true ground state of strongly interacting
matter \cite{Bodmer1971,Witten1984}. If this hypothesis is realized, compact
stars could in principle be strange stars rather than ordinary neutron stars.
In the present thesis, however, the models studied contain only two quark
flavors. The strange matter hypothesis is therefore relevant as motivation, but
the calculations performed here should not be interpreted as a direct test of
absolutely stable strange matter.

A direct first-principles treatment of cold dense QCD remains difficult. Lattice
QCD is highly successful at zero baryon chemical potential, but the sign problem
prevents straightforward simulations of the cold, dense regime relevant for
neutron-star interiors. Perturbative QCD is reliable only at densities much
higher than those expected in the cores of observed compact stars. As a result,
one must often rely on effective models that retain selected symmetries and
degrees of freedom of QCD while remaining tractable. Such models cannot replace
QCD itself, but they provide useful laboratories for studying how chiral symmetry,
deconfinement-inspired quark degrees of freedom, and pairing mechanisms affect
the equation of state and compact-star structure
\cite{Baym2018,Alford2008,Kurkela2010,Komoltsev2022}.

The present thesis studies such effective quark-matter descriptions using the Quark--Meson (QM)
model and its extension with diquark degrees of freedom. The QM model describes
quarks coupled to mesonic fields and provides a simple framework for studying
chiral symmetry breaking and restoration in dense matter. By minimizing the
thermodynamic potential in the mean-field approximation, one obtains an EoS that
can be used as input to the TOV equations. This makes it possible to study how
parameters in the chiral sector, such as the sigma mass, affect the stiffness of
quark matter and the resulting compact-star mass--radius relations.

The thesis also considers a phenomenological Bodmer--Witten-inspired shift of
the vacuum normalization in the two-flavor Quark--Meson model. This construction
is useful for studying how changes in the zero-pressure point affect quark-star
sequences, in a way similar to a bag-like pressure shift. Since the model is
two-flavor, this should not be understood as a calculation of stable strange
matter. Rather, it is used as a diagnostic tool for exploring how the mass and
radius scales of quark-star solutions respond to changes in the vacuum
normalization.

The main extension studied in this thesis is the Quark--Meson--Diquark (QMD) model. This model includes
a diquark condensate and allows the two-flavor color-superconducting (2SC) phase to be studied explicitly. Color
superconductivity is expected to be a generic possibility in dense quark matter,
where attractive quark--quark channels can lead to Cooper pairing
\cite{Alford2008}. The QMD model therefore provides a framework for studying how
2SC pairing modifies the EoS, the speed of sound, and the compact-star
mass--radius relation.

A central question in this thesis is whether such two-flavor quark-matter models
can produce compact-star sequences with sufficiently large maximum masses,
reasonable radii, and stable stellar branches. The aim is not to construct a
final realistic neutron-star equation of state, since the models used here do
not include a hadronic crust or envelope, a nuclear-to-quark transition, strange
quarks, or color-flavor-locked (CFL) pairing. Instead, the
goal is to isolate the quark-sector physics and identify which mechanisms and
parameters are most important for compact-star phenomenology.

The thesis is organized as follows. Chapter~\ref{chap:qft} introduces the
quantum-field-theoretic tools used in the effective models.
Chapter~\ref{chap:qm_model} develops the two-flavor Quark--Meson model and its
mean-field thermodynamics. Chapter~\ref{chap:quark_matter_stars} applies the QM
and QMD models to dense quark matter and compact stars, including the
Bodmer--Witten-inspired shift, 2SC pairing, parameter sensitivity, and comparison
with selected astrophysical constraints.
