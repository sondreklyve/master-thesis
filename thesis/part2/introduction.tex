\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:introduction2}

Neutron stars are compact stellar remnants formed in core–collapse supernovae,
with typical masses of order one to two solar masses and radii of roughly ten
kilometres. Observations accumulated over the past decades—most notably precise
mass measurements from radio timing of pulsars, X-ray observations of neutron-star
surfaces, and gravitational-wave signals from neutron-star mergers—have established
neutron stars as key laboratories for physics at extreme densities. Any theoretical
description of these objects must therefore be consistent with a growing set of
astrophysical constraints \cite{LattimerPrakash2004,glendenning2000}.

The structure of a non-rotating neutron star is described within general relativity
by the Tolman, Oppenheimer, and Volkoff (TOV) equations \cite{tolman1939,oppenheimer1939}.
These equations relate the pressure, energy density, and enclosed mass of the star
under the assumption of hydrostatic equilibrium. In order to solve them, one must
specify an equation of state (EoS) for dense matter. The EoS encodes assumptions
about the matter content of the star and their interactions, and it is
the primary source of theoretical uncertainty in neutron-star modelling.

Early neutron-star models employed simple equations of state based on ideal,
degenerate Fermi gases of neutrons. These models provide a useful starting point
and lead to the classic Oppenheimer–Volkoff limit of approximately
$0.7\,M_{\odot}$ for the maximum neutron-star mass \cite{oppenheimer1939}. While
historically important, this result is now known to be far below the masses of
observed neutron stars, demonstrating that interactions between particles are
essential for a realistic description of dense matter.

Including interactions between nucleons leads to significantly stiffer equations
of state and substantially higher maximum masses. Models that treat neutrons and
protons as interacting particles, supplemented with leptons to ensure charge
neutrality and $\beta$-equilibrium, represent a clear improvement over idealized Fermi-gas
descriptions. Such interacting models are capable of producing neutron stars with
masses closer to those observed in nature. However, even these more realistic
descriptions remain uncertain at the highest densities and often struggle to
reproduce the full range of current observational constraints. This issue was
demonstrated explicitly in the project thesis (Part~I), where we looked at the
$npe\mu$ model for matter. It was shown that,
despite significant improvement over idealized models, the predicted mass-radius
relations still do not align with well-measured neutron stars.

A more detailed description of dense hadronic matter is provided by the
Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall (APR) equation of state, which is based on
realistic two- and three-nucleon interactions constrained by nucleon--nucleon
scattering data and many-body calculations \cite{Akmal1998}. When three-nucleon
forces and leading relativistic corrections are included, the APR model can
support neutron stars with maximum masses above two solar masses, placing it
among the stiffest purely hadronic equations of state. However, this stiffness
comes at a cost: at the highest densities, the model approaches or exceeds
limits set by causality, as the speed of sound can become comparable to or larger
than the speed of light. This highlights a general issue faced by very stiff
hadronic equations of state: while they can reproduce large neutron-star
masses, their behavior at extreme densities becomes increasingly uncertain.
As a result, even advanced hadronic models such as APR do not fully resolve the
tension between theoretical consistency and observational constraints, and
they motivate the exploration of alternative descriptions of dense matter at
the highest densities.

The persistence of these discrepancies suggests that further improvements of the
equation of state are required. One possible direction is that the relevant
degrees of freedom at the highest densities differ qualitatively from those of
ordinary nuclear matter. The development of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) in the
early 1970s, as the fundamental theory of the strong interaction, provided a
microscopic description of hadrons in terms of quarks and gluons
\cite{GrossWilczek1973}. This, in turn, opened the possibility of considering 
regimes in which quark degrees of freedom are no longer confined in hadronic
bound states, but instead play a direct role in the description of bulk matter.

Shortly after the formulation of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) in the early
1970s, the idea emerged that matter at sufficiently high density might undergo a
transition from a hadronic description to one in terms of deconfined quarks.
Early studies in the 1970s and 1980s explored the implications of such a
transition for compact stars, leading to the proposal of new classes of stellar
objects \cite{Itoh1970,Bodmer1971,Witten1984}. Within this framework, neutron
stars may contain cores of deconfined quark matter, commonly referred to as
hybrid stars, or, in more speculative scenarios, be composed entirely of quark
matter, giving rise to quark or strange stars.

A closely related idea is the strange matter hypothesis, originally proposed by
Bodmer and later developed by Witten \cite{Bodmer1971,Witten1984}. In this
scenario, bulk quark matter containing up, down, and strange quarks could have
a lower energy per baryon than ordinary nuclear matter. If this were the case,
the hadronic matter observed in nuclei and neutron stars would not represent
the absolute ground state of the strong interaction, but rather a very
long-lived, metastable phase. Neutron stars provide one of the few environments
in the present universe where conditions might allow such a phase to form, due
to their extremely high central densities. Depending on the details of the
transition, this could lead either to stars with quark cores or to stars
composed entirely of strange quark matter, commonly referred to as strange
stars \cite{glendenning2000}.

If strange quark matter were absolutely stable, an important consequence would
be that the formation of a sufficiently large region of strange matter inside a
neutron star could trigger the conversion of the surrounding hadronic matter.
In this case, the star would not remain a hybrid object but could instead
convert almost entirely into a strange star. Although the dynamics of such a
conversion process are uncertain and model dependent, this possibility makes
strange stars qualitatively different from ordinary neutron stars and hybrid
stars. One proposed observational way to distinguish between these scenarios is
through rapid rotation. Strange stars are self-bound by the strong interaction,
rather than being bound by gravity alone, and may therefore be able to rotate
faster than neutron stars without shedding mass. The discovery of a pulsar with
a rotation period shorter than what is theoretically possible for a neutron
star would be difficult to explain within purely hadronic models and would
provide strong evidence in favor of a strange star interpretation
\cite{glendenning2000}.

A theoretical description of these possibilities calls for models that go
beyond purely hadronic descriptions of dense matter. While hadronic models are
commonly formulated within quantum field theory, they describe matter in terms
of composite bound states. A more fundamental description is provided by QCD,
which is the underlying theory governing the
interactions between quarks and gluons \cite{PeskinSchroeder1995}. While QCD is
well established as the correct microscopic theory of the strong interaction,
its direct application to the high-density, low-temperature regime relevant for
neutron stars remains notoriously challenging. As a result, first-principles
calculations in this domain are limited, and one must instead rely on
approximate approaches. This has motivated the development of effective models
inspired by QCD, which aim to capture the relevant degrees of freedom and
symmetry properties of strongly interacting matter while remaining tractable
\cite{glendenning2000}. Such approaches provide a practical starting point for
studying deconfined quark matter in compact stars and for exploring how a change
in the microscopic description of matter can affect macroscopic stellar
properties.

The present master thesis follows this line of reasoning. Motivated by the
limitations of hadronic equations of state and supported by the findings of
Part~I, it explores quantum field–theoretic descriptions of dense matter with
quark degrees of freedom and investigates their implications for the structure
and observable properties of compact stars.
